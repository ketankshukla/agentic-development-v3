# Red Earth

## Genre
Action-Adventure

## One-Line Premise
A disgraced wildlife ranger must lead a group of stranded tourists across 400 kilometers of hostile Australian outback while hunted by a mining corporation's private security for witnessing a massacre.

## Expanded Premise
Jack Maddox lost his career as a ranger after a confrontation with illegal miners left one of them dead. Now he drives tourists through the Northern Territory's remote wilderness for minimum wage. When his latest group stumbles onto a massacre site—Aboriginal protestors killed by private security for a lithium mining consortium—they become targets themselves. Their vehicles are destroyed. Their phones are dead. Between them and civilization lies 400 kilometers of some of Earth's most hostile terrain: no water, no shade, daytime temperatures exceeding 50°C, and a professional kill team methodically hunting them. Jack must get eight civilians to safety through country that kills even experienced bushmen, using skills he thought he'd never need again.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Jack Maddox
**Age:** 45
**Occupation / Background:** Former Northern Territory Parks and Wildlife ranger, now a tour bus driver. Raised on a cattle station, trained by Aboriginal elders in traditional bush skills, destroyed his career defending sacred land from miners.
**Core skill:** Unmatched outback survival expertise—he can find water where there is none, navigate by stars, and read the land like a language. He's also killed before and will do it again.
**Fatal flaw:** Isolation that he mistakes for strength. He pushed everyone away after his dismissal and has spent five years proving he doesn't need anyone.
**Character arc:** Believes he failed when he lost his career—that the system beat him and nothing matters anymore. Discovers that protecting people isn't a job; it's who he is. Ends reconnected to purpose and community.
**Voice and register:** Laconic, sparse, speaking only when words add something. When he does talk, it's usually about survival. His rare humor is bone-dry.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Gareth Vickers, head of security for Pacific Lithium Consortium, ex-Australian SAS, now runs "problem removal" for mining corporations across the region.
**Goal:** Eliminate all witnesses to the massacre before they can reach communications. The lithium deposit is worth $40 billion; the protestors were eliminated for far less. Eight tourists are a small additional cost.
**Method:** Commands a four-man team with military training, satellite phones, vehicles, and drones. They know the outback—but not as well as Jack does.
**Connection to protagonist:** Vickers was the one who framed Jack five years ago—that "illegal miner" Jack killed was one of Vickers's advance scouts. Jack doesn't know this, but Vickers has always wondered if Jack would come back to cause problems. Now he has his answer.

## Supporting Characters

**Nadia Okonkwo** — Nigerian-Australian ER doctor on holiday with her teenage daughter. The group's medical expert, she keeps people alive and pushes back on Jack's loner tendencies.
**Marcus Webb** — American tech executive, initially useless, who discovers reserves of courage he didn't know he had when his wife's life depends on it.
**Sarah Webb** — Marcus's wife, an ultramarathon runner whose endurance becomes crucial and who refuses to be a victim.
**Tommy Marr** — Young Aboriginal man working as Jack's tour assistant, who knows traditional routes through country the maps call empty.
**Dr. Peter Holloway** — British academic in his sixties, a geologist who unwittingly provided Pacific Lithium with the data that led to the massacre.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Northern Territory's remote western reaches—from the massacre site near the Tanami Desert through spinifex country, dry riverbeds, and sacred Aboriginal lands to the Stuart Highway.
**Time period:** Present day, late summer—the "build-up" before the wet season, maximum heat and humidity before monsoons arrive.
**Atmosphere:** Flat red earth extending to horizons so distant they shimmer. No trees taller than a person. The sun is a physical assault; shade is survival. At night, cold drops suddenly, and the stars are so thick they cast shadows. Everything here kills—snakes, spiders, heat, thirst.
**Key locations:** The massacre site (starting point), the Abandoned Bore (first water), the Sacred Caves (midpoint shelter), the Dry Riverbeds (navigation corridor), the Stuart Highway (destination).

## Central Conflict
Jack must keep eight terrified civilians alive across terrain that would kill a lone survival expert, managing dehydration, heat exhaustion, snake bites, and interpersonal collapse—all while evading a team of professional killers with every advantage in technology and firepower. Every day covers less distance than planned. Every water source could be watched. Every decision determines who lives.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The group reaches the highway and exposes the massacre. Pacific Lithium faces accountability. Sacred land is protected. Jack proves that his skills and his moral code still matter.
**If the protagonist fails:** Eight more bodies join the protesters in unmarked graves. The massacre is covered up. Jack dies having confirmed his belief that nothing he does matters. The lithium mining proceeds.
**Personal stakes:** Jack either finds a reason to stop punishing himself for losing his career—discovers that leadership and protection are who he is, not what he did—or he dies still believing he already failed.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Jack drives tourists through spectacular but inhospitable country, cynical and going through the motions. They stop at an unscheduled point for photos. They find the bodies—a dozen Aboriginal protestors, shot execution-style.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Before they can react, Vickers's team arrives. The tour bus is destroyed. Three tourists die in the initial attack. The survivors scatter into the spinifex, and Jack takes command because there is no one else.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: First night in the bush. Jack takes inventory: eight survivors, limited water, no communications, 400 kilometers to the highway. He plans their route while the group fractures—Marcus blames him, Peter admits what he knew, Tommy reveals traditional paths.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: First full day of walking. The heat is relentless. One tourist collapses from dehydration. Nadia performs field medicine. They reach the Abandoned Bore only to find Vickers has poisoned it. They have twenty-four hours of water and three days of walking.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Tommy leads them to a sacred site his grandmother taught him about—water in rock pools, shelter in caves. For one day, they rest and recover. Jack teaches basic survival. The group begins to function as a unit.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Vickers finds them. Running firefight through the caves. Tommy is shot, dying. Jack kills one of Vickers's team, but they're driven out, losing most of their water. Peter, overwhelmed by guilt, walks into the desert alone. Jack can't save both him and the group.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Crossing the most hostile stretch—no water, no shade, temperatures that cause hallucinations. Sarah helps Jack carry her husband, who's failing. Nadia's daughter, Adaeze, shows unexpected resilience. Jack confronts his isolation: he's spent five years proving he doesn't need anyone, but these people need him.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: They reach dry riverbeds that will lead to the highway. Vickers is ahead of them with remaining men, setting an ambush. Jack decides to hunt them first—become the predator. The group objects. Nadia tells him they're not bait, they're partners. They plan together.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The group springs a counter-ambush using the terrain Jack knows intimately. Fighting in a dry riverbed, using flash-flood warnings to time their move. One of Vickers's men is killed by a snake, another drowned when the flash flood arrives. Final confrontation: Jack and Vickers in the flood channel, fighting as water rises around them. Jack wins when Vickers's weapon jams from mud. He lets the desert take him.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The survivors reach the Stuart Highway as a supply truck approaches. They're airlifted to Darwin. The massacre story breaks internationally. Pacific Lithium faces criminal charges. Tommy is buried with ceremony. Jack is offered his ranger job back. He declines—takes a position training Indigenous rangers instead. The final scene: Jack teaching young Aboriginal men to read country, passing on what was passed to him.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Survival against an unforgiving environment and human predators.
**Deep theme:** The difference between isolation and independence—and how protecting others requires accepting connection.
**Thematic question:** When everything you valued was taken from you, can you find purpose in protecting what others value?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Cave Battle**: Running firefight through ancient Aboriginal sacred caves, headlamps and muzzle flashes in the dark, Tommy's death buying time for the others to escape.
2. **The Dry Crossing**: Trudging across salt pans in 52°C heat, hallucinations setting in, the group literally carrying each other when legs fail.
3. **The Flood Channel Fight**: Jack versus Vickers as a flash flood rushes toward them, fighting in rising water, using the environment as a weapon.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *Wolf Creek* meets *The Way Back*
**Book comparables:** *Wake in Fright* by Kenneth Cook, *The Dry* by Jane Harper

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Relentless heat and pressure. The Australian outback is not just setting but antagonist—every chapter carries the weight of sun and thirst. Violence is brutal and has consequences; the group diminishes as the story progresses. Dialogue is sparse and functional—no one wastes breath. The beauty of the landscape is ever-present alongside its lethality.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Australian names and terminology should sound authentic—outback drawl for Jack, varied accents for the tourists. Aboriginal terms should be handled respectfully with correct pronunciation. The heat should be implied through pacing—slower, more labored descriptions as characters dehydrate. Tommy's death needs weight; he's not a supporting character casualty but a genuine loss.

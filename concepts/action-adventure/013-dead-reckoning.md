# Dead Reckoning

## Genre
Action-Adventure

## One-Line Premise
A commercial pilot must land a sabotaged aircraft on a remote Pacific island after terrorists seize the cockpit—using only dead reckoning navigation after all electronics fail.

## Expanded Premise
Captain Rosa Delgado is six hours into a trans-Pacific flight from Tokyo to Los Angeles when a coordinated attack disables the aircraft's electronics, kills her co-pilot, and leaves 287 passengers at the mercy of terrorists who've hijacked the plane for a ransom the airline won't pay. With no functioning instruments, no communication, and a bomb set to detonate in ninety minutes, Rosa must retake her aircraft, navigate by stars and intuition to a runway that might not exist, and land a modern jetliner using skills from an era before computers. The ocean below offers no second chances.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Captain Rosa Delgado
**Age:** 51
**Occupation / Background:** Senior 787 captain for Pacific Air, former US Navy P-3 Orion pilot, one of the first Latina women to command transoceanic flights. Daughter of Filipino immigrants, raised in San Diego.
**Core skill:** Old-school celestial navigation and dead reckoning—skills that the Navy taught her and modern aviation has mostly abandoned. She can calculate position by stars and estimate drift without instruments.
**Fatal flaw:** Pride in her career has become rigidity. She does everything by the book because the book protected her in a hostile industry—but this situation has no book.
**Character arc:** Believes that following rules and procedures keeps people safe. Discovers that real leadership means improvisation when the rules can't help. Ends willing to trust herself beyond what she was trained to do.
**Voice and register:** Calm, authoritative, professional—the voice passengers trust during turbulence. Under extreme stress, navy profanity emerges. She narrates her actions, a habit from CRM training that keeps her focused.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Viktor Saban, Serbian former pilot turned professional terrorist-for-hire, who's spent twenty years hijacking aircraft for various employers. This job is personal—Rosa's Navy squadron bombed his hometown during the Kosovo conflict.
**Goal:** Collect $200 million in cryptocurrency or crash the plane into the ocean, demonstrating that no aircraft anywhere is safe. If he has to die, he takes 287 people with him—a fair trade for what NATO took from him.
**Method:** Commands a three-person team with military training, has disabled all aircraft systems except basic hydraulics, and has rigged a bomb that will detonate if the plane drops below 10,000 feet before his countdown ends.
**Connection to protagonist:** Rosa flew combat missions during Kosovo. She may have been part of the squadron that killed Viktor's family. She doesn't remember—it was one mission among many—but he remembers everything.

## Supporting Characters

**First Officer David Chen** — Rosa's co-pilot, shot in the initial takeover. He's dying, but conscious enough to help with calculations until he isn't.
**Maya Okonkwo** — Nigerian-American air marshal aboard undercover, who must be found and recruited without alerting the terrorists.
**Dr. Takeshi Yamada** — Japanese cardiologist traveling to a conference, who becomes the in-flight medical support as casualties mount.
**Elena Voronova** — Russian passenger who speaks Serbian, potentially useful as interpreter—or potentially Viktor's inside asset.
**Captain James Hartley (retired)** — Elderly former Pan Am navigator, the last generation who flew without GPS, dying of cancer and determined to go out useful.

## Setting

**Primary location:** A Boeing 787 Dreamliner at 38,000 feet over the Pacific Ocean, between Hawaii and the US mainland—the most remote stretch of open water on any commercial route.
**Time period:** Present day, night crossing (which enables celestial navigation)
**Atmosphere:** The eerie calm of cruise altitude—the constant engine hum, the dimmed cabin lights, passengers who don't know they're hostages yet. In the cockpit: blood, sparking electronics, a co-pilot who won't stop bleeding. Outside: endless dark, stars wheeling overhead, and water that stretches for a thousand miles in every direction.
**Key locations:** Cockpit (primary battleground), first-class galley (Rosa's staging area), cargo hold (bomb location), overhead ceiling (maintenance access), Johnston Atoll (emergency landing target—barely maintained US military strip).

## Central Conflict
Rosa must retake a plane from armed terrorists while flying without instruments over the most remote stretch of ocean on Earth, navigate to a runway she's never seen using techniques from the pre-GPS era, and land a 787 on a strip barely long enough for smaller aircraft—all with a bomb ticking down, passengers panicking, and a hijacker who's been planning this for twenty years.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** 287 passengers survive, Viktor faces justice, and Rosa proves that the skills and instincts the industry has forgotten still matter when technology fails.
**If the protagonist fails:** The plane crashes into the Pacific with no survivors. It's the deadliest aviation disaster since 9/11. Viktor's message is delivered: nowhere is safe.
**Personal stakes:** Rosa either validates a career spent fighting for a seat at the table—proves that her training, instincts, and judgment are equal to any crisis—or she dies as the woman who lost her aircraft when everything depended on her.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Routine flight, six hours out of Tokyo. Rosa jokes with David, reviews weather. Then the electromagnetic pulse hits, killing every screen in the cockpit. Men with guns emerge from economy class. David is shot protecting Rosa.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Rosa is forced to continue flying while Viktor explains the terms: $200 million or death. She begins secretly calculating position from stars visible through the windscreen. She identifies possible landing options—all of them terrible.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Rosa convinces Viktor to let her treat David. In the galley, she encounters Maya Okonkwo, who's avoided detection. They plan in whispers: Rosa will navigate, Maya will neutralize the terrorists one by one.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Maya eliminates the first terrorist in the cargo hold but discovers the bomb is more sophisticated than expected. Rosa runs celestial navigation calculations with Captain Hartley's help—Johnston Atoll is possible, barely.
- Ch 5 — The Push: The ransom deadline passes. The airline refuses to pay. Viktor executes a passenger to demonstrate seriousness. Rosa realizes he's not bluffing—he will crash the plane. She commits to landing at Johnston, with or without instruments.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Viktor discovers Rosa's navigation notes and realizes she's planning a landing. He kills Captain Hartley in front of her and moves the bomb trigger deadline up. David dies from his wounds. Rosa is alone in the cockpit with a man who wants her dead.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Rosa must fly while Viktor holds a gun to her head, pretending to comply while Maya works through the aircraft. Elena is revealed as innocent—she helps translate Viktor's radio communications, revealing his desperation. The window for Johnston is closing.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Maya takes down the second terrorist, but Viktor barricades himself in the cockpit with Rosa. The plane is forty minutes from Johnston. Rosa begins an approach by dead reckoning alone—calculating drift, wind, position from stars that are fading with approaching dawn.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: As Rosa descends, Viktor realizes she's actually attempting a landing and panics—his plan assumed no survival. Fight in the cockpit at 15,000 feet, the plane shuddering through turbulence. Rosa incapacitates Viktor with a fire extinguisher while flying single-handed. The bomb countdown accelerates. Maya disarms it with thirty seconds remaining. Rosa finds Johnston Atoll—barely visible strip of white in the gray dawn.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Landing a 787 on a 9,000-foot strip designed for C-130s, using techniques from her Navy days. The plane stops with a hundred feet to spare. Military personnel swarm the aircraft. 284 passengers survive. Viktor is taken into custody. Rosa walks off the plane as the sun rises. Six months later, she's teaching celestial navigation to the next generation of pilots. The book has a new chapter.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** One pilot against impossible odds.
**Deep theme:** The danger of trusting technology over skill—and the importance of preserving human capability even when it seems obsolete.
**Thematic question:** When everything you depend on fails, are you enough?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Cockpit Takeover**: The instant when everything changes—EMP flash, gunfire, David falling, Rosa's world narrowing to the controls and the gun at her head.
2. **The Celestial Navigation**: Rosa in the darkened cockpit, measuring star angles with improvised tools while Captain Hartley talks her through calculations, racing against a countdown.
3. **The Johnston Landing**: Descending through clouds to find a strip that might not be where she thinks it is, no instruments, no second chances, the aircraft shuddering at the edge of its envelope.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *United 93* meets *Sully*
**Book comparables:** *Airframe* by Michael Crichton, *Flight 232* by Laurence Gonzales

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Claustrophobic tension in an enclosed space where every sound carries. Technical aviation detail grounds the action without overwhelming—readers should understand what's difficult without needing a pilot's license. The countdown structure provides relentless momentum. Violence is contained and shocking; death has weight. Rosa's internal monologue provides emotional access while her external professionalism demonstrates competence.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Aviation terminology should sound natural and confident—Rosa is an expert in her domain. Spanish and Filipino names use standard pronunciation (Rosa = ROH-sa, Delgado = del-GAH-doh). Viktor's Serbian accent can be subtle. The cockpit environment—constant engine hum, radio static, air pressure—should be present throughout. The landing sequence needs careful pacing: technical precision building to visceral relief.

# The Bone People

## Genre
supernatural-thriller

## One-Line Premise
A disgraced Maori detective investigating disappearances in the New Zealand wilderness discovers that the old guardians his grandmother warned him about are very real — and are taking people who have desecrated sacred ground.

## Expanded Premise
Detective Rawiri Tamati was suspended from Auckland CID after a corruption investigation destroyed his reputation. Returning to his ancestral territory on the East Cape, he's asked by local iwi to investigate hikers vanishing from a remote area developers want to open for tourism. What he finds makes no sense: tracks that end abruptly, belongings arranged in ritual patterns, and witnesses describing figures carved from bone and wood moving through the bush at night. His grandmother told him these stories — guardians who protect tapu places from those who would violate them. She also warned him that the guardians don't distinguish between ignorant violators and knowing ones. The development team keeps sending people in. The guardians keep taking them.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Detective Rawiri Tamati
**Age:** 46
**Occupation / Background:** Suspended Auckland detective, formerly Major Crimes. Born in Tikitiki, sent to city schools, lost touch with his Maori heritage. Now forced home in disgrace.
**Core skill:** Investigation — Rawiri can read crime scenes, interpret evidence, and understand human behavior patterns. He's excellent at finding what people are hiding.
**Fatal flaw:** Disconnection from his culture. He dismissed his grandmother's stories as superstition, choosing the Pakeha world. This makes him both credible (he doesn't want to believe) and vulnerable (he has no defense).
**Character arc:** Believes he can solve this with police procedure. Discovers that some knowledge requires belief, not skepticism. Must reclaim the identity he abandoned to survive what's hunting the violators.
**Voice and register:** Blunt and practical, a cop's voice. Narration is observational and frustrated. Interior voice reveals someone who has spent decades suppressing parts of himself.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The Patupaiarehe — ancient guardians of sacred lands in Maori tradition, appearing as pale figures that move like shadows through the bush. Neither good nor evil — simply executing their eternal function.
**Goal:** To protect the tapu sites on the mountain. They have done this since before humans arrived and will continue until the land itself dies.
**Method:** They take violators into the bush. What happens there is unclear — bodies are never found, though sometimes bone carvings appear at forest edges.
**Connection to protagonist:** Rawiri's bloodline is tied to this land. His ancestors were tohunga who knew how to negotiate with the guardians. He has forgotten everything they taught.

## Supporting Characters

**Mere Pania** — Rawiri's second cousin, a local teacher and keeper of tribal histories. She becomes his guide to the knowledge he needs.

**Harrison Blake** — Australian property developer determined to open the area for eco-tourism. His arrogance and refusal to respect warnings make him the catalyst for escalation.

**Sergeant Nikau Hohaia** — Local police officer, Maori, young, caught between departmental pressure to dismiss the disappearances and his own cultural awareness.

## Setting

**Primary location:** East Cape region of New Zealand's North Island — remote, rugged, sparsely populated
**Time period:** Present day, winter — short days, long wet nights, mist rolling through forested valleys
**Atmosphere:** Dense native bush that blocks sunlight at noon, birdsong that stops without warning, the constant sound of water. A landscape that feels conscious, watching. The collision between modern New Zealand (SUVs, smartphones, property law) and something much older.
**Key locations:** Tikitiki village (Rawiri's birthplace); the sacred mountain Hikurangi; the abandoned development base camp; Mere's farmhouse with its library of tribal history; the bone grove (where the guardians leave their carvings).

## Central Conflict
Rawiri must stop the disappearances without becoming a victim himself. The developers won't stop sending people in, the guardians won't stop taking them, and the only person who might know how to negotiate — his grandmother — died twenty years ago. He must find the old knowledge quickly, or watch the body count rise.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The development is stopped, the sacred site remains protected, and a bridge is rebuilt between Rawiri and his heritage.
**If the protagonist fails:** The developers push through anyway, triggering a response from the guardians that spreads beyond the mountain. An ancient balance is broken.
**Personal stakes:** Rawiri's professional credibility is already destroyed. If he reports what's actually happening, his sanity will be questioned next. But if he lies, he becomes complicit.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Rawiri returns home in disgrace, avoiding old connections. A kuia (elder woman) finds him at his grandmother's abandoned house and asks him to investigate: three hikers are missing, and the police aren't listening.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Rawiri examines the disappearance sites. Evidence makes no sense — tracks simply ending, personal items arranged in spiral patterns. He interviews witnesses who describe "people made of sticks and bone" in the bush at dusk.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Alone at the base camp at night, Rawiri sees them — pale figures at the tree line, motionless, watching. His flashlight reveals faces carved from bone. He runs. They don't follow. He's not the one who violated.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Harrison Blake arrives with a new survey team, ignoring all warnings. Rawiri confronts him legally and loses — the development has permits. He turns to Mere for cultural knowledge, beginning his education.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Using tribal histories, Rawiri identifies the sacred sites being violated. He convinces Blake to reroute the survey around them. For a few days, no one vanishes.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Blake's team ignores the rerouting, taking a shortcut through tapu land. Three more people disappear, including Blake's son. Blake blames Rawiri and threatens escalation. The guardians are seen closer to inhabited areas.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Rawiri is arrested on Blake's accusation of obstruction. In jail, he dreams of his grandmother speaking words he doesn't understand. He realizes he's been approaching this as a crime to solve, not a violation to address.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Released without charges, Rawiri goes to Mere. She takes him through a ceremony he resisted his whole life — reconnecting him to his whakapapa, his lineage. He learns his ancestor's role and what it requires.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Rawiri ascends the sacred mountain alone, carrying symbols of his bloodline. He finds the guardians' place — a grove where bone carvings hang from every tree, hundreds of them. The guardians materialize. He speaks the old words, acknowledges the violation, offers to serve as intermediary as his ancestors did. A negotiation occurs without language.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Rawiri descends with a message: the land is closed. Not forever — but until those who would enter it learn respect. Blake's company abandons the project. The missing are not returned. Rawiri remains on the East Cape, serving a function he never knew he was born for.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** A detective confronting supernatural justice in the New Zealand wilderness.
**Deep theme:** We cannot solve problems by running from who we are.
**Thematic question:** What do we lose when we abandon our heritage, and what does reclaiming it cost?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Base Camp Encounter**: Rawiri alone in the abandoned camp, his flashlight sweeping the tree line, revealing figure after figure of bone-white guardians standing motionless, watching.
2. **The Bone Grove**: Rawiri entering the heart of the sacred mountain, hundreds of human-bone carvings hanging from branches, clicking in the wind, each one a record of someone taken.
3. **The Negotiation**: Rawiri standing among the Patupaiarehe, speaking words he learned hours ago, the guardians circling him, deciding whether to accept or add him to their collection.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Bone Tomahawk meets Wind River
**Book comparables:** The Luminaries by Eleanor Catton (for NZ atmosphere); The Troop by Nick Cutter (for wilderness horror)

## Tone and Pacing Notes
This book moves at the pace of an investigation — methodical, procedural, building evidence — until supernatural encounters interrupt with visceral horror. The landscape is a character, constantly described. Maori cultural elements are presented respectfully, with the narrative voice shifting as Rawiri reconnects with his heritage. The climax is a negotiation, not a battle — tense and strange rather than violent.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Maori names and terms require attention: Rawiri (rah-WEE-ree), Tamati (tah-MAH-tee), Patupaiarehe (pah-too-pie-ah-REH-heh), tapu (TAH-poo), whakapapa (fah-kah-PAH-pah). Place names: Tikitiki (tee-kee-TEE-kee), Hikurangi (hee-koo-RAHNG-ee). The narrator should approach Maori terms with respect rather than hesitation. Guardians do not speak — their presence is described, not vocalized.

# The Binding

## Genre
supernatural-thriller

## One-Line Premise
A Prague antiquarian book dealer purchases a manuscript that writes itself — and discovers it's a contract that binds him to collect seven specific souls before the winter solstice or forfeit his own.

## Expanded Premise
Jakob Novak has spent thirty years trading rare books from his shop in Prague's Jewish Quarter, inheriting his father's business and his father's secrets. When a dying collector offers him a medieval manuscript for a suspiciously low price, Jakob cannot resist. The book is blank — until it isn't. Text appears in ink that shouldn't exist, describing a contract: seven names, seven souls, collected before the solstice. Jakob is now the Collector, bound to the book, and if he fails to deliver, the book takes him instead. His only chance is to understand who made this contract, why these seven people are chosen, and whether there's a clause that lets him escape.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Jakob Novak
**Age:** 62
**Occupation / Background:** Third-generation antiquarian book dealer specializing in medieval manuscripts and Jewish mystical texts. His grandfather was a Kabbalist; his father pretended the traditions didn't exist. Jakob falls somewhere between.
**Core skill:** Textual analysis — Jakob can date, authenticate, and interpret manuscripts others cannot read. He knows how books work as objects and as vessels.
**Fatal flaw:** Greed disguised as scholarship. He took the book knowing something was wrong because he couldn't bear to let it pass to someone else.
**Character arc:** Believes knowledge is neutral — books are just objects. Discovers that some knowledge is alive and hungry, and that his family's tradition of binding such books was not superstition.
**Voice and register:** Scholarly and precise, prone to digression into historical detail. Interior voice reveals someone who has used erudition to avoid emotional connection his entire life.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The Sefer ha-Qinyan (Book of Acquisition) — a living manuscript created in 1589 by Rabbi Judah Loew and a consortium of European mystics to contain a demon. The demon is the book now, bound into its pages, and it collects souls through whoever holds the contract.
**Goal:** To complete its collection. Every century, it requires seven specific souls to sustain its existence. If it fails, it dissolves — but it has never failed, because it always finds a Collector.
**Method:** The book writes names; the Collector must bring these people to contact the book before the solstice. The touch is enough — the book does the rest.
**Connection to protagonist:** Jakob's grandfather was a previous Collector who completed his contract. The book recognizes the bloodline and chose Jakob deliberately.

## Supporting Characters

**Dr. Helena Králová** — Czech historian and occult researcher, estranged daughter of one of the named souls. She becomes Jakob's reluctant partner when she realizes her father is on the list.

**Rabbi Yitzchak Stern** — Elderly Kabbalist in Prague's surviving Jewish community, one of the few who knows the book exists and how it was made.

**Emil Novak** — Jakob's nephew, who inherits the shop if Jakob dies. He doesn't believe in the supernatural — until he has to.

## Setting

**Primary location:** Prague, Czech Republic — the Jewish Quarter and surrounding old city
**Time period:** Present day, December — counting down to the winter solstice
**Atmosphere:** Medieval architecture under Christmas lights, snow falling on cobblestones, the layered history of a city that has housed mystics, alchemists, and scholars for a thousand years. The claustrophobia of narrow streets and ancient buildings pressing close.
**Key locations:** Novak's antiquarian shop on Maiselova Street; the Old Jewish Cemetery (tombs stacked seven deep); Prague Castle's hidden archives; the crypt beneath the Old-New Synagogue; a manor house outside the city where one of the seven hides.

## Central Conflict
Jakob must find a way to break the contract without completing it — saving both himself and the seven named souls. The book cannot be destroyed while active; it must be bound by the same Kabbalistic methods used to create it. But the last person who knew those methods died in 1944, and their knowledge is scattered across occupied Europe's ruins.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The Sefer ha-Qinyan is finally bound or destroyed, ending centuries of collection. The seven souls live. Jakob loses the book but keeps his life.
**If the protagonist fails:** The seven die, adding to the book's count. Jakob becomes part of the book's binding, a ghost voice in its pages for eternity.
**Personal stakes:** Jakob has avoided his heritage his entire life. Saving himself means embracing the traditions his father rejected and his grandfather practiced — becoming what he refused to be.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Jakob purchases the blank manuscript from a dying collector who weeps with relief as it leaves his hands. That night, text appears — Jakob's name as Collector, seven other names below.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Jakob researches the manuscript's history, finding fragmentary references to "the book that writes" and its century-cycle. He identifies the first name on the list — a Prague city councillor.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Attempting to warn the councillor, Jakob accidentally causes him to touch the manuscript. The man ages to death in seconds. The book absorbs something and marks the first name complete. Six remain.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Horrified, Jakob seeks help from Rabbi Stern, who explains the book's creation and purpose. It can be re-bound, but the ritual requires components scattered when the Nazis destroyed Prague's Kabbalistic archives.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Helena Králová, researcher, confronts Jakob — her father is on the list. They form an alliance. Together, they locate the first component: a binding chain hidden in the crypt beneath the Old-New Synagogue.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: The book resists. It shows Jakob visions of his grandfather completing his own collection — murdering seven people to survive. The book implies Jakob is already damned by bloodline. Helena's father dies before they can protect him — she blames Jakob.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Helena abandons him. Four names remain, three weeks until solstice. Jakob cannot stop the collections — the book writes, and people die. He drinks. He considers letting the book take him.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Rabbi Stern, dying of natural causes, offers himself as the final component — his knowledge can substitute for what was lost, but only if Jakob leads the ritual. Jakob must accept his grandfather's tradition to have any chance.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The solstice approaches. Two names remain, including Jakob's nephew Emil. Helena returns, having found the final binding words in her father's research. Together, they confront the book directly, initiating the re-binding ritual as it fights back with visions, promises, and threats.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The binding succeeds — barely. The book is sealed, the demon trapped within reduced to dormancy. Two souls were lost, but Emil and the seventh survive. Jakob inherits his grandfather's true legacy: not collecting, but binding. He becomes the guardian of dangerous books.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** A cursed book forcing a man to kill.
**Deep theme:** We cannot escape our heritage by ignoring it — we can only learn to carry it responsibly.
**Thematic question:** What do we owe the traditions we abandon?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The First Collection**: The councillor touching the book accidentally, his face aging through decades in seconds, his body collapsing to dust while Jakob screams.
2. **The Crypt Descent**: Jakob and Helena descending into the sealed crypt beneath the synagogue, finding the binding chains amid bones and shadows, the book sending visions to stop them.
3. **The Solstice Ritual**: The climactic binding in the book shop as snow falls outside — candles, Hebrew prayers, the book manifesting its demon face as Jakob and Helena speak the words that trap it.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Ninth Gate meets A Dark Song
**Book comparables:** The Golem and the Jinni by Helene Wecker; Johannes Cabal the Necromancer by Jonathan L. Howard

## Tone and Pacing Notes
This book moves at the pace of research — methodical discovery punctuated by horror when the book acts. Prague's winter atmosphere saturates everything: cold, dark, ancient. The countdown to the solstice creates urgency that increases chapter by chapter. Dialogue is dense with historical and mystical detail, but action sequences are visceral and fast.

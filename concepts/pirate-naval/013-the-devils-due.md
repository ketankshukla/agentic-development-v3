# The Devil's Due

## Genre
pirate-naval

## One-Line Premise
A Baltimore privateer must destroy a British gold convoy to ransom his captured crew—but the convoy commander is the man who once saved his life.

## Expanded Premise
Captain Jack Rourke built the fastest schooner on the Chesapeake and made his fortune raiding British merchantmen during the War of 1812. Then HMS *Dauntless* captured his crew while he was ashore. Captain James Everett offers terms: bring him the shipping schedule for the Royal Navy's gold convoy, and the crew goes free. Refuse, and they hang as pirates. Jack's crew includes his son. He steals the intelligence—but he's not going to deliver it. Instead, he'll use it to take the convoy himself. Except Everett saved Jack's life at Tripoli, before the war that made them enemies, and debts in this world carry weight.

## Protagonist

**Name:** John "Jack" Rourke
**Age:** 44
**Occupation / Background:** Master shipbuilder and privateer captain from Baltimore. He built *The Defiant* with his own hands and knows her capabilities better than he knows his own. Before the war, he was a merchant captain; before that, a young officer in the fledgling U.S. Navy.
**Core skill:** Ship design and handling—he built *The Defiant* to be the fastest schooner ever launched, and he sails her like she's part of him. He reads wind and water with almost supernatural precision.
**Fatal flaw:** His honor demands he repay debts, but his honor also demands he protect his crew. These obligations conflict, and the conflict nearly paralyzes him.
**Character arc:** Begins seeing the world in simple terms: Americans versus British, captor versus captive, debt versus duty. Discovers that loyalty to his country and loyalty to his conscience are not always the same thing. Ends choosing honor over vengeance and finding freedom in the choice.
**Voice and register:** Plain-spoken, practical, with occasional flashes of wit. He sounds like a working man who's read more books than he admits, comfortable in taverns and counting houses alike.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Captain James Everett, RN, commanding HMS *Dauntless*, a frigate hunting American privateers
**Goal:** Protect the gold convoy, capture American raiders, and serve his King—while honoring a debt he never expected to pay
**Method:** Professional, competent, and honorable by his own lights. He doesn't enjoy the war but prosecutes it efficiently. He respects Jack and hates that he must destroy him.
**Connection to protagonist:** During the Barbary Wars, Jack pulled Everett from a sinking boat at Tripoli, saving his life. They spent six weeks together as prisoners. Everett owes a debt he cannot pay without betraying his duty.

## Supporting Characters

**Samuel Rourke** — Jack's son, eighteen, captured with the crew. Headstrong, idealistic, he joined his father's ship against Jack's wishes and now might die for it.

**Marie Laveau** — A free woman of color who runs intelligence for American privateers out of New Orleans. She provides the convoy intelligence—for a price, and with her own agenda.

**First Mate O'Brien** — The only officer who escaped capture, now sailing with Jack. Irish-born, experienced, cynical—he's the voice of practicality when Jack risks everything for honor.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The American eastern seaboard and the Caribbean—from Baltimore harbor to the British convoy routes, the neutral waters of New Orleans, and the open Atlantic
**Time period:** 1813-1814, during the War of 1812
**Atmosphere:** Low-slung Baltimore clippers racing under every scrap of sail. The brick warehouses and smoky taverns of Fells Point. Caribbean heat and hurricane season. Prize money and letters of marque. The constant knowledge that capture means the noose, regardless of paperwork.
**Key locations:** Baltimore's shipyards, the British naval station at Bermuda, the neutral port of New Orleans, the prize court at Halifax (mentioned), the open water where convoys are most vulnerable

## Central Conflict
Jack must take the convoy, free his crew, and navigate his debt to Everett—a man who is both his enemy and the only officer who might treat his people honorably. The closer Jack gets to his goal, the more he understands that total victory might require betraying everything he believes.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** His crew is freed, the gold funds the struggling American war effort, and Jack proves that a single ship can change history.
**If the protagonist fails:** Samuel hangs, the war effort collapses, and Jack lives as the man who let his son die.
**Personal stakes:** The debt to Everett. If Jack destroys Everett, he becomes something he despises. If he spares Everett, he might lose the battle.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Jack receives Everett's ultimatum via released prisoner. The crew, including Samuel, will hang unless Jack provides intelligence. The deadline: three weeks.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Jack contacts Marie Laveau and secures the convoy schedule—then reveals his real plan to O'Brien. Not delivery. Capture. O'Brien thinks he's insane.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: *The Defiant* raids a British supply ship for weapons and information. A close call with a Royal Navy sloop. Jack's reputation means every British ship will hunt him.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Jack recruits additional privateers—three captains willing to share the risk and reward. They form a small squadron, the closest thing to an American navy in these waters.
- Ch 5 — The Push: The squadron ambushes the convoy's lead elements. Success—two prize ships captured, scattered survivors. The main convoy alters course, but Jack knows where it must go.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Everett's *Dauntless* catches Jack separated from his squadron. Running battle. *The Defiant* escapes but O'Brien is killed. Jack is alone, ship damaged.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Repairing at a hidden cove, Jack confronts what he's become. He's sacrificed his best friend for a plan that might not work. Samuel's face haunts him. Is this revenge or rescue?
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: A message from Everett, delivered by fishing boat. Everett knows Jack is coming. He offers a duel—ship to ship, personal honor—to settle everything. Winner takes all.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: *The Defiant* versus *Dauntless* in open water. A battle fought by captains who respect each other. Fire, shot, the screaming of wounded wood. Jack boards the frigate at the end, facing Everett on the quarterdeck.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Everett yields. Samuel is freed. The gold is captured. But Jack cannot take Everett prisoner—the debt demands he let him go. Everett sails for Bermuda, broken but alive. Jack returns to Baltimore a hero who knows what heroism cost.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** The privateer as American hero
**Deep theme:** The impossible weight of competing loyalties—family, country, conscience, and honor all demanding different choices
**Thematic question:** Can you fight honorably in a dishonorable war, and what do we owe our enemies when they're better men than we deserve?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Baltimore Launch**: *The Defiant* racing down the Chesapeake past British blockade ships, Jack at the wheel, every sail set, the fastest ship in America earning her name.
2. **The Convoy Ambush**: Four American privateers surrounding British merchantmen. Cannons roar. Prize crews leap across. Gold bars glimpsed in the holds.
3. **Ship to Ship**: *The Defiant's* speed versus *Dauntless's* firepower. Jack threading through cannon fire, closing the range, the boarding action that decides everything.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *Master and Commander* meets *The Last of the Mohicans*; the nautical precision of *Hornblower* with the personal stakes of *3:10 to Yuma*
**Book comparables:** *Pride and Prometheus* (nautical romance era) by John Kessel; Patrick O'Brian's Aubrey-Maturin novels

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Propulsive, practical, patriotic without jingoism. The naval action is technically precise—these ships feel real. But the emotional core is the impossible choice Jack faces. The prose moves fast; the dilemmas cut deep. The ending should satisfy and unsettle in equal measure.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
American names are straightforward: Rourke (ROARK), O'Brien (oh-BRY-en). British naval terms and ranks should be clear. Jack's voice should sound authentically early American—direct, slightly rough, no aristocratic affectation. Everett's British precision contrasts. Marie Laveau's French-inflected English adds texture.

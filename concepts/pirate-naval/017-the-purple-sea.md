# The Purple Sea

## Genre
pirate-naval

## One-Line Premise
A Phoenician merchant captain discovers that Assyrian conquest of his home city is imminent—and must unite the trading colonies of the western Mediterranean into a navy before their civilization is erased.

## Expanded Premise
Hanno of Tyre commands the swiftest ship in the Phoenician fleet, carrying tin from Britain and silver from Iberia. When he returns from a year-long voyage, he finds Tyre already paying tribute to Assyria—and learns that King Sennacherib plans to destroy the city entirely, ending Phoenician independence forever. The great cities of the Levant will fall. Only the western colonies—Carthage, Utica, Gades—might survive, but they've never fought as one. Hanno must sail west, convince fractious colonial governors to unite, and build a navy capable of destroying Assyrian transport ships in open water. He has three months before the invasion fleet launches.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Hanno, son of Hiram, of Tyre
**Age:** 45
**Occupation / Background:** Master merchant captain, third generation in the trade. His family built the route from Tyre to the Tin Islands. He has personally sailed farther than any Phoenician alive.
**Core skill:** Navigation beyond sight of land—he reads stars, currents, and bird migration to cross open water where other captains hug the coast. He also knows every harbor, every governor, and every trade debt from Tyre to the western edge of the world.
**Fatal flaw:** He believes in commerce over conflict. His instinct is to negotiate, to find the deal that makes fighting unnecessary. This makes him slow to accept that some enemies cannot be bargained with.
**Character arc:** Begins as a merchant who sees war as a failure of imagination. Discovers that some values cannot be traded for—that preserving a civilization sometimes requires destroying its enemies. Ends becoming the warrior his people need while mourning the trader he was.
**Voice and register:** Precise, commercial, efficient. He calculates everything: distances, resources, loyalties. But beneath the merchant's practicality lies a poet who has seen the edge of the world.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** King Sennacherib of Assyria, whose armies have conquered everything from Babylon to Egypt—and who hates Tyre for a decade of defiance
**Goal:** Complete dominion over the Levantine coast, destruction of Phoenician sea power, and control of the Mediterranean trade that has enriched his enemies
**Method:** Overwhelming military force, terror as policy, and the use of conquered peoples as soldiers. His armies number in the hundreds of thousands.
**Connection to protagonist:** Sennacherib's father was killed by a conspiracy that included Hanno's uncle. The vendetta is personal.

## Supporting Characters

**Elissa** — A princess of Tyre fleeing the Assyrian approach, destined to found Carthage. She carries the sacred fire of Melqart and the legitimacy to unite the colonies. Her partnership with Hanno becomes the foundation of something greater than either.

**Admiral Magon** — Commander of Carthage's small fleet, skeptical of a Tyrian telling him how to fight. His pride is the obstacle Hanno must overcome.

**The Navigator** — An unnamed Egyptian pilot who claims to have circumnavigated Africa. He provides the key to Hanno's strategy: approaching the invasion fleet from the unexpected direction.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Mediterranean Sea—from Tyre to Carthage, the Strait of Gibraltar (the Pillars of Melqart), the coasts of Iberia and North Africa
**Time period:** 701 BCE, during Sennacherib's campaigns against the Levantine coast
**Atmosphere:** Wine-dark waters under impossible stars. Galleys driven by oars and sail, trading the same routes for generations. The purple dye that made Phoenician fortunes staining hands and harbor water. Cities of cedar and stone rising from coastal rocks. The smell of salt, commerce, and cedar resin.
**Key locations:** Tyre's island fortress, Carthage's new harbor, the Pillars of Melqart (Gibraltar), the Assyrian anchorage on the Lebanese coast, the open water where galleys become vulnerable

## Central Conflict
Hanno must convince rival colonies to contribute ships they need for their own defense, forge them into a fighting force that's never existed, and intercept the Assyrian invasion fleet before it reaches Tyre—knowing that even total victory may only buy time.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The invasion fleet burns, Tyre survives another generation, and Carthage emerges as the new center of Phoenician civilization.
**If the protagonist fails:** Tyre is destroyed (which historically happened), the colonies fall one by one, and Phoenician culture disappears from history rather than transforming into Carthaginian greatness.
**Personal stakes:** His family—parents, siblings, wife—remain in Tyre. If the city falls before he returns, they die.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Hanno's ship enters Tyre's harbor after a year away to find Assyrian tribute collectors and his family under house arrest. His father passes coded intelligence: invasion is coming.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Hanno escapes with Elissa and the sacred fire. The Assyrians pursue. They must reach Carthage before they're caught.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: An Assyrian patrol squadron intercepts them. Hanno's seamanship saves the ship, but the cost is high—he reaches Carthage with a damaged vessel and wounded crew.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Hanno addresses the colonial assembly. They refuse to commit ships for a homeland that taxed them for centuries. Only Elissa's religious authority prevents outright rejection.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Hanno wins over Magon by navigating a legendary strait that Magon thought impossible. The admiral commits Carthage's fleet—but other colonies remain reluctant.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: News arrives: Sidon has fallen. The invasion fleet is launching early. There's no time to gather more ships. They must fight with what they have.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: The allied fleet sails toward impossible odds—fifty galleys against two hundred Assyrian transports with warship escorts. Hanno considers whether he's leading his people to death.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: The Navigator reveals his secret: a route through the Strait that emerges behind the Assyrian fleet. They can achieve surprise—but only if they trust a man who might be lying.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The Battle of the Cedar Coast. Fire ships, ramming, boarding actions. Hanno's galley smashes into the command transport. The destruction of an empire's reach for the sea.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The invasion fleet burns, but Tyre is not saved—only delayed. Hanno returns home knowing his victory bought years, not peace. But years are enough. Elissa sails west to found a new city. The center of Phoenician civilization has shifted—and survived.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Uniting scattered colonies against extinction
**Deep theme:** The transformation of identity—when your homeland dies, what survives is what you become
**Thematic question:** Is it better to preserve a civilization by changing it completely, or to die preserving what it was?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Escape from Tyre**: Hanno's ship slipping past Assyrian patrols at night, Elissa carrying the sacred fire in a bronze vessel, the lights of home fading behind them.
2. **The Strait at Dawn**: Hanno's fleet emerging from morning mist, the Assyrian anchorage spread before them like sheep before wolves, the moment before chaos.
3. **The Burning Fleet**: Bronze rams smashing through wooden hulls. Fire spreading ship to ship. Hanno fighting his way to the Assyrian admiral's deck.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *300* meets *Troy*; the civilizational stakes of *Exodus: Gods and Kings* with the naval warfare of *Ben-Hur*
**Book comparables:** *Salammbô* by Gustave Flaubert; *The Purple Quest* by Frank G. Slaughter

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Ancient, epic, tinged with inevitable doom. The Phoenicians were real—and they really fell. This book doesn't pretend otherwise, but it finds victory in transformation rather than simple survival. The prose should feel old without being archaic. Naval sequences are bronze-age brutal. The relationship between Hanno and Elissa provides emotional grounding.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Phoenician names use simplified pronunciation: Hanno (HAH-noh), Hiram (HY-ram), Elissa (eh-LIS-ah), Magon (MAH-gon). Place names: Tyre (TIRE), Carthage (KAR-thij), Sidon (SY-don). Assyrian names: Sennacherib (sen-NAK-er-ib). Terms like Melqart (mel-KART) should be spoken clearly. The ancient setting should be audible in formal phrasing, but accessibility matters more than period authenticity.

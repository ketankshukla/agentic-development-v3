# Daughter of the Horizon

## Genre
pirate-naval

## One-Line Premise
The last trained wayfinder of a dying navigation tradition must unite warring island nations to destroy a colonial armada before her people are erased from history.

## Expanded Premise
Moana Temauri carries the star paths in her blood—she is one of the last wayfinders trained to navigate by wave patterns, stars, and the flight of birds across thousands of miles of open ocean. When Spanish conquistadors establish a fortress on her home island and begin capturing islanders for the slave mines of Peru, Moana flees with a single outrigger canoe and a desperate plan. She must find the scattered islands of her ancestors, convince chieftains who have warred for generations to unite their war canoes, and lead a fleet against iron ships and cannon. Her navigational gift is the key—but the stars offer no guidance for the waters she must sail through human hearts.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Moana Temauri
**Age:** 29
**Occupation / Background:** Wayfinder—navigator of the sacred voyaging tradition passed from master to student for a thousand years. She trained under her grandfather, the last master, who died before completing her instruction.
**Core skill:** Wayfinding—she reads the ocean itself. The shape of swells, the color of water, the behavior of birds, the angle of stars. She can navigate a thousand miles without instruments. She also understands the sea's moods: when to sail and when to shelter.
**Fatal flaw:** Isolation. She has spent her life observing rather than connecting. She can read the sea but struggles to read people, and her conviction that she must do everything herself nearly destroys the alliance she builds.
**Character arc:** Begins believing the sea is her true home and human connection is a distraction from her sacred duty. Discovers that wayfinding was never meant to be done alone—the tradition was always about connecting people across impossible distances. Ends understanding that her greatest voyage is bringing her scattered people together.
**Voice and register:** Spare, poetic, deeply observational of nature. She describes human behavior with the same clinical precision she applies to wave patterns—until emotion breaks through, and then she's raw.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Capitán Rodrigo de Mendoza, commander of the Spanish colonial fortress and slaving operation
**Goal:** Establish a permanent base for the Lima slave trade, convert the islands to profitable labor sources, and earn promotion to the Viceroyalty's inner circle
**Method:** Strategic brutality—public executions to break resistance, playing chieftains against each other with trade goods and muskets, building a native auxiliary force of collaborators
**Connection to protagonist:** Mendoza captured Moana's younger brother in the first raid. He uses the brother as leverage, knowing the wayfinder will come for him.

## Supporting Characters

**Chief Taranga** — An aging warrior queen who rules the most powerful island confederation. She lost her sons to war and sees Moana as either a useful tool or a dangerous idealist who will get more of her people killed.

**Koa** — A young warrior from a rival island who becomes Moana's first and most loyal convert. Hotheaded, skilled with the shark-tooth club, he represents the generation that must either unite or die.

**Father Sebastián** — A Spanish missionary who has grown disillusioned with the conquest. He provides Moana with intelligence about Mendoza's plans, seeking a redemption he knows he doesn't deserve.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The South Pacific—the Society Islands, the Tuamotu Archipelago, and the scattered high islands and atolls between them
**Time period:** 1595, during the early Spanish exploration of the Pacific
**Atmosphere:** Turquoise lagoons sheltered by coral reefs. Open ocean swells that roll unbroken for two thousand miles. The smell of salt and frangipani. War canoes with carved prows cutting through moonlit waters. The fortress at the harbor mouth, Spanish cannon pointing at the village that once held only prayer and fish.
**Key locations:** Moana's destroyed home island of Te Fatu, the hidden sacred island where wayfinders trained for generations, Chief Taranga's fortified harbor, the Spanish fortress of Santa Maria del Mar, the open ocean crossing where the war fleet must survive

## Central Conflict
Moana must unite island nations that have warred for centuries, teach them to fight as a single fleet, and destroy Spanish ships that outgun them completely—using only their knowledge of wind, wave, and the sea that raised them.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The Spanish fortress falls, the slave trade is broken, and her people survive to adapt to the changed world on their own terms.
**If the protagonist fails:** The slave ships keep coming, the islands are depopulated, and a thousand years of voyaging tradition dies with her.
**Personal stakes:** Her brother Tane is held in the fortress. Every day of delay is another day of his suffering.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Moana returns from a trading voyage to find her island burning. Spanish soldiers round up survivors. She escapes, but her brother is taken.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Hiding on a nearby atoll, Moana receives word that a slave ship is coming for a second wave. She cannot save her brother alone. She needs a fleet.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Moana approaches Chief Taranga's island and is seized as a spy. She must prove her value as a wayfinder by reading conditions the chief's navigators cannot—and survives.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Moana proposes alliance. Taranga agrees conditionally: bring in the rival islands first. Moana sails into enemy waters carrying only her canoe and her arguments.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Against odds, three islands join. Koa becomes her champion. A combined fleet of forty war canoes assembles. They drill, they practice, they begin to believe.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: A collaborator betrays them to Mendoza. A Spanish ship catches the fleet at anchor. Fifteen canoes burn. Koa is badly wounded. Chief Taranga blames Moana.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: The alliance crumbles. Moana is exiled, sailing alone toward the sacred wayfinding island—perhaps to die, perhaps to find an answer in the old ways.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: On the sacred island, Moana completes her grandfather's teaching by teaching it to herself. She realizes wayfinding isn't just about reading stars—it's about connecting. She returns with a strategy that turns Spanish advantages into weaknesses.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The war fleet attacks during a storm the Spanish don't know is coming—Moana read the signs they couldn't. Canoes swarm the fortress harbor. Fire. Boarding. Moana reaches the cells where Tane is held.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Mendoza dead, the fortress destroyed, Tane alive but changed by captivity. The fleet disperses—but the islands have learned they can fight together. Moana sails on, carrying the star paths to every island that will listen.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Indigenous resistance against colonialism
**Deep theme:** The choice between isolation and connection—survival requires both holding to tradition and reaching across old boundaries
**Thematic question:** Can a fractured people unite before it's too late, and what must be sacrificed to make them whole?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Impossible Crossing**: Moana leads the war fleet across three hundred miles of open ocean in conditions the Spanish believe are unsurvivable. Navigation by swells that only she can read, the fleet emerging from the storm as if from myth.
2. **The Night of Fire Ships**: Canoes packed with dried palm fronds glide into the harbor at midnight. The Spanish ships burn. War canoes surge through the firelight, warriors leaping onto fortress walls.
3. **The Final Wave**: The climactic assault on the fortress. Moana confronting Mendoza on the sea wall, the battle raging below, her brother's life in the balance—and the ocean itself seeming to rise against the colonizers.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *Moana* (the Disney film) meets *Apocalypto*; the cultural resistance of *Avatar* with the naval tactics of *Master and Commander*
**Book comparables:** *The Bone People* by Keri Hulme; *Sea People: The Puzzle of Polynesia* by Christina Thompson (for the awe of traditional navigation)

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Slow, deep breathing in the navigation sequences—the patience required to read the sea should be palpable. The political sequences move faster, sharp dialogue and compressed tension. Battle sequences are furious and chaotic. Throughout, the ocean is almost a character: beautiful, deadly, indifferent, and somehow sacred.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Polynesian names use standard English-compatible spelling: Moana (moh-AH-nah), Temauri (teh-MAH-oo-ree), Taranga (tah-RAHN-gah), Tane (TAH-nay). Avoid any macron characters. Spanish names and terms are straightforward. The wayfinding sequences should be narrated slowly, meditatively—the pacing is part of the meaning.

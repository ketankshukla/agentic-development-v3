# The Spice Trade

## Genre
pirate-naval

## One-Line Premise
A Dutch company captain discovers his employers are massacring an island to control nutmeg—and must become a pirate to stop genocide and destroy the corporation from within.

## Expanded Premise
Pieter van Hoorn was a loyal captain of the VOC—the Dutch East India Company—until he witnessed the massacre at Banda. Company soldiers murdered fifteen thousand islanders to seize the nutmeg monopoly. When Pieter refused orders to execute survivors, his career ended and his death warrant began. Now he commands a stolen company ship with a crew of deserters, Bandanese survivors, and English rivals, raiding VOC spice convoys and exposing the Company's atrocities to the world. But Governor-General Jan Coen will stop at nothing to silence him—and Coen commands the most powerful private navy on earth.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Pieter van Hoorn
**Age:** 36
**Occupation / Background:** Former VOC senior captain, son of a Company director, raised to believe the VOC brought civilization and prosperity to the Indies. His family's fortune is built on spice money.
**Core skill:** Navigation of the Indies—he knows every current, monsoon pattern, and hidden channel from Java to the Moluccas. He also knows VOC shipping routes, schedules, and weaknesses intimately.
**Fatal flaw:** Guilt. He was complicit in Company crimes for years before Banda broke him. He struggles to believe he deserves redemption, which makes him reckless with his own life.
**Character arc:** Begins seeking absolution through violence—if he destroys enough Company ships, maybe the balance tips. Discovers that redemption comes not from destroying evil but from protecting the survivors. Ends finding purpose in building rather than breaking.
**Voice and register:** Formal Dutch cadences beneath learned English, the voice of an educated man whose certainties have shattered. He speaks carefully, as if testing each word against what he now knows to be true.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Jan Pieterszoon Coen, Governor-General of the Dutch East Indies, architect of the Banda genocide
**Goal:** Complete monopoly control of the spice trade, elimination of all rivals (European and indigenous), and the personal wealth and glory this will bring
**Method:** Overwhelming corporate power: twelve thousand soldiers, hundreds of ships, authority to make war without consulting Amsterdam. He is the Company incarnate.
**Connection to protagonist:** Coen personally promoted Pieter three times. He saw Pieter as a protégé—ruthless, competent, loyal. Pieter's betrayal is personal.

## Supporting Characters

**Bandanese Navigator** — Amal, a survivor of the massacre whose entire family was murdered. He guides Pieter through waters no Dutch chart records. His presence is Pieter's constant reminder of what was done.

**English Privateer** — Captain Jonas Blackwood, operating under letters of marque against Dutch shipping. An unlikely ally who sees profit in Pieter's war but slowly develops genuine respect.

**Company Spy** — Helena van der Berg, a Dutch merchant's widow who trades in secrets. She provides intelligence on Company movements but her ultimate loyalties remain unclear.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Indonesian Archipelago—from Batavia (Jakarta) to the Banda Islands, the Straits of Malacca, and the scattered spice islands of the Moluccas
**Time period:** 1621-1623, during the VOC's consolidation of the spice monopoly
**Atmosphere:** Volcanic islands rising from impossibly blue seas. The overwhelming fragrance of nutmeg and clove. Bamboo ports where a dozen flags fly and a hundred languages mingle. Dutch forts with cannon and counting houses, built on indigenous bones. Heat that never relents.
**Key locations:** The ruins of the Banda Islands (site of the massacre), the VOC fortress at Batavia, hidden pirate harbors in the Riau Archipelago, the spice loading docks at Ambon, the open water where convoys are most vulnerable

## Central Conflict
Pieter must intercept evidence of VOC atrocities and deliver it to Amsterdam, where it might turn public opinion—while evading Coen's fleet, protecting the Bandanese survivors who've joined him, and confronting his own complicity in the system he's trying to destroy.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The massacre becomes known in Europe, public outrage constrains the VOC, and surviving Bandanese might retain some homeland.
**If the protagonist fails:** The genocide is erased from history, Coen's methods become standard practice, and Pieter's crew dies as pirates.
**Personal stakes:** Pieter's father still sits on the Company board. Exposing the VOC means destroying his family. He must choose between bloodline and conscience.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Pieter witnesses the final executions at Banda—the elite he was ordered to kill. He refuses. His lieutenant attempts to arrest him; Pieter kills him and flees with a handful of loyal crew.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: In hiding, Pieter learns that documents exist proving the massacre was ordered from Amsterdam—not just Coen's madness but Company policy. Those documents could change everything.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Pieter raids his first VOC ship, seeking the documents. They're not there—but he gains crew, a captured ship, and Coen's full attention.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Alliance with Captain Blackwood. The combined force raids a spice convoy. Success—but Bandanese crew members want not just piracy but vengeance. Pieter must balance their demands.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Helena provides intelligence: the documents are in Batavia, in Coen's personal vault. An impossible target—but Helena knows a way in.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: The infiltration partially succeeds—they get some documents—but Coen springs a trap. Amal is captured. Pieter barely escapes. The partial evidence might not be enough.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Coen sends Pieter's father as an emissary. The old man offers a deal: return, accept exile, and the family name survives. Pieter confronts everything his family represented.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Pieter burns his father's letter. He will rescue Amal and take the remaining documents—or die trying. He's no longer fighting for redemption but for the people who trust him.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Attack on Batavia harbor itself. Fire ships, diversionary raids, Pieter leading a boarding party onto Coen's flagship while Blackwood's force assaults the fortress. Amal rescued. Documents seized.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Pieter sails for Amsterdam with the evidence. Coen survives but is recalled, disgraced. The Bandanese cannot go home—there's no home left—but they sail with Pieter to a new life. The Company endures, but its crimes are known.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** One man against a corporation
**Deep theme:** Complicity and redemption—we cannot undo what we enabled, but we can stop enabling it
**Thematic question:** What do we owe the victims of systems we benefited from, and is exposure of evil enough or must there be action?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Killing Field**: Pieter's flashback to Banda—the systematic execution of the orang kaya, the island's elite. The moment he breaks.
2. **The Convoy Ambush**: Dutch and English pirates working together, surrounding VOC merchantmen heavy with spice. Boarding actions across multiple ships simultaneously.
3. **The Burning of Batavia**: Fire ships drifting into the harbor. Pieter's crew scaling the fortress walls. The climactic confrontation in Coen's counting house, surrounded by ledgers that record death in columns of profit.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *Blood Diamond* meets *Master and Commander*; the corporate evil of *The Constant Gardener* with the naval action of *Pirates of the Caribbean*
**Book comparables:** *Nathaniel's Nutmeg* by Giles Milton (narrative history); *Tai-Pan* by James Clavell (for colonial trading company drama)

## Tone and Pacing Notes
The horror of colonial violence is never gratuitous but never softened. The VOC is not cartoonishly evil—it's systematically evil, which is worse. Pieter's guilt weighs on every page; his moments of redemption are earned through action, not absolution. The naval sequences are sharp and technical; the political sequences are tense with competing agendas.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Dutch names use anglicized pronunciation: Pieter (PEE-ter), van Hoorn (van HORN), Coen (KOON). Indonesian names: Amal (ah-MAHL). VOC terminology should be clear: Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (fair-AY-nich-duh ohst-IN-dish-uh com-pahn-YEE), but "VOC" is fine for repeated reference. The contrast between Pieter's formal Dutch speech and Blackwood's rougher English should be audible.

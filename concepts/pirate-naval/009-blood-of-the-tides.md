# Blood of the Tides

## Genre
pirate-naval

## One-Line Premise
A Highland chieftain turned privateer must smuggle Bonnie Prince Charlie to safety across a sea patrolled by the Royal Navy—while hunting the English officer who murdered his clan.

## Expanded Premise
Alasdair MacKinnon watched British soldiers burn his village and execute his family after Culloden. He escaped to the sea with forty clansmen and a single birlinn, swearing vengeance. Three months later, he's become a ghost the Royal Navy fears—a privateer without a letter of marque, preying on English ships with Highland fury. Then word comes: Prince Charles, the cause they bled for, is hunted and alone on the Scottish coast. To reach France, he needs a captain willing to risk everything. Alasdair will carry the prince—but his true hunt is Captain William Ashcroft, the butcher of Clan MacKinnon, whose ship patrols these waters.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Alasdair MacKinnon of Strath
**Age:** 36
**Occupation / Background:** Former chieftain of Clan MacKinnon, educated in France, veteran of the Jacobite risings. He was raised to lead his people in war and peace; now he leads them in survival.
**Core skill:** Small-boat handling in treacherous coastal waters—he knows every rock, current, and hidden anchorage from Skye to Mull. He learned these waters as a boy running from excise men; now he uses them to destroy warships.
**Fatal flaw:** His grief has become rage. He struggles to distinguish between serving the cause and serving his need for vengeance, and this confusion endangers everyone who follows him.
**Character arc:** Begins seeing the prince's escape as secondary to his personal hunt for Ashcroft. Discovers that protecting the living matters more than avenging the dead. Ends choosing his people's future over his own satisfaction.
**Voice and register:** Formal Scots English with Gaelic rhythms beneath it. He speaks with the weight of a man accustomed to being obeyed, but exhaustion and grief have worn away the courtly polish.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Captain William Ashcroft, Royal Navy commander of the patrol frigate HMS *Vengeance*
**Goal:** Capture the Young Pretender and destroy the remnants of Highland resistance, earning the rewards and advancement this will bring
**Method:** Methodical hunting, interrogation of prisoners, and the liberal use of reprisals against coastal communities suspected of harboring rebels
**Connection to protagonist:** Ashcroft commanded the troops at Strath. He personally ordered the executions of Alasdair's father, wife, and children. He knows Alasdair survived and considers catching him a matter of professional pride.

## Supporting Characters

**Fiona MacLeod** — A fisherman's widow who runs the intelligence network that warns Highland fugitives of English patrols. Sharp-tongued, practical, and secretly in love with a cause she knows is doomed.

**Father Dougal** — A Jesuit priest traveling with the prince, serving as confessor and advisor. He urges Alasdair toward mercy and caution, which Alasdair resents but sometimes heeds.

**Callum** — Alasdair's nephew, seventeen, last surviving male of their direct line. He worships his uncle and wants to fight; Alasdair desperately wants to keep him alive.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The west coast of Scotland—the Inner Hebrides, the sea lochs of the Highlands, and the open waters between Scotland and France
**Time period:** September 1746, five months after Culloden
**Atmosphere:** Mist that clings to heather-covered mountains sliding down to the sea. Black lochs where a ship can vanish and patrol boats flounder on hidden rocks. Cold rain that never quite stops. The smell of peat smoke from villages that watch the sea and say nothing to strangers. Ancient stone castles crumbling above storm-wracked harbors.
**Key locations:** The hidden anchorage at Loch nan Uamh (where the prince waits), the killing grounds of the Sound of Mull, the open Atlantic west of the Hebrides, Ashcroft's patrol zone around the Isle of Skye, a secret cove on Eigg where intelligence is exchanged

## Central Conflict
Alasdair must smuggle Prince Charles past a naval blockade, with Ashcroft hunting him specifically, while managing a crew more interested in vengeance than royal missions—and confronting the moment when he must choose between killing Ashcroft and completing the mission.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The prince reaches France, the cause retains hope (however faint), and the MacKinnon survivors reach safety.
**If the protagonist fails:** Charles is captured and executed, the cause dies completely, and Alasdair's clan disappears from history.
**Personal stakes:** Callum. If Alasdair pursues Ashcroft instead of the mission, he risks his nephew's life. Vengeance may cost him everything he's trying to save.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Alasdair's birlinn ambushes an English supply vessel. In the captured dispatches: new intelligence on Ashcroft's patrol patterns—and word that someone important is hiding at Loch nan Uamh.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Alasdair reaches the loch and finds Prince Charles, broken and hunted. The prince's party offers gold for passage to France. Alasdair agrees—but the direct route crosses Ashcroft's hunting ground.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Running the Sound of Mull, they're spotted. A running chase through fog and rocks. Alasdair's seamanship saves them, but Ashcroft now knows he's active.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Ashcroft begins methodical sweeps. Alasdair hides in a sea loch while Fiona's network feeds false sightings. But time is running out—autumn storms will soon make the crossing impossible.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Alasdair lures one of Ashcroft's patrol boats into a trap. Victory—they sink her and capture prisoners. But one prisoner reveals where Ashcroft will be tomorrow night. The chance for vengeance.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Alasdair detours to ambush Ashcroft, leaving the prince exposed. Ashcroft springs a counter-trap. Callum is captured. The prince's party berates Alasdair for choosing revenge over duty.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Callum is held aboard the *Vengeance*. Alasdair knows Ashcroft will use the boy as bait. The crew is divided—some want rescue, others want to abandon the boy and complete the mission.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Father Dougal confronts Alasdair: which matters more—avenging the dead or saving the living? Alasdair chooses his nephew. The rescue plan will require abandoning any chance at Ashcroft.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: A two-pronged attack: fire ships to distract, Alasdair leading a boarding party to rescue Callum. The confrontation with Ashcroft on the frigate's deck. Alasdair defeats him—and chooses not to kill him, instead escaping with his nephew.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The prince reaches France. Alasdair's surviving clan scatters into exile. Ashcroft lives—but broken and disgraced. Alasdair sails for the Spanish service, carrying nothing but his sword and the living family he saved.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** The last gasp of a lost cause
**Deep theme:** Choosing the future over the past—when holding onto vengeance means losing what's left of what you love
**Thematic question:** How do we survive the death of everything we believed in, and what do we owe the living when the dead cry out for justice?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Sound of Mull Chase**: Alasdair's birlinn threading between rocks in dense fog, English frigate behind, the crew rowing to exhaustion while Alasdair reads currents by instinct.
2. **The Burning Ships**: Fire ships drifting into Ashcroft's anchorage at night. The *Vengeance* cuts her cables to escape. Alasdair's boarding party climbs the frigate's chains in the chaos.
3. **Steel on the Quarterdeck**: Alasdair and Ashcroft, broadswords in the firelight. The duel that doesn't end in death—and the walk away that costs more than killing.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *Rob Roy* meets *Master and Commander*; the Highland tragedy of *Outlander* with the naval pursuit of *Hornblower*
**Book comparables:** *Kidnapped* by Robert Louis Stevenson; *Dragonfly in Amber* by Diana Gabaldon

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Elegiac but fierce. The Jacobite cause is doomed—reader and protagonist both know it—which gives every victory a melancholy edge. Action sequences are fast and brutal, Highland warfare adapted to ships. The emotional climax is not the battle but the choice. Gaelic phrases add texture but should not require translation.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Scots names use standard pronunciation: Alasdair (AL-as-ter), MacKinnon (mak-KIN-on), MacLeod (mak-LOWD). Gaelic place names: Loch nan Uamh (lok nan OO-ah), Eigg (EGG). The Scots accent should be suggested but comprehensible—not a caricature. Highland formality in Alasdair's speech contrasts with the prince's cultivated French-tinged English.

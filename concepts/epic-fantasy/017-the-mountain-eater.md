# The Mountain Eater

## Genre
Epic Fantasy

## One-Line Premise
A geomancer who can move mountains discovers her homeland is built on the back of a sleeping titan—and it's about to wake up hungry.

## Expanded Premise
Nüwa has spent her life reshaping the earth for the Emperor's roads, dams, and fortresses—mundane work for someone with the power to split continents. When tremors unlike any earthquake shake the Celestial Kingdom, she investigates and discovers the horrifying truth: the mountain range at the kingdom's heart is not geology. It is a titan, curled in sleep, and someone is deliberately waking it. The Mountain Eater, as ancient texts call it, consumed three civilizations before falling asleep millennia ago. Now cults believe awakening it will bring ascension, while Nüwa must find a way to either return it to sleep or—if that fails—move an empire off its back before it rises.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Nüwa Chen
**Age:** 39
**Occupation / Background:** Imperial Geomancer, Second Class; she has served the Celestial Court for eighteen years, building infrastructure and preventing natural disasters; daughter of miners who died in a collapse she couldn't prevent
**Core skill:** Geomancy of unprecedented power—she can move earth, shape stone, and sense deep geological structures; with effort, she could reshape landscapes
**Fatal flaw:** She has spent her life making herself small, using her power only as directed, afraid of what she might become if she acted freely
**Character arc:** Begins as a government functionary afraid of her own potential; forced to embrace her full power; ends understanding that power is only dangerous when unguided by conscience
**Voice and register:** Practical, slightly bureaucratic, with moments of awe at the earth's secrets. Her internal voice is more lyrical, more connected to the land she shapes.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The Awakening Cult, led by the Sage of the Deep Root—and the Mountain Eater itself, indifferent to humanity as a whale is to barnacles
**Goal:** The cult believes awakening the titan will force the gods to intervene, bringing a new golden age; the titan simply hungers
**Method:** Ritual destabilization of key geological points, assassination of geomancers who could stop them, manipulation of court politics to prevent intervention
**Connection to protagonist:** The Sage was Nüwa's first teacher, who left imperial service when his theories about the mountains were dismissed as madness

## Supporting Characters

**General Zhao Wei** — Commander of the Imperial Northern Army, pragmatic and skeptical; he doesn't believe in titans but does believe in Nüwa's skills; becomes her unlikely protector
**Mei-Li** — A young geomancer apprentice who idolizes Nüwa; her fresh perspective helps when Nüwa's experience becomes a liability
**The Earthsinger** — An ancient spirit bound in the mountains, one of those who originally put the titan to sleep; weakened, cryptic, but necessary

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Celestial Kingdom, a vast empire inspired by Ming Dynasty China, dominated by a massive mountain range that runs through its center
**Time period:** A secondary-world equivalent of 15th century, with advanced bureaucracy, impressive engineering, and state-controlled magic
**Atmosphere:** Mist-shrouded peaks, terraced rice paddies, fortress cities built into cliffsides. The empire is orderly on the surface, but the earth beneath is restless—tremors, new hot springs, mountains that seem to shift between visits.
**Key locations:** The Imperial Geomancy Bureau in the capital, the Sleeping Range (the titan's spine), the Deep Root Temple (cult headquarters), the Heart Cavern (center of the titan), the Ancient Boundary Stones (old sleeping spell anchors)

## Central Conflict
Nüwa must race across the empire, finding and restoring the magical anchors that keep the titan asleep while cultists try to destroy them—and she must confront her former teacher, a man she respected, who believes he's saving the world through apocalypse.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The titan sleeps, the empire survives, and geomancy is reformed to prevent future crises
**If the protagonist fails:** The Mountain Eater rises, consuming millions; the empire falls; Nüwa probably dies trying to stop it anyway
**Personal stakes:** Nüwa must face her fear of her own power and accept that being useful means being dangerous; she also must stop someone she loved without destroying what she learned from him

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Routine work, the strange earthquake, Nüwa's investigation revealing the mountains are alive
- Ch 2 — The Pull: She reports to the court; no one believes her; she finds ancient texts confirming her discovery; the cult makes their first major move
- Ch 3 — First Contact: She reaches the first Boundary Stone as cultists try to destroy it; she defends it but the damage is done; the titan stirs visibly

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: General Zhao is assigned to support her; they begin the race to the remaining Stones; she meets Mei-Li
- Ch 5 — The Push: Three Stones restored; she contacts the Earthsinger; learns the sleeping spell can be renewed with enough power—power she has but has never used
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: The Sage reveals himself; two Stones are destroyed simultaneously; the titan's head rises from the mountain range
- Ch 7 — The Descent: The empire panics; evacuation begins; Nüwa realizes she may need to sacrifice herself to fuel the renewed spell
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: She confronts the Sage, learns his true motivation—grief over a devastated homeland the empire ignored; she cannot simply hate him, but she cannot let him win

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The final attempt at the Heart Cavern; Nüwa versus the Sage; the titan fully waking; the renewed sleeping spell
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The titan sleeps; Nüwa survives (barely) through unexpected help; the empire must rebuild its relationship with geomancers; she becomes something new—not a functionary, but a guardian

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** The danger of ignoring warnings until too late
**Deep theme:** Power requires responsibility, not hiding—and the greatest courage is using your strength when it scares you
**Thematic question:** When duty conflicts with conscience, and conscience with safety, what guides the right choice?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Rising Head**: The moment a mountain ridge reveals itself as a vast skull, ancient eyes opening, villages sliding off what was a cheek
2. **The Race to the Stones**: Nüwa shaping the earth to travel faster, leaping canyons, tunneling through mountains, geomancy as movement
3. **The Heart Cavern**: Inside the titan's body, stone veins like tunnels, Nüwa confronting the Sage while the heartbeat shakes the world

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Pacific Rim meets Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon
**Book comparables:** The Stone Sky by N.K. Jemisin, The Poppy War by R.F. Kuang

## Tone and Pacing Notes
This novel combines bureaucratic intrigue with kaiju-scale disaster. Early chapters should feel constrained, institutional; pacing explodes as the stakes become clear. The prose should be grounded in earth imagery, tactile and weighty, with moments of terrible beauty.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Nüwa's voice is professional but warms when she discusses the earth. The Sage speaks with passionate certainty. General Zhao is gruff and direct. The Earthsinger is ancient, hollow, resonant. Mandarin-influenced names: "Nüwa" (noo-WAH), "Chen" (CHEN), "Mei-Li" (may-LEE). Disaster sequences need dynamic pacing; bureaucratic scenes need measured delivery.

# 010 - The Shanghai Protocol

## Genre
Techno-Thriller

## One-Line Premise
A Chinese dissident journalist discovers that her government has created a digital twin of every citizen, and the simulations are being used to pre-arrest people for crimes they haven't committed yet.

## Expanded Premise
Li Wei spent three years in a re-education camp for her reporting on corruption. Now she's free but surveilled, unable to publish, surviving as a translator. When her brother is arrested for "predicted subversion," she investigates—and discovers Project Mirror, a secret AI that maintains digital twins of every Chinese citizen. These simulations are used to predict behavior and pre-emptively detain anyone whose twin shows potential for dissent. Li Wei's own twin exists somewhere in that system. If she can find and manipulate it, she can prove the program's fundamental flaw: that people are not their simulations. But every step she takes to find Mirror brings her closer to the arrest her twin has already predicted.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Li Wei
**Age:** 35
**Occupation / Background:** Former investigative journalist, current freelance translator; grew up in Chengdu, educated at Peking University; her reporting on Party corruption made her briefly famous before it made her imprisoned
**Core skill:** Investigative journalism—she can find sources, verify information, and construct narratives from fragments
**Fatal flaw:** Stubbornness that becomes suicidal; she cannot let go of a story, even when pursuing it means certain destruction
**Character arc:** Begins believing that exposing the truth is enough to change things; learns that truth must be strategically deployed, and that sometimes survival is resistance
**Voice and register:** Precise, observational, trained to notice details that reveal character. Her internal monologue is constantly assessing threat levels. Dark humor surfaces unexpectedly.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Minister Chen Xiaoming of the Ministry of State Security, architect of Project Mirror
**Goal:** Chen believes the Party can perfect society by eliminating crime and dissent before it occurs. Project Mirror is his vision of a friction-free future.
**Method:** The digital twins are constructed from surveillance data, social credit histories, genetic information, and behavioral analysis. They run accelerated simulations to predict future actions. Those whose twins show high probability of dissent are detained preemptively.
**Connection to protagonist:** Chen personally approved Li Wei's release from the camp. He believes she's been reformed. Her brother's arrest is a test—if she remains quiet, she's proof the system works. If she investigates, she's proof the twins are accurate.

## Supporting Characters

**Dr. Zhang Feng** — The computer scientist who built Mirror's simulation engine, now riddled with guilt; provides Li Wei with inside information at enormous personal risk
**Song Mei** — Li Wei's childhood friend, now a mid-level Party official; her relationship with Li Wei is complicated by loyalty to the state that employs her
**David Park** — Korean-American tech journalist based in Hong Kong; offers Li Wei a path to publish internationally if she can get evidence out

## Setting

**Primary location:** Beijing, Shanghai, and Hong Kong
**Time period:** Near-future, approximately 2028
**Atmosphere:** A society that has achieved stability through prediction. Cameras on every corner, algorithms in every interaction. Citizens living normally because they've internalized the surveillance—except for those like Li Wei, who remember when it wasn't always this way. Gleaming tower blocks and traditional hutong neighborhoods, all equally monitored.
**Key locations:** Li Wei's cramped Beijing apartment, swept daily for bugs; the Ministry of State Security headquarters in Beijing; Dr. Zhang's laboratory at Tsinghua University; Hong Kong's press quarter, where foreign journalists still operate; the server complex in Xinjiang that houses Mirror.

## Central Conflict
Li Wei must expose Project Mirror to the world and free her brother—while evading a system specifically designed to predict and prevent exactly what she's trying to do.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Project Mirror is exposed internationally, creating pressure that forces its suspension or modification; her brother is released; the fundamental flaw in predictive policing is demonstrated
**If the protagonist fails:** Her brother is never seen again, she joins him in permanent detention, and Mirror expands globally as China licenses the technology to authoritarian governments worldwide
**Personal stakes:** Li Wei's twin exists in the Mirror system. It knows what she'll do before she does. The only way to beat it is to become someone her simulation wouldn't predict—which might mean becoming someone she doesn't recognize.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Li Wei receives notification that her brother has been detained for "potential state security violations." No specific charges, no trial date. When she visits his apartment, it's empty—erased as if he never existed.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Li Wei's old journalist instincts surface. She contacts sources from her previous life. Most are afraid to speak—but one, Dr. Zhang Feng, agrees to meet. He tells her about Mirror, a system so accurate it has eliminated human judgment from security decisions.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Zhang provides Li Wei with a partial data dump from Mirror's system. She sees her own twin—a simulation of herself that has predicted this meeting, predicted her investigation, and flagged her for imminent detention.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Li Wei contacts David Park in Hong Kong. He's interested but needs hard evidence. To get it, Li Wei must access her brother's Mirror file—which means infiltrating the Ministry of State Security.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Song Mei, conflicted by loyalty and old friendship, provides Li Wei with credentials to access Ministry systems. Li Wei downloads her brother's file—his twin predicted he would share his sister's "unreformed tendencies." The system arrested him for crimes her twin committed.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Song Mei is arrested. The credentials were traced. Li Wei is now the Ministry's top priority. She's forced to flee Beijing for Hong Kong, but Chen has anticipated this—agents are waiting at the border.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Li Wei escapes through smuggling networks to Shenzhen, then Hong Kong. She has the evidence, but Park's newspaper is pressured by Beijing—they won't publish. Her resources are gone, her contacts are compromised, and her twin has predicted every move.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Li Wei realizes she can't outrun her simulation—but she can outgrow it. The twin was built from her past self. If she acts in ways her former self wouldn't, the predictions fail. She stops running and starts preparing a trap.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Li Wei publicly surrenders to Chinese authorities in Hong Kong—but not before distributing her evidence to a hundred international journalists, academics, and human rights organizations. The story breaks globally as she's taken into custody. Inside the Ministry, Chen confronts her. She explains: her twin predicted she would run forever. Surrender was the one thing it couldn't anticipate. The system's flaw is exposed not by evading it, but by choosing something it couldn't imagine.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: International pressure forces a public trial—unprecedented. The evidence is undeniable. Mirror is "temporarily suspended" while the world watches. Li Wei's brother is released. She remains in China, under house arrest but alive, writing again. The system is damaged but not destroyed. The fight continues.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** The danger of predictive surveillance states
**Deep theme:** Human beings are not deterministic—we can choose to become something our past doesn't predict
**Thematic question:** Can a system that knows everything about you predict who you will become?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Mirror File**: Li Wei seeing her digital twin for the first time—every email, every conversation, every moment of hesitation, reconstructed into a simulation that moves like her, speaks like her, and has already predicted her death.
2. **The Border Escape**: A tense chase through Shenzhen's urban labyrinth, using the chaos of a street market to evade facial recognition, ducking through apartments whose residents look away.
3. **The Surrender**: Li Wei walking calmly toward Ministry agents in Hong Kong's central station as journalists broadcast live, her voice steady as she explains why surrender is the only move the system couldn't predict.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Minority Report (2002), Snowden (2016)
**Book comparables:** 1984 by George Orwell, The Ministry for the Future by Kim Stanley Robinson

## Tone and Pacing Notes
A slow burn that accelerates into sprint. The early chapters are observational, building the texture of life under total surveillance. Li Wei's journalism background means careful, layered revelation. The action is punctuated by moments of stillness where she processes what she's learned. The climax is intellectual rather than physical—the trap is a rhetorical one, a demonstration of the system's flaw.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Chinese names require correct pronunciation: Li Wei (LEE WAY), Chen Xiaoming (CHEN SHAO-MING), Zhang Feng (JAHNG FUNG). Beijing locations should be pronounced properly. Li Wei's internal voice should be measured, analytical. Chen's authority should come through in controlled delivery. The surrender sequence should be narrated slowly, emphasizing Li Wei's calm.

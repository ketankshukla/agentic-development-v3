# Storm Surge

## Genre
Survival-Thriller

## One-Line Premise
When a Category 5 hurricane makes a direct hit on a Louisiana nursing home that failed to evacuate, a night-shift nurse must keep 30 elderly patients alive through 12 hours of apocalyptic wind and water.

## Expanded Premise
Isabelle Broussard stayed because someone had to. When Hurricane Celestine shifts course and the evacuation buses don't come, she becomes the only able-bodied adult responsible for 30 residents, most of them bedridden. The facility loses power, then water, then structural integrity. As the storm surge rises and the building begins to fail, Isabelle must move patients through flooded halls, perform emergency medicine with no supplies, and make impossible choices about who to save — all while the hurricane's eye passes directly overhead, bringing a brief false calm before the back wall hits.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Isabelle Broussard
**Age:** 28
**Occupation / Background:** Cajun night-shift LPN, single mother, grew up in the bayou, first in family to work in healthcare
**Core skill:** Practical bayou resourcefulness; ability to improvise medical solutions; deep knowledge of hurricane behavior
**Fatal flaw:** Martyrdom instinct; won't ask for help until it's almost too late
**Character arc:** Believes she must sacrifice everything alone; learns that survival means accepting help and forgiving imperfect choices
**Voice and register:** Cajun warmth with steel underneath. Thinks in practical solutions. Faith tradition underlies her resilience.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Hurricane Celestine — Category 5, 180 mph sustained winds, 20-foot storm surge
**Goal:** The hurricane has no goal; it is force without intention
**Method:** Wind that tears roofs away; water that rises faster than you can move; debris that kills
**Connection to protagonist:** Isabelle's grandmother survived Audrey in 1957; she carries that survival in her blood

## Supporting Characters

**Mister Earl Thibodeaux** — 85-year-old retired shrimper, wheelchair-bound but mentally sharp; becomes Isabelle's tactical advisor
**Nursing Assistant Jerome Williams** — Only other staff member who didn't flee; young, inexperienced, terrified but loyal
**Sister Mary Agnes** — Ancient nun, blind, her prayers become the rhythm of their survival

## Setting

**Primary location:** Bayou Vista Care Home, Louisiana Gulf Coast, 20 miles from open Gulf
**Time period:** Present day, August hurricane season, over approximately 14 hours
**Atmosphere:** The sky turns green, then black. The pressure drops so fast your ears pop. The wind doesn't gust — it pushes constantly, an invisible wall. Rain falls horizontally. Then comes the water — not rain but ocean, rising through the floor, warm and salt-stinking. The eye brings false silence. Then the back wall, worse than the first.
**Key locations:** The main ward, the supply closet, the attic they retreat to, the second-floor chapel, the roof

## Central Conflict
Isabelle must keep 30 bedridden patients alive through a direct hurricane strike with no power, no outside communication, and a building that's falling apart around her.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Maximum lives saved; her choices are vindicated; she becomes the face of hurricane nursing home reform
**If the protagonist fails:** Mass casualty event; criminal charges against administrators but also against her for staying; her daughter grows up without a mother
**Personal stakes:** Seeing her daughter again; proving she can save people like her grandmother couldn't in '57

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Night shift routine; the evacuation order; the buses that never come; the storm arriving
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Power fails; the building begins to shake; Isabelle realizes they're on their own
- Ch 3 — First Contact: First patient death from the chaos; first hard choice about who to prioritize

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Storm surge begins; water entering the ground floor; they start moving patients up
- Ch 5 — The Push: Everyone reaches the second floor; they organize, find supplies, establish a system
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: The eye arrives, bringing false hope; then the back wall hits harder; the roof begins to fail; they must move to the attic
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Trapped in the attic with water still rising; Jerome panics; Isabelle nearly gives up
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Mister Earl's words restore her; they break through to the roof; they will ride out the surge there

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Hours on the roof in wind and rain; helicopter extraction begins; Isabelle insists on going last
- Ch 10 — Resolution: 22 of 30 patients survive; Isabelle's testimony changes evacuation requirements; she reunites with her daughter

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Surviving a direct hurricane strike in a vulnerable facility
**Deep theme:** The impossible ethics of triage and the weight of choosing who lives
**Thematic question:** When you cannot save everyone, who do you save?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Storm Surge Arrival**: Brown water pouring through the windows, Isabelle chest-deep, pushing beds toward the stairs
2. **The Eye Passage**: The sudden silence, the blue sky overhead, the residents weeping with relief, then the wind returning
3. **The Rooftop Rescue**: Clinging to the roof in dawn light, helicopters fighting the wind to reach them

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Impossible (2012), Crawl (2019)
**Book comparables:** Five Days at Memorial by Sheri Fink, Isaac's Storm by Erik Larson

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Intimate epic — huge forces viewed through small human choices. The facility is a pressure cooker. Cajun cultural elements woven throughout without caricature. Each patient has a name and a detail — these are not statistics. The pacing tracks the storm's progression: buildup, strike, eye, back wall, aftermath. Isabelle's faith is tested but not broken.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Cajun French terms and pronunciation should be correct (Broussard, Thibodeaux, bayou, cher). Isabelle's internal monologue has Cajun rhythm and occasional French phrases. Mister Earl speaks with elderly bayou cadence. The storm's sound should be reflected in description intensity — louder passages read faster. Sister Mary Agnes's prayers provide quiet moments. Radio static and wind are constant background implied in prose.

# Red Dust

## Genre
Survival-Thriller

## One-Line Premise
When a prison transport crashes in the heart of the Australian outback, an Indigenous ranger must escort three escaped convicts across 400 kilometers of desert to reach help before the prison gangs hunting them close in.

## Expanded Premise
Jirra Marlo was tracking feral camels when she found the burning prison bus. Two guards dead, three convicts alive — and one of them is serving life for killing her brother. Now Jirra faces an impossible choice: leave them to die in the desert, or guide them 400 kilometers to the nearest town. When it becomes clear that prison gang members are hunting the crash site to kill one of the survivors, Jirra reluctantly becomes their only hope. But the outback doesn't forgive mistakes, and Jirra's duty to save lives conflicts with her desire for justice against the man who destroyed her family.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Jirra Marlo
**Age:** 34
**Occupation / Background:** Indigenous Australian park ranger, Pitjantjatjara woman, raised traditional but educated in Adelaide, returned to country
**Core skill:** Traditional desert survival knowledge combined with modern ranger training; can read country that kills outsiders
**Fatal flaw:** Unresolved rage about her brother's death; desire for vengeance that conflicts with her values
**Character arc:** Believes the killer deserves to die in the desert; learns that letting someone die makes you a killer too
**Voice and register:** Laconic, dry outback humor, Indigenous English patterns. Underneath, spiritual connection to country. Speaks more when teaching survival.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The outback — and Trevor "Trev" McKenna, convicted gang leader whose rivals want him dead before he can testify
**Goal:** The desert has no goal — it kills equally; the gang wants Trev silenced
**Method:** Heat, thirst, distance, disorientation; the gang uses satellite phones and helicopter
**Connection to protagonist:** Trev is one of the three convicts; he was framed, but that doesn't change that the man who actually killed Jirra's brother is also among the survivors

## Supporting Characters

**Danny Cole** — Young first-time offender, terrified, becomes Jirra's responsibility and redemption project
**Raymond Cross** — The actual killer of Jirra's brother, serving time for lesser crimes, his secret unknown to others
**Marcus Webb** — Corrupt corrections officer, the inside man who arranged the gang ambush, survives the crash

## Setting

**Primary location:** Central Australian outback, Simpson Desert region
**Time period:** Present day, height of summer (December-January), over approximately one week
**Atmosphere:** Red earth meeting blue sky at a horizon that never gets closer. Heat that shimmers and distorts. Flies that swarm any moisture. Spinifex and mulga as far as the eye can see. Sacred sites that demand respect. Waterholes that are dry or poisoned. Animals that kill — snakes, spiders, the sun itself. The stars at night so bright they hurt. The silence so complete you hear your blood.
**Key locations:** The crash site, the dry riverbed, the sacred waterhole, the abandoned station, the highway

## Central Conflict
Jirra must guide three men across 400 kilometers of killing desert while one of them is her brother's killer, another is being hunted, and the third is not what he seems.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Three lives saved — including her brother's killer; her testimony exposes the gang and corrupt officer; she maintains her integrity
**If the protagonist fails:** All four die in the desert; the gang cleans up their witness; her country claims her the way it has claimed so many
**Personal stakes:** Becoming a killer by inaction — or proving that her values are stronger than her pain

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Jirra's ranger work; finding the crash; the immediate choice
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Learning who the convicts are; Trev reveals the gang threat; Jirra agrees to guide them
- Ch 3 — First Contact: First day in the desert; first near-death from dehydration; Jirra's skills become essential

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Gang helicopter spotted; they go to ground; Raymond's identity begins to emerge
- Ch 5 — The Push: They reach the waterhole; drink, rest, recover; Jirra and Danny bond
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Marcus sabotages supplies; Raymond is revealed; Jirra must decide whether to leave him for the desert
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Jirra leads Raymond away from the group, intending to abandon him; faces her rage
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: A conversation with Raymond reveals complexity; Jirra cannot let him die; returns with him

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Final push to the highway; gang intercepts them; Marcus's betrayal complete; Jirra uses the country itself
- Ch 10 — Resolution: They reach the highway; gang defeated by environment and Jirra's tactics; Raymond faces trial; Jirra returns to country, at peace

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Crossing the Australian outback with people you hate
**Deep theme:** The difference between justice and vengeance, and finding a third way
**Thematic question:** What do we owe our enemies when letting them die is indistinguishable from killing them?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Crash Site**: The burning bus in the red desert, Jirra approaching through smoke, discovering what she's found
2. **The Waterhole**: The ancient sacred site, drinking water that has sustained her people for millennia, the ancestors present
3. **The Desert Fight**: Final confrontation with the gang, Jirra using knowledge of country — snakes, terrain, heat — to defeat armed men

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Walkabout (1971), Sweet Country (2017)
**Book comparables:** The Dry by Jane Harper, Into the Outback by Robyn Davidson

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Red, hot, relentless. The outback should feel like a character — ancient, indifferent, beautiful. Indigenous spirituality is present and treated with respect. The pace mirrors the desert — long stretches of endurance punctuated by crisis. Jirra's internal struggle with Raymond is the emotional engine. The gang threat provides human stakes against environmental ones. The ending should feel earned, not easy.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Indigenous Australian terms should be pronounced correctly (Jirra, Pitjantjatjara, country). The Australian outback has specific vocabulary (mulga, spinifex, billabong). Jirra speaks with Indigenous Australian English patterns — different from standard Australian. The silence of the desert should be reflected in pacing. Sacred sites are described with reverence. Heat should be audible in shortened sentences and breathless description.

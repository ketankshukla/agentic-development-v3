# The Killing Ground

## Genre
Survival-Thriller

## One-Line Premise
When a bush plane crashes in the Brooks Range, a trauma therapist must lead four PTSD survivors through 100 miles of grizzly country while one of them is a murderer who engineered the crash.

## Expanded Premise
Dr. Sarah Running Bear takes veterans into the Alaskan wilderness for therapeutic retreats. When their bush plane crashes deep in the Brooks Range, killing the pilot, Sarah must get her four patients to safety — but one of them sabotaged the plane and is now hunting the others. In a landscape where grizzly bears, hypothermia, and starvation are constant threats, Sarah must identify the killer while keeping everyone alive. The wilderness doesn't care who survives. But the murderer has very specific plans.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Dr. Sarah Running Bear
**Age:** 44
**Occupation / Background:** Lakota trauma therapist specializing in veteran treatment, former Army psychologist, wilderness therapy pioneer
**Core skill:** Ability to read people's psychological states; wilderness survival knowledge; de-escalation expertise
**Fatal flaw:** Over-identification with patients; struggles to see them as potential threats
**Character arc:** Believes her patients are all victims; learns to accept that damaged people can still be dangerous
**Voice and register:** Calm, measured, therapeutically trained. Lakota spirituality underlies her worldview. Iron core beneath gentle exterior.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Corporal Marcus Thorn, decorated veteran, apparently the most recovered of the group — actually a psychopath who used PTSD treatment to access victims
**Goal:** Kill the other survivors one by one, enjoying the hunt, then disappear into Alaska
**Method:** Manipulation of group dynamics; staged "accidents" that exploit the wilderness; psychological warfare
**Connection to protagonist:** Sarah diagnosed Marcus as recovered; she wrote the evaluation that got him into this retreat; she feels responsible

## Supporting Characters

**Specialist Rita Navarro** — Mexican-American veteran, survivor of an IED attack, suspicious of Marcus from the start
**Sergeant David Okonkwo** — Nigerian-American veteran, natural leader, becomes Sarah's ally in survival efforts
**Private Tommy Hale** — Youngest survivor, fragile, becomes both liability and moral test

## Setting

**Primary location:** Brooks Range, Alaska — 200 miles north of the Arctic Circle
**Time period:** Present day, late August (edge of winter), over approximately 10 days
**Atmosphere:** Mountains that have never been named. Rivers that appear on no map. Tundra in autumn colors — red, gold, orange — with winter waiting just over the horizon. Grizzlies fatten for hibernation. The light is strange — long twilights, brief darkness. The scale is inhuman; you can walk for days and change nothing.
**Key locations:** The crash site, the river crossing, the abandoned mining camp, the caribou migration corridor, the village of Anaktuvuk Pass

## Central Conflict
Sarah must navigate five people across 100 miles of wilderness while one of them systematically kills the others, and she cannot be certain who to trust.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Survivors reach safety; Marcus is exposed; Sarah's method is validated despite this failure
**If the protagonist fails:** Marcus kills everyone; the wilderness claims her body; her life's work is discredited
**Personal stakes:** Her ability to judge character — the foundation of her career and identity — is on trial

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: The therapeutic flight; the crash; the pilot's death; the survivors assess their situation
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Supplies are damaged; nearest help is 100 miles; they must walk out; group dynamics begin to fracture
- Ch 3 — First Contact: First death — Tommy's "accident" — Sarah begins to suspect it wasn't an accident

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Sarah privately shares suspicions with David; they watch the others; Rita confronts Sarah about her doubts
- Ch 5 — The Push: They reach the abandoned mining camp; find supplies; briefly hope increases
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: David is killed during the night; Marcus frames Rita; Sarah doesn't know who to believe
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Alone with Rita and Marcus; grizzly encounter forces desperate flight; Sarah's certainty collapses
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Rita proves her innocence; Sarah and Rita plan to confront Marcus together

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Marcus drops his mask; final pursuit through the tundra; Sarah uses her psychological knowledge to outmaneuver him
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Sarah and Rita reach Anaktuvuk Pass; Sarah returns to work with renewed clarity about her limitations

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Survival in the Alaskan wilderness with a killer among you
**Deep theme:** The limits of understanding others, and the danger of professional blindness
**Thematic question:** How well can we ever know another person's mind?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Crash**: The plane tumbling through mountain air, impact scattering debris across the tundra
2. **The Grizzly**: A close encounter with a grizzly and cubs, the group frozen, trying to retreat without triggering attack
3. **The Final Hunt**: Marcus pursuing Sarah and Rita across the open tundra, using his military training, Sarah using the land

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Grey (2011), Wind River (2017)
**Book comparables:** Into the Wild by Jon Krakauer, A Stranger in the House by Shari Lapena

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Psychological tension layered over physical survival. The wilderness is harshly beautiful but not malevolent — it's simply indifferent. Trust erodes slowly across the first half, then collapses. Sarah's therapeutic observations become survival tools. The pacing allows for character study between crises. Marcus's reveal is gradual — readers may suspect before Sarah does.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Lakota terms should be pronounced correctly when Sarah uses them. Marcus speaks with perfect calm until his mask drops. Rita's Mexican-American accent is subtle; David's includes Nigerian cadences. The silence of the tundra should be reflected in pacing. Grizzly scenes need visceral weight. Sarah's internal monologue shifts from therapeutic to survival as the book progresses.

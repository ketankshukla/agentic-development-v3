# The Jade General

## Genre
Military Fantasy

## One-Line Premise
A dragon rider grounded by her dragon's death must lead a peasant uprising with nothing but her reputation and her grief.

## Expanded Premise
General Wen Zhixin was the Jade Dragon, terror of the empire's enemies, bonded to the great dragon Tianming for forty years. When Tianming is killed by an assassination meant for the Emperor, Zhixin's purpose dies with him. Exiled to a backwater province to mourn in obscurity, she finds the peasants on the edge of starvation, crushed by a corrupt governor who is selling grain to foreign powers. When the harvest fails completely and the governor demands taxes in corpses, Zhixin faces a choice: die forgotten as the empire rots, or do something she never imagined—fight for people instead of power. But she has no dragon, no army, and no authority. Only the name that once made empires tremble.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Wen Zhixin
**Age:** 62
**Occupation / Background:** Retired General of the Dragon Corps, forty years of service, the most decorated soldier in imperial history
**Core skill:** Tactical genius that can turn farmers with pitchforks into an effective fighting force—she understands every aspect of warfare
**Fatal flaw:** She has only ever fought for abstract loyalty—the throne, the empire—and doesn't know how to fight for people she actually sees
**Character arc:** Believes her life ended with Tianming's death; discovers she never truly lived because she never chose her own cause; becomes a champion of the people rather than the crown
**Voice and register:** Formal and archaic, the speech patterns of imperial courts. As she bonds with the peasants, colloquialisms creep in—she's learning a new language.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Governor Shen Yao, administrator of Heifang Province, nephew of the Emperor's chief concubine
**Goal:** To extract maximum wealth before ascending to a better posting, regardless of how many peasants die
**Method:** Corrupt magistrates, hired mercenaries, and the implicit threat of imperial displeasure if anyone interferes
**Connection to protagonist:** Shen openly mocked Zhixin at court after Tianming's death, calling her "the broken toy"—he never expected her to matter again

## Supporting Characters

**Li Feng** — A young farmer's daughter with the rare gift of dragon-speech, potentially bonded to an egg hidden in the mountains
**Master Bo** — A wandering monk who leads the peasant resistance, initially hostile to imperial involvement
**Captain Ru Yan** — Commander of the governor's mercenaries, a former soldier who served under Zhixin and must choose sides

## Setting

**Primary location:** Heifang Province, a remote mountainous region of the Celestial Empire
**Time period:** Classical Chinese-inspired era, with dragon riders forming a military elite
**Atmosphere:** Terraced rice paddies climb misty mountains. Bamboo forests whisper. Smoke rises from villages burning under the governor's taxes. In hidden valleys, dragon eggs wait for riders who may never come.
**Key locations:** The provincial capital of Heifang City; the Mountain of Jade where Tianming's bones rest; the Hidden Temple where dragons nest; the Valley of Ghosts where the final battle occurs; the Dragon Gate pass

## Central Conflict
Zhixin must transform starving farmers into an army capable of defeating professional mercenaries and seizing the provincial capital—while the empire itself might send forces to restore "order."

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The governor is overthrown, the province is freed, and a new model of governance emerges that could spread across the empire
**If the protagonist fails:** The peasants are massacred, the province is depopulated, and the empire slides further into decay without even noticing
**Personal stakes:** Zhixin either finds meaning after Tianming's death, or confirms that she died with him and is just waiting for her body to catch up

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Zhixin arrives in Heifang broken and purposeless; she witnesses the governor's soldiers burning a village for "tax evasion"
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Master Bo's resistance captures her, planning to ransom "the Jade General"; Li Feng recognizes her legend and begs her to help
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Zhixin organizes the villagers to ambush a tax collection—it works, but the governor responds by burning three more villages

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: The governor hires mercenaries from abroad; Zhixin realizes she must build a real army; Li Feng reveals the hidden dragon eggs
- Ch 5 — The Push: Zhixin's peasant army wins a major battle using terrain and deception; recruits flood in; Captain Ru Yan secretly meets with her
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: The governor's mercenaries use scorched earth tactics, starving the rebellion; the mountain temple is burned; Li Feng bonds with a dragon but it's only a hatchling
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Master Bo is captured and publicly executed; the rebellion fragments; Zhixin faces Tianming's grave, questioning everything
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Li Feng's bond with the young dragon sparks something in Zhixin—she realizes her purpose wasn't the dragon but what she did with it

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The Battle of Ghosts Valley—peasant army against mercenaries, Zhixin's tactics against superior arms, Li Feng's untrained dragon providing desperate support
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The governor flees; the province is free; Zhixin refuses offers to become governor, instead training Li Feng as the next dragon rider—her legacy lives on

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Revolution and the power of the common people
**Deep theme:** Loyalty without love is empty—true service requires choosing whom to serve
**Thematic question:** When does a lifetime of service to the wrong cause become wasted, and can it be redeemed?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Dragon's Funeral**: Zhixin conducts the ritual for Tianming alone, fire consuming forty years of partnership
2. **The Harvest Battle**: Peasants use farming tools and harvest techniques to defeat armored mercenaries in the rice terraces
3. **The Hatchling's First Flight**: Li Feng's young dragon joins the battle untrained, chaotic and beautiful, while Zhixin commands from the ground

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Seven Samurai (1954), Mulan (1998)
**Book comparables:** Daughter of the Moon Goddess by Sue Lynn Tan, She Who Became the Sun by Shelley Parker-Chan

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Builds from elegiac slowness to revolutionary momentum. The first third is quiet, focused on grief and the discovery of injustice. The middle accelerates with military training and growing hope. The final act is sustained warfare with high emotional stakes. The tone balances tragedy with triumph—Zhixin's loss gives weight to her new purpose.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Zhixin's voice should age through the story—formal and hollow at the start, warming with purpose by the end. Chinese-inspired names: Zhixin (zhi-SHEEN), Tianming (tyen-MING), Heifang (hay-FAHNG). Dragon sounds should be described majestically. Peasant dialogue should be simple and direct, contrasting with Zhixin's courtly speech.

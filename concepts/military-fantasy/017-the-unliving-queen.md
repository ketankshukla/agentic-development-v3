# The Unliving Queen

## Genre
Military Fantasy

## One-Line Premise
A soldier resurrected as undead must lead an army of the risen against the living kingdom that murdered them—while fighting to keep her humanity.

## Expanded Premise
Marta Kowalczyk died defending the village of Strazh against the Valdorian Empire. She woke three days later, along with every soldier who fell beside her, bound to the will of the Necromancer King who raised them. But something went wrong—Marta kept her mind, her will, her humanity. Now she commands an army of undead soldiers who follow her because she remembers what they've forgotten: who they were, why they fought, what they loved. The Necromancer King wants her to lead his conquests. The living kingdom wants her destroyed. Marta just wants to protect the villages that still remember their names—but to do that, she may have to become the monster both sides fear.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Marta Kowalczyk
**Age:** 29 at death (current state: deceased two years)
**Occupation / Background:** Former sergeant of the village militia, a smith's daughter who joined the guard after her father was killed by raiders
**Core skill:** Indomitable will—something in her soul refused to break when the necromancy should have claimed her mind
**Fatal flaw:** She clings so tightly to her humanity that she refuses to use her full power, potentially dooming those she protects
**Character arc:** Believes humanity means refusing to embrace what she's become; learns that humanity is about choice, not condition; becomes something new—neither living nor undead, but herself
**Voice and register:** Warm and alive, refusing the hollow tones of her condition. She jokes when she should be menacing, loves when she should be cold.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The Necromancer King Zivoslav, who raised the army and believes Marta is his greatest creation
**Goal:** To conquer the living lands using armies that never tire, never break, and cost nothing to maintain
**Method:** Mass resurrection of fallen soldiers, binding their wills to his, using Marta as the general who makes them effective
**Connection to protagonist:** Zivoslav doesn't hate Marta—he's fascinated by her anomaly; he believes she will eventually accept what she is

## Supporting Characters

**Tomasz the Burned** — Her former lover, now an undead soldier who retains fragments of memory when Marta is near
**Lady Helena Ostrowski** — A living noble whose lands border the undead kingdom, seeking alliance rather than war
**Brother Filip** — A priest who believes Marta's soul can be saved, traveling with her army to minister to the unliving

## Setting

**Primary location:** The borderlands between the living Kingdom of Polania and the Necromancer King's realm of the Risen
**Time period:** Late medieval Eastern European-inspired era, with Slavic folk magic
**Atmosphere:** Snow falls on the unfeeling dead. Villages burn, but some are saved. The line between life and death blurs. Church bells ring, and the undead remember they once answered prayers.
**Key locations:** The village of Strazh where Marta died; the Bone Fortress where Zivoslav rules; the living city of Kravosk; the Church of the Blessed Dead (a shrine to those who rest peacefully); the Valley of the Final Battle

## Central Conflict
Marta must use the power of undeath to protect the living while resisting the Necromancer King's will, proving that monsters can choose to be heroes.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The undead become protectors rather than destroyers, the living and dead find coexistence, and Marta proves choice matters more than condition
**If the protagonist fails:** The undead armies rampage, the living are exterminated or enslaved, and Marta becomes what everyone fears
**Personal stakes:** Marta keeps her soul—literally—or loses herself to become the weapon the Necromancer King wants

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Marta wakes from death, realizes she retains herself, hides her awareness from the Necromancer King
- Ch 2 — The Pull: She gathers other undead who respond to her presence, finds Tomasz, begins secretly protecting villages
- Ch 3 — First Contact: The living army of Polania attacks her forces; she must fight people she would have defended; she wins but is shattered

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Zivoslav notices her independence and is intrigued; he offers partnership; she pretends compliance while building resistance
- Ch 5 — The Push: Marta's undead save a major town from raiders; Lady Helena approaches for alliance; hope emerges
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Zivoslav tests her loyalty by commanding her to destroy a village she saved; her refusal is revealed; he binds her will
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Under his control, she leads atrocities; Tomasz breaks through to her; she tears free but loses most of her army
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Brother Filip performs a ritual that anchors her to her humanity permanently; she can never be controlled again

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The coalition of undead and living storms the Bone Fortress; Marta confronts Zivoslav as equals
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The Necromancer King falls; the undead who wish it find true rest; Marta leads those who remain in a new existence

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Undead versus the living
**Deep theme:** Humanity is defined by choice, not by what you are but what you do
**Thematic question:** Can someone monstrous be a hero, and at what point does the monster become the person?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Awakening**: Marta claws from her grave, sees her army of dead standing waiting, realizes she is their only mind
2. **The Village Saved**: Undead soldiers form defensive lines around living villagers, protecting children from raiders, impossible mercy
3. **The Breaking of Control**: Marta shatters Zivoslav's binding in an explosion of will, her army falling then rising with her

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Warm Bodies (2013), The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King (Army of the Dead)
**Book comparables:** Sabriel by Garth Nix, Gideon the Ninth by Tamsyn Muir

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Walks a line between horror and heroism. The pacing begins slowly—Marta discovering what she is—then accelerates through action and politics. The horror should be real but not overwhelming; the humanity should feel earned. Dark humor balances grimness. The ending should feel like a beginning, not a resolution—there's more story beyond this book.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Marta's voice should be warm despite her condition—deliberately alive. Polish-inspired names: Kowalczyk (koh-VAL-chik), Zivoslav (ZIH-voh-slav), Polania (poh-LAH-nya). The undead (other than Marta) should speak haltingly, remembering words slowly. Battle scenes mix visceral action with the eerie silence of soldiers who don't grunt or scream.

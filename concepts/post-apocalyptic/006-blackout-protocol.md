# Blackout Protocol

## Genre
Post-Apocalyptic

## One-Line Premise
A North Korean defector must hijack a nuclear submarine before its dead-man switches launch missiles that will complete humanity's extinction.

## Expanded Premise
When a cascading AI failure triggered automated military responses worldwide, the exchange lasted nineteen minutes. Park Jin-ae, a former North Korean intelligence officer who defected five years ago, survived in a Seoul bunker. Now she's learned that a North Korean submarine carrying twenty nuclear missiles is adrift in the Sea of Japan — its crew dead from radiation, but its automated launch systems counting down because no signal has confirmed regime survival. She has seventy-two hours to reach the submarine, override systems designed specifically to resist her, and prevent the missiles from launching at targets that no longer need destroying — but are still full of survivors.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Park Jin-ae
**Age:** 35
**Occupation / Background:** Former Korean People's Army intelligence analyst, defected via China, became a military consultant for South Korean intelligence before the exchange
**Core skill:** Intimate knowledge of North Korean military protocols, codes, and psychological operations — she helped design some of the failsafe systems she must now defeat
**Fatal flaw:** Survivor's guilt — she escaped while her family remained in the North; every action is haunted by whether she has the right to live
**Character arc:** Believes her survival is a cosmic error she must correct through sacrifice; discovers that survival creates obligation not to die but to prevent further death; chooses to live
**Voice and register:** Controlled and analytical, trained to suppress emotion. Her internal monologue is bitter and self-critical, dark humor as defense mechanism. When she cracks, she speaks to dead family members.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The Unification Protocol — an automated launch system designed to guarantee nuclear retaliation even if all command authority is destroyed; it has no mind to persuade and no off switch
**Goal:** Fulfill its programming: if no valid stand-down code is received within 72 hours of losing contact with command, launch all missiles at pre-programmed targets
**Method:** Locked bulkheads, disabled manual controls, countermeasure systems designed to kill intruders, and authentication requirements that died with specific people
**Connection to protagonist:** Jin-ae briefed intelligence officials on this protocol's existence. They didn't believe her. Now she's the only person who understands exactly how impossible her task is.

## Supporting Characters

**Lieutenant Commander Yuki Hasegawa** — Japanese Maritime Self-Defense Force officer commanding the only ship close enough to reach the submarine; skeptical of defectors but respects competence
**Sergeant Major Cho Hyun-woo** — A South Korean special forces diver and Jin-ae's former colleague; they have romantic history and unfinished business
**Dr. Evelyn Cross** — An American nuclear security expert coordinating via satellite phone; she can advise on the technical systems if Jin-ae can describe them

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Sea of Japan and the interior of a North Korean Sinpo-class ballistic missile submarine
**Time period:** Twelve days after the exchange
**Atmosphere:** The submarine is a tomb — emergency lighting, radiation-contaminated compartments, frozen crew members at their stations. The Sea of Japan is calm, indifferent. Communication with other survivors is sporadic and desperate. The countdown clock provides constant pressure.
**Key locations:** The Japanese destroyer Ashigara (command base), the submarine's conning tower, the missile compartment, the command center with its authentication stations, the reactor room

## Central Conflict
Jin-ae must board a hostile submarine, navigate compartments designed as death traps, defeat an authentication system requiring multiple dead operators, and manually disable a launch sequence — all within 72 hours and while managing lethal radiation exposure.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Twenty missiles remain unfired; cities full of survivors (many still unaware of each other) continue to exist; reconstruction becomes possible
**If the protagonist fails:** Five million additional deaths, remaining agricultural regions destroyed, nuclear winter intensified, human extinction becomes mathematically likely
**Personal stakes:** Jin-ae will take a lethal radiation dose completing this mission; the question is whether she dies having prevented catastrophe or having failed

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Jin-ae emerges from a Seoul bunker into a city of ash; a radio intercept reveals the submarine's automated distress beacon and she recognizes the protocol signature
- Ch 2 — The Pull: She contacts Japanese survivors with the horrible math: the submarine exists, the protocol exists, seventy-two hours exist; they have a destroyer that can reach it
- Ch 3 — First Contact: The boarding team breaches the submarine's sail; the first compartment contains dead crew and active radiation; three team members refuse to continue

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Jin-ae explains the authentication system: three officers with partial codes, all dead, their biometrics still required; they must find the bodies and extract data
- Ch 5 — The Push: Working through contaminated compartments, they locate two officers and extract their credentials; the third is in the flooded missile section
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Cho Hyun-woo takes a lethal dose retrieving the third officer's body; the codes don't work — someone changed them before dying
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Cho dies in Jin-ae's arms; the countdown reaches twelve hours; Dr. Cross calculates that the changed code could be one of 10,000 combinations
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Jin-ae remembers her father, a protocol designer, teaching her the logic of fail-safe systems; she realizes the changed code must follow a pattern — and her father wrote that pattern

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Jin-ae enters the command center alone, radiation exposure critical, and works through her father's logic — the code is a date: the birthday of his granddaughter, a girl he never met
- Ch 10 — Resolution: The missiles stay silent. Jin-ae emerges to the destroyer's deck, knowing she has weeks at most. Hasegawa promises to take her somewhere with a view. She asks for mountains.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Automated systems without human oversight create extinction-level risks
**Deep theme:** The sins of nations become personal; someone must bear the burden of stopping inherited catastrophes
**Thematic question:** If you can only save the world by dying, does survival guilt finally become resolved or compounded?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Breach**: Cutting through the submarine's sail in choppy seas, descending into darkness lit only by emergency strips, finding the first frozen body.
2. **The Flood Dive**: Cho swimming through the missile compartment in full contamination gear, reaching a corpse pinned under debris, extracting biometric data as his suit integrity fails.
3. **The Code**: Jin-ae alone in the command center, radiation burns visible, methodically entering combinations while speaking to her dead father about the logic he taught her.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Hunt for Red October, Crimson Tide
**Book comparables:** On the Beach by Nevil Shute, The Road by Cormac McCarthy

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Technical thriller pacing compressed into apocalyptic stakes. The submarine sections are claustrophobic, procedural, and tense. Jin-ae's internal monologue provides emotional texture against the operational focus. The relationship with Cho develops through shared professional competence and quiet moments between crises. The ending is sacrificial but not despairing — Jin-ae dies having chosen meaning.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Korean names require attention: Jin-ae (jin-AY), Park (standard), Cho Hyun-woo (cho HYUN-woo). Japanese names: Hasegawa (hah-seh-GAH-wah), Ashigara (ah-shee-GAH-rah). Technical terminology should be confident. Jin-ae's internal monologue is quieter than her operational voice. The final sequence should be delivered slowly, with weight. Brief Korean phrases may appear — these should be spoken without artificial emphasis.

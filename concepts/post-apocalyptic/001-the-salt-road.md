# The Salt Road

## Genre
Post-Apocalyptic

## One-Line Premise
A Moroccan salt trader must cross the irradiated Sahara to deliver medicine to her dying daughter before the last caravan route closes forever.

## Expanded Premise
Fifteen years after a limited nuclear exchange collapsed global infrastructure, Fatima Benjelloun runs one of the last trading caravans across the Sahara. When her daughter contracts a mutated strain of radiation sickness, the only cure exists in the fortified city-state of Lagos. Fatima must navigate tribal wars, radioactive dead zones, and a mercenary company hunting her caravan's cargo — all while racing a disease that gives her daughter thirty days to live. The journey will force her to choose between the caravan families who depend on her and the child she would burn the world to save.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Fatima Benjelloun
**Age:** 44
**Occupation / Background:** Caravan master, former logistics coordinator for the Moroccan military before the Exchange
**Core skill:** Route memory and negotiation — she knows every water source, shelter, and tribal territory across 3,000 kilometers of desert, and speaks six languages fluently
**Fatal flaw:** Maternal obsession — she will sacrifice others to save her daughter without hesitation
**Character arc:** Believes survival requires absolute self-reliance; discovers that the caravan families are not obligations but her true family; loses her daughter but gains a people
**Voice and register:** Speaks in short, practical sentences honed by years of command. Thinks in distances, water ratios, and threat assessments. Her internal voice is warmer than her exterior suggests.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Colonel Marcus Webb, commander of the Saharan Reclamation Company
**Goal:** Capture Fatima's caravan and seize her route maps — the last comprehensive navigation data for trans-Saharan trade, worth a fortune to whoever controls post-collapse Africa
**Method:** Deploys drone scouts and fast assault vehicles to track the caravan, sets ambushes at known water sources, and recruits local militias with promises of territorial rights
**Connection to protagonist:** Webb was Fatima's commanding officer before the Exchange. He taught her everything about logistics — and she knows his tactical patterns as well as he knows hers.

## Supporting Characters

**Yusuf Benjelloun** — Fatima's 17-year-old son, the caravan's lead scout; torn between loyalty to his mother and growing awareness that her choices endanger everyone
**Dr. Amara Okonkwo** — Nigerian physician traveling with the caravan; carries the knowledge to synthesize the medicine if they can reach her laboratory in Lagos
**Hassan the Elder** — 70-year-old caravan patriarch who remembers the pre-Exchange world; serves as moral compass and keeper of oral histories

## Setting

**Primary location:** The trans-Saharan trade corridor from Marrakech to Lagos
**Time period:** 2042, fifteen years after the Exchange
**Atmosphere:** Endless ochre dunes broken by the rusted skeletons of crashed aircraft and abandoned vehicles. Nights are cold and star-blazed, days blinding white. The wind carries fine radioactive dust that stings exposed skin. Trade posts are mud-walled fortresses where water is currency and strangers are searched for weapons.
**Key locations:** The Marrakech Souk (walled trading hub), the Tanezrouft Dead Zone (irradiated wasteland), the Ahaggar Mountain refuge, the Niger River crossing, Lagos Wall

## Central Conflict
Fatima must transport her caravan 3,000 kilometers across hostile territory while evading a superior military force, all within a thirty-day window before her daughter's disease becomes untreatable — and she must do this without sacrificing the forty-seven people who depend on her for survival.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The Lagos route remains open, trans-Saharan trade survives, her daughter lives, and Webb's mercenary empire collapses
**If the protagonist fails:** Her daughter dies, the caravan families are killed or enslaved, and Webb controls all African trade for a generation
**Personal stakes:** Fatima's entire identity is built on protecting her family — losing Samira would destroy her reason for surviving

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Fatima negotiates a trade deal in Marrakech when Dr. Okonkwo diagnoses her daughter Samira with radiation sickness; the only cure is in Lagos
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Fatima announces an emergency crossing despite the wrong season; half the caravan refuses to go, but enough follow
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Webb's drones locate the caravan at the first water stop; a nighttime raid kills three guards and wounds Yusuf

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Fatima learns Webb has placed bounties at every oasis; there is no safe route, only faster or slower death
- Ch 5 — The Push: The caravan crosses the Tanezrouft Dead Zone in record time, using Fatima's secret route; they gain a week on Webb
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Samira's condition worsens; Fatima must choose between a faster route through hostile Tuareg territory or the safe path that will arrive too late
- Ch 7 — The Descent: The Tuareg demand half the caravan's goods as passage toll; Fatima refuses, and the resulting battle leaves her wounded and Hassan dead
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Delirious from her wound, Fatima hallucinates a conversation with Hassan who reminds her why caravan masters exist — to bring everyone home, not just blood family

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: At the Niger River crossing, Webb springs his final trap; Fatima leads a diversionary group while Yusuf and Dr. Okonkwo rush Samira to Lagos
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Samira survives; Fatima kills Webb in single combat but loses her right hand; she returns to find Yusuf has become the new caravan master, and she finally rests

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Survival in extreme environments requires adaptation and sacrifice
**Deep theme:** The families we choose are as sacred as the families we're born into
**Thematic question:** How much of ourselves are we allowed to sacrifice for our children before we become monsters?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Tanezrouft Crossing**: The caravan moves by night across a landscape of glass-fused sand and skeletal vehicles, radiation counters clicking, while a dust storm masks them from drone surveillance.
2. **The Ahaggar Ambush**: A running battle through mountain passes at dawn, camel-mounted warriors against armored vehicles, ending with an avalanche that buries pursuers and route alike.
3. **The Niger River Stand**: A siege on the ruins of a colonial-era bridge, Fatima holding the northern approach with six fighters while the wounded are ferried across on makeshift rafts.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Mad Max: Fury Road, The Way Back
**Book comparables:** The Road by Cormac McCarthy, Station Eleven by Emily St. John Mandel

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Relentless forward momentum. Every chapter ends with a new complication or deadline. The prose is spare and sensory, focused on physical experience — heat, thirst, pain, exhaustion. Quiet moments exist only in brief camp scenes that deepen character before the next crisis. The emotional register builds from professional detachment to raw maternal desperation.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Arabic and Berber names should be phonetically clear: Fatima (fah-TEE-mah), Benjelloun (ben-jeh-LOON), Tanezrouft (tan-ez-ROOFT). Webb speaks with clipped military precision. Dr. Okonkwo has a warm Nigerian cadence. Caravan scenes benefit from slightly slower pacing to convey the grinding rhythm of desert travel. Action sequences should accelerate.

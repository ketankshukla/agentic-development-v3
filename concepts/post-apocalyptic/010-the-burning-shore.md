# The Burning Shore

## Genre
Post-Apocalyptic

## One-Line Premise
A Maori fisherman must unite warring coastal tribes to defend New Zealand from a Australian refugee fleet desperate enough to kill for landfall.

## Expanded Premise
When the Pacific Firestorms consumed Australia, millions fled across the Tasman Sea in anything that floated. Hemi Parata is a commercial fisherman on the South Island's West Coast when the first boats arrive — not refugees seeking shelter but an organized armada claiming settlement rights by force. New Zealand's government collapsed months ago; now tribal councils and community militias are the only law. Hemi, who has spent his life avoiding politics and his own Maori heritage, must convince rival communities to stop their petty feuds and face a threat that will drown them all — while deciding whether the desperate Australians deserve mercy or resistance.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Hemi Parata
**Age:** 47
**Occupation / Background:** Third-generation commercial fisherman, estranged from his iwi (tribe) after a family dispute decades ago; knows every bay, current, and fishing ground on the West Coast
**Core skill:** Seamanship and coastal knowledge — he can navigate by star, read weather from cloud patterns, and predict what any captain will do based on their vessel's capabilities
**Fatal flaw:** Isolation — he's spent twenty years avoiding community and now must build one from scratch
**Character arc:** Believes he can stay neutral and let others fight; discovers that neutrality is complicity; reclaims his identity as tangata whenua (person of the land) by defending it
**Voice and register:** Working-class Kiwi vernacular, sparse but not unfriendly. He speaks when he has something to say and stays silent otherwise. His internal voice is more philosophical than his speech suggests.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Admiral Catherine Marsh, former Royal Australian Navy commander, now leader of the Southern Armada
**Goal:** Secure beachheads on the South Island for 60,000 Australian survivors; she believes New Zealand's sparse population forfeits any claim to unused land
**Method:** Naval blockade, controlled landings, and overwhelming numbers; she's trying to manage a humanitarian crisis while her people grow desperate
**Connection to protagonist:** Marsh rescued Hemi's boat during a storm three years before the Firestorms; they shared a drink in Greymouth; she's not a monster, just a leader of monsters

## Supporting Characters

**Anahera Kahurangi** — A young woman who claims to speak for Ngai Tahu iwi; she's building alliances Hemi abandoned decades ago and needs his knowledge of the coast
**James "Bluey" Thornton** — An Australian refugee who washed ashore alone and now serves on Hemi's boat; his presence complicates Hemi's growing rage toward the armada
**Matua Wiremu** — Hemi's estranged uncle, elder of the Parata hapu (subtribe); he holds the key to coastal war strategies that haven't been used in 150 years

## Setting

**Primary location:** The West Coast of New Zealand's South Island, from Greymouth to Fiordland
**Time period:** Eighteen months after the Pacific Firestorms
**Atmosphere:** A world of grey-green sea, temperate rainforest, and constant rain. The coast is beautiful but brutal — few harbors, treacherous rocks, unpredictable weather. Settlements cling to river mouths and small bays. The Australians anchor offshore in deteriorating ships while New Zealanders watch from headlands.
**Key locations:** Greymouth harbor (Hemi's home), the Punakaiki coastal batteries, the Haast Pass supply route, the Milford Sound ambush point, the final defense at Stewart Island

## Central Conflict
Hemi must unify fractured coastal communities into a defensive force capable of stopping a numerically superior armada, while wrestling with the moral weight of fighting refugees who are dying of starvation on their ships.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** New Zealand's communities survive intact; the armada is forced to negotiate rather than conquer; a humanitarian solution becomes possible
**If the protagonist fails:** The South Island is overrun; indigenous culture is subsumed; sixty thousand desperate refugees become an occupying army
**Personal stakes:** Hemi's rejection of his heritage was a personal choice; now his people need what he abandoned, and he must decide if he's still one of them

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Hemi pulls crayfish pots off Greymouth when he spots the first armada ships on the horizon; by nightfall, there are hundreds
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Anahera arrives seeking a pilot to scout the armada; Hemi refuses until he sees children's faces on the ships and realizes this ends only one way
- Ch 3 — First Contact: A delegation meets with Marsh; she delivers an ultimatum — prepare for settlement or face armed landing; Hemi recognizes her and sees the desperation she's hiding

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: The first landing attempt is repelled at Hokitika, but the armada has dozens of ships; they can try every bay simultaneously
- Ch 5 — The Push: Hemi travels inland to unite the coastal communities; using his uncle's old war strategies, they create a mobile defense network
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: The armada captures Greymouth in a night assault; Hemi's boat is destroyed; Bluey is captured and presumably executed
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Hiding in the bush with Anahera, watching his home burn, Hemi believes the war is already lost
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Matua Wiremu finds them and reveals that Hemi's father once faced a similar choice — fight and die or retreat and preserve; Hemi chooses his father's path

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: A final stand at Milford Sound, using the fiords to negate the armada's numbers; Marsh's flagship is driven onto rocks, and she signals surrender
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Peace negotiations grant the Australians settlement in specific regions under New Zealand authority; Hemi becomes a treaty negotiator, finding a role he never expected

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Small nations can resist larger forces through knowledge of terrain
**Deep theme:** Identity isn't inherited, it's reclaimed through action; you become your ancestors by defending what they loved
**Thematic question:** When does self-defense become atrocity — and does desperation excuse invasion?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Armada Arrival**: Hundreds of vessels emerging from sea mist, from pleasure yachts to cargo ships, a nation afloat and dying.
2. **The Fiordland Approach**: Canoes and fishing boats moving through misty fiords at dawn, positioning for an ambush on ships that have never seen water this narrow.
3. **The Greymouth Burning**: Hemi watching from the hills as the town he's called home for twenty years goes up in flames, warships in the harbor, refugees pouring onto the wharves.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Patriot, Tomorrow When the War Began
**Book comparables:** Tomorrow When the War Began by John Marsden, The Power of the Dog by Don Winslow

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Sea rhythms — the pacing matches the water, long swells of tension broken by sudden storms of action. The prose is sensory, focused on weather, water, and coastal light. Maori concepts are woven naturally without excessive explanation. The moral complexity doesn't resolve cleanly; even victory tastes of ash. The relationship with Marsh prevents simple heroism.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Maori names and terms need attention: Hemi (HEH-mee), Parata (pah-RAH-tah), Anahera (ah-nah-HEH-rah), Matua (mah-TOO-ah), Wiremu (wee-REH-moo), iwi (EE-wee), hapu (HAH-poo), tangata whenua (TAHNG-ah-tah FEH-noo-ah), Ngai Tahu (NYE TAH-hoo). New Zealand place names: Punakaiki (poo-nah-KYE-kee), Hokitika (hoh-kih-TEE-kah). Kiwi dialogue is casual and understated. Australian refugees speak broader Australian English. The sea itself should be audible in the prose — the rhythm of waves underlying everything.

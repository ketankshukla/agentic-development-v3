# The Last Lighthouse

## Genre
Post-Apocalyptic

## One-Line Premise
A lighthouse keeper must guide the final refugee fleet to safety while a tsunami approaches and her own daughter lies dying on the rocks below.

## Expanded Premise
The Cascadia Subduction Zone finally ruptured, and the Pacific Northwest drowned. Ida Sorensen has kept the Cape Disappointment lighthouse burning for seven years, guiding survivor boats through debris-choked waters. Now the largest refugee fleet ever assembled — 2,000 people on 47 vessels — is approaching the Columbia River mouth in a storm, trusting her light to bring them home. But a second mega-thrust earthquake has triggered a tsunami, her daughter has fallen onto the rocks trying to secure the dock, and Ida has forty-five minutes to choose: climb down to save her daughter or stay at the light to save two thousand strangers.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Ida Sorensen
**Age:** 54
**Occupation / Background:** Third-generation lighthouse keeper, former marine biologist, now the most important navigation aid on the Pacific Coast
**Core skill:** Storm reading and coastal navigation — she can predict wave patterns, identify safe channels through debris fields, and communicate course corrections through light signals
**Fatal flaw:** Duty obsession — she believes her responsibility to the light supersedes everything, including personal connections
**Character arc:** Believes her purpose is the lighthouse and nothing else; discovers that purpose without connection is hollow; chooses imperfectly and lives with the result
**Voice and register:** Sparse and weathered, shaped by years of solitude and storm. She speaks to the light, to the sea, to the ghosts of ships she's saved and lost. Her internal voice is lonelier than she admits.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The Cascadia Fault and the tsunami it has unleashed
**Goal:** None — geology has no malice; the wave is simply physics
**Method:** A 100-foot wall of water traveling at 500 miles per hour, set to arrive in forty-five minutes
**Connection to protagonist:** Ida survived the first quake by being in the lighthouse; her husband did not; she has spent seven years believing the lighthouse saved her for a reason

## Supporting Characters

**Maren Sorensen** — Ida's 24-year-old daughter, who has helped maintain the lighthouse since the collapse; she lies on the rocks below with a broken leg and rising water
**Captain Jonas Reykdal** — Commander of the refugee fleet, guiding 47 vessels based on Ida's signals; he trusts her completely but doesn't know about the tsunami
**The Voice** — A ham radio operator somewhere inland who provides weather data and emotional support; Ida has never met them in person

## Setting

**Primary location:** Cape Disappointment Lighthouse, Washington State
**Time period:** Seven years after the Cascadia Event
**Atmosphere:** Permanent storm season. The Pacific Northwest is a drowned landscape of islands that were hilltops, channels that were valleys. The lighthouse stands on a headland that has become a critical navigation point. The sea is grey-green, churning, filled with debris from a civilization that went underwater. The sky is rarely clear.
**Key locations:** The lighthouse tower, the keeper's cottage, the dock and rocks below, the approach channel where the fleet will pass, the radio room

## Central Conflict
Ida must guide 47 vessels carrying 2,000 people through a debris-choked channel in a storm, with a tsunami approaching, while her daughter bleeds out on the rocks below — and she cannot do all three things at once.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds at the light:** Two thousand refugees reach safe harbor; the largest survival community on the coast is established
**If the protagonist saves her daughter:** Maren lives, but without guidance the fleet scatters into debris and rocks; hundreds drown
**If the protagonist fails entirely:** Everyone dies — the fleet, Maren, and Ida herself when the tsunami hits
**Personal stakes:** Ida has already lost her husband to the sea. Losing Maren would complete her isolation. But abandoning the light would make every death her fault.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Ida spots the fleet on the horizon as the earthquake hits; she feels the lighthouse sway; The Voice reports a tsunami warning
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Maren runs to secure the dock; a wave throws her onto the rocks; Ida sees her fall from the tower and hears her scream
- Ch 3 — First Contact: The fleet begins its approach; Captain Reykdal signals for guidance; Ida must respond or they'll take the wrong channel

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Ida signals the fleet while tracking the tsunami timeline; thirty-five minutes remaining; Maren is conscious and trying to climb
- Ch 5 — The Push: The lead vessels enter the channel; Ida's signals guide them around the worst debris; twenty-five minutes remaining
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Maren stops moving; the tide is rising; Ida calculates — she could reach her daughter in eight minutes, but the fleet needs fifteen more minutes of guidance
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Ida tries to automate the light pattern, fails, and must choose; she chooses the fleet; fifteen minutes remaining
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: The last vessel enters safe water; Ida runs for the rocks; ten minutes remaining; Maren is underwater

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Ida reaches Maren, pulls her from the water, carries her toward high ground; the tsunami appears on the horizon; they're not going to make the cottage
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Ida and Maren take shelter in a sea cave as the wave passes; the lighthouse survives; the fleet survives; Maren survives with permanent injuries. Ida tends the light the next night, but she no longer tends it alone.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Duty to many versus duty to one
**Deep theme:** Isolation masquerading as purpose is still isolation; connection is what gives purpose meaning
**Thematic question:** If you must choose between saving your child and saving strangers, what does either choice make you?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Fleet Approach**: Forty-seven vessels in a storm, their running lights visible through rain, all following a single beam from a tower on a cliff.
2. **The Impossible Choice**: Ida at the light, seeing Maren on the rocks below through the rain, the fleet needing guidance, the clock counting down.
3. **The Wave**: The tsunami appearing on the horizon as a dark line that becomes a wall, Ida and Maren scrambling for the cave, the lighthouse disappearing behind white water.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Impossible, The Finest Hours
**Book comparables:** The Light Between Oceans by M.L. Stedman, Into the Drowning Deep by Mira Grant

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Ticking-clock thriller in a classical tragedy frame. The forty-five-minute timeline provides relentless structure. The prose is elemental — water, stone, light, storm. Ida's isolation has given her an almost mystical relationship with the lighthouse; she anthropomorphizes it. The impossible choice is the center; everything before sets it up, everything after deals with the consequences. The ending is survival with cost, not triumph.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Scandinavian-American names: Sorensen (SOR-en-sen), Reykdal (RAKE-dahl). Maritime and lighthouse terminology should be confident. The storm is a constant audio presence — wind, waves, rain. Ida's internal voice is measured even under pressure; she's spent years in crises. Maren's pain should be audible but not melodramatic. The tsunami's approach should build with quiet dread. The resolution is exhausted relief, not celebration.

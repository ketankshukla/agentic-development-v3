# The Night Harvest

## Genre
Post-Apocalyptic

## One-Line Premise
A Welsh farmer must protect her village's last harvest from a vampire cult that has ruled Britain since the bombs made the sun deadly.

## Expanded Premise
The nuclear exchange created more than fallout — it filled the atmosphere with particles that block ultraviolet light and enable creatures of legend to walk by day. Britain is now ruled by the Blood Houses, aristocratic vampires who emerged from hiding to claim what they consider their rightful domain. Bronwen Llewelyn is a farmer in a protected valley, one of the few communities that still grows food. When the local Blood House demands an impossible tribute — half the harvest, plus three villagers for "service" — Bronwen must choose between submission and resistance. But the vampires aren't invincible, and the old stories say they can be killed. Bronwen begins hunting the hunters.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Bronwen Llewelyn
**Age:** 46
**Occupation / Background:** Third-generation farmer in the Brecon Beacons; her family has worked this land for a century, surviving the collapse by providing food others couldn't grow
**Core skill:** Land knowledge and guerrilla thinking — she knows every valley, cave, and stream for twenty miles, and understands that predators can become prey
**Fatal flaw:** Stubbornness — she will not compromise or retreat, even when compromise would be wiser
**Character arc:** Believes survival means keeping your head down; discovers that some prices are too high; becomes the symbol of resistance she never intended to be
**Voice and register:** Welsh farmer's practicality, unsentimental and direct. She speaks of killing vampires the way she speaks of slaughtering pigs — a necessary task, not a heroic one. Her internal voice is angrier than she shows.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Lord Cyrus Ashworth, master of the Brecon Blood House
**Goal:** Maintain his territory's productivity while expanding his personal power; he sees humans as livestock and is offended when livestock resist
**Method:** Terror, public executions, and the threat of turning resisters into vampires as punishment (which they consider worse than death)
**Connection to protagonist:** Ashworth "acquired" Bronwen's older sister fifteen years ago; she's now a vampire who serves as his enforcer — and may not remember who she was

## Supporting Characters

**Reverend Gareth Morgan** — The village's spiritual leader who has adapted Christianity to include vampire-killing as holy duty; he provides moral framework and silver crosses
**Maddox** — A former soldier who has studied vampire weaknesses; wounded and cynical, he knows how they can be killed but not why anyone would try
**Enfys Llewelyn-Ashworth** — Bronwen's sister, now vampire, who leads the tribute collection; she's a monster but may retain something of who she was

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Brecon Beacons, Wales
**Time period:** Twenty years after the exchange
**Atmosphere:** Eternal autumn twilight. The sun rises dim and orange, barely warm, no longer dangerous to vampires. Wales is green with rainfall, but the light is wrong. Castle ruins have been reoccupied. Farming communities are walled against both raiders and the night. The old stories are true, and everyone knows it.
**Key locations:** Bronwen's farm and the village of Cwmtawe, Castle Ashworth (the Blood House seat), the hidden forge where silver weapons are made, the old slate mine (refuge and ambush site)

## Central Conflict
Bronwen must organize a farming community into a vampire-hunting resistance, kill or drive out the local Blood House, and do it while protecting the harvest that keeps everyone alive — all while facing a sister who is now the enemy.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The Brecon communities free themselves; proof exists that vampires can be defeated; other regions begin resisting
**If the protagonist fails:** The village is destroyed as an example; the harvest is taken; survivors are scattered or turned
**Personal stakes:** Bronwen may have to kill Enfys. She's not sure she can. She's not sure she can live with herself if she does — or if she doesn't.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: The tribute collectors arrive at harvest festival; Enfys leads them; Bronwen sees her sister's eyes and finds nothing human there
- Ch 2 — The Pull: The village council debates submission; Bronwen argues for resistance; Maddox offers knowledge if someone will use it
- Ch 3 — First Contact: A night ambush of a vampire patrol using silver-tipped stakes; one vampire dies; Bronwen's rebellion begins

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Lord Ashworth responds with punitive raids; two farms burn; the resistance grows rather than shrinks as survivors join
- Ch 5 — The Push: A guerrilla campaign across the valley — traps, ambushes, denied territory; Ashworth's forces cannot be everywhere
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Enfys captures Reverend Morgan and offers a trade: the resistance leaders for his life; Bronwen must negotiate with her sister
- Ch 7 — The Descent: The negotiation fails; Morgan is executed publicly; Bronwen watches her sister kill him and finally accepts that Enfys is gone
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: In the slate mines, preparing for the final assault, Bronwen realizes the resistance has become more important than her personal grief; she can grieve after

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: A coordinated attack on Castle Ashworth during the day (weak sun still slows vampires); the resistance breaks through; Bronwen faces Lord Ashworth and Enfys together
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Ashworth is destroyed. Enfys, given the choice between death and fleeing, chooses to leave — a small mercy Bronwen grants for who her sister was. The valley is free. The harvest comes in.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Vampires are metaphors for aristocratic predation
**Deep theme:** Resistance costs everything — the question is whether submission costs more
**Thematic question:** If someone you love becomes a monster, at what point do you stop hoping they can be saved?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Tribute Assembly**: Vampires walking among cowering villagers at twilight, selecting their victims, while Bronwen watches her sister give orders.
2. **The Mine Ambush**: Resistance fighters collapsing tunnels on vampire pursuers, fighting in darkness where humans know the terrain.
3. **The Castle Assault**: Storming a medieval fortress occupied by the undead, silver weapons against vampire strength, Bronwen and Enfys finally face to face.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** 30 Days of Night, Priest
**Book comparables:** The Passage by Justin Cronin, I Am Legend by Richard Matheson

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Folk horror meets resistance narrative. The vampires are genuinely frightening — predators, not romantic antiheroes. The prose is rooted in agricultural rhythm: seasons, harvests, land. Welsh phrases may appear naturally. The pacing follows a guerrilla warfare structure: strike, retreat, build, strike again. The emotional core is Bronwen's relationship with who Enfys was versus what she is.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Welsh names and terms require attention: Bronwen (BRON-wen), Llewelyn (leh-WEL-in), Enfys (EN-vis), Gareth (GAH-reth), Cwmtawe (COOM-tah-weh), Brecon (BREH-kon). Welsh terms: nos da (good night), croeso (welcome). Ashworth speaks aristocratic English. Vampires should sound predatory, not melodramatic. The farming community's dialogue is practical and understated. Action sequences are swift and brutal.

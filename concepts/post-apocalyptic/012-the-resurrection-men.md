# The Resurrection Men

## Genre
Post-Apocalyptic

## One-Line Premise
A disgraced physician must steal corpses to harvest organs for transplant before the warlord's daughter dies, but the bodies he needs are fresh — and still being mourned.

## Expanded Premise
In the twenty years since the Collapse, Dr. Victor Crane has built a surgical practice in the fortified town of Providence, serving anyone who can pay. When the regional warlord's daughter develops kidney failure, Crane is given an impossible choice: find compatible organs or watch his town burned. The only compatible donors are a recently murdered family in a rival settlement — people whose relatives are still guarding the graves. Crane must become what he despised in his former life: a grave robber, a body snatcher, a resurrection man. But his surgical nurse has her own agenda, the rival settlement is hunting for the murderers, and the organs won't stay viable forever.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Dr. Victor Crane
**Age:** 56
**Occupation / Background:** Former Johns Hopkins transplant surgeon, now Providence's only physician; fled Baltimore after a malpractice scandal he was innocent of but couldn't fight
**Core skill:** Surgical expertise — he can perform procedures under conditions that would make other doctors refuse; he's saved hundreds with salvaged equipment and expired drugs
**Fatal flaw:** Cowardice masquerading as pragmatism — he always chooses the path of least immediate resistance, even when it creates worse outcomes later
**Character arc:** Believes compromising ethics is sometimes necessary; discovers that each compromise makes the next easier until you can't recognize yourself; finds a line he won't cross and crosses it anyway, but the opposite direction
**Voice and register:** Educated, articulate, with dark humor about bodies and death. He speaks like someone who's seen too much and handles it by not caring. His internal voice is more conflicted than he admits.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Marshal Carter Briggs, warlord of the Providence Territory
**Goal:** Save his daughter at any cost; he's a brutal man who loves one person with absolute devotion
**Method:** Military force, hostage-taking, and economic control; he doesn't threaten, he simply states consequences
**Connection to protagonist:** Crane has served as Briggs's personal physician for five years; they have an understanding — Crane's skills for Briggs's protection — but this demand shatters the balance

## Supporting Characters

**Nurse Anna Volkov** — Crane's surgical assistant, a former combat medic with a secret: the murdered family were her relatives, and she wants revenge more than she wants to help
**Samuel Vance** — Leader of the settlement where the bodies lie; a peaceful man who will kill to protect his dead
**Elena Briggs** — The warlord's daughter, 22 years old, slowly dying; she's a better person than her father and doesn't want people to die for her

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Providence Territory (former Rhode Island) and surrounding settlements
**Time period:** Twenty years after the Collapse
**Atmosphere:** New England gothic. Stone walls and bare trees, church steeples visible from fortified farmhouses. The settlements look almost normal until you notice the watchtowers and armed patrols. Medical equipment is religious artifact. Surgeons are wizards who can sometimes return the dead.
**Key locations:** Providence Fort (Briggs's headquarters), Crane's clinic, the Westerly settlement, the family cemetery, the surgical theater

## Central Conflict
Crane must steal organs from a guarded cemetery, transport them alive outside the body, and complete a transplant in a settlement that may be burning by the time he's done — all while his assistant plans to use the chaos for murder.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Elena lives, Providence is spared, but Crane has become a grave robber who violated the deepest taboo
**If the protagonist fails:** Elena dies, Briggs destroys Providence in grief, and Crane dies knowing his cowardice killed everyone
**Personal stakes:** Crane has spent twenty years believing he's a healer. This mission will prove whether that's true or just a story he tells himself.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Crane treats Elena for what he initially believes is food poisoning; tests reveal kidney failure; Briggs makes his demand
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Crane seeks alternatives until Briggs kills a hostage to demonstrate seriousness; there's no negotiating; Crane begins planning the theft
- Ch 3 — First Contact: The team crosses into Westerly territory; they locate the cemetery; Anna reveals she knows the guard patterns because she knows the settlement

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: The exhumation at midnight; Crane working under a tarp with flashlights while Anna stands guard; the organs are viable — barely
- Ch 5 — The Push: They escape with the organs; the surgery begins; everything proceeds perfectly until hour six
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Westerly riders arrive at Providence gates, demanding the body snatchers be surrendered; Briggs refuses; siege begins while Crane is mid-surgery
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Anna sabotages the surgery to kill Elena, then admits her plan to Crane: let the daughter die, let Briggs destroy Westerly in retaliation, then kill Briggs
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Crane must choose between completing the surgery to save Elena or letting Anna's revenge unfold; he chooses surgery and renders Anna unconscious

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: As the surgery concludes, Westerly breaches the walls; Crane negotiates directly with Vance, confessing everything, offering himself as punishment if the siege ends
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Elena survives. Crane is exiled. Briggs and Vance reach an uneasy peace. Crane walks into the wilderness, unsure if he's a healer or a monster, but alive.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Medical ethics collapse when civilization does
**Deep theme:** The compromises we make for survival become who we are — we can choose not to make them, but not to undo them
**Thematic question:** If you can save one life by violating the dead, should you — and who decides?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Exhumation**: A midnight surgery in a cemetery, Crane working knee-deep in a grave while guards patrol thirty meters away, harvesting by hand.
2. **The Operating Room**: A twelve-hour transplant with improvised equipment, the building shaking from siege weapons, anesthesia running low.
3. **The Negotiation**: Crane walking alone to Westerly's line, hands raised, explaining exactly what he did and offering himself as payment.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Revenant, The Road
**Book comparables:** The Road by Cormac McCarthy, The Island of Doctor Moreau by H.G. Wells

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Moral horror. The squeamish details aren't gratuitous but serve to illustrate what survival costs. Crane's dark humor provides relief without undercutting gravity. The medical procedures are accurate enough to feel real without becoming technical manuals. The pacing builds constantly — the surgery itself is a ticking clock within a larger ticking clock.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Medical terminology should sound natural, not hesitant. Crane's internal voice is weary but articulate. Anna's Russian accent should be subtle — she's lived in America for years. The cemetery sequences benefit from quiet, tense delivery. Surgical descriptions should be precise without being rushed. Briggs speaks with command authority. Elena is fading but clear.

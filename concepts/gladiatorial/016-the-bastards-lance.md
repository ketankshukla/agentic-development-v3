# The Bastard's Lance

## Genre
Gladiatorial Medieval Fiction

## One-Line Premise
A baseborn knight enters the tournament of the century to claim his murdered father's lands—but his half-brother, the legitimate heir, has hired every champion in Europe to stop him.

## Expanded Premise
William Blackwood is the bastard son of the Earl of Ashford, raised in secret, trained in secret, and legitimised by his father's deathbed confession. But the confession was witnessed only by dying men, and William's half-brother Edmund now holds the earldom and has destroyed the evidence. By ancient law, any man can claim lands through Trial by Combat—if he can defeat the current holder's champion. Edmund has hired twelve champions, the finest knights in Christendom, and announced a grand tournament: William must defeat them all in sequence to stake his claim. No one expects him to survive the first day.

## Protagonist

**Name:** William Blackwood (called "the Bastard of Ashford")
**Age:** 26
**Occupation / Background:** Son of an Earl and a merchant's daughter, raised as a squire to a minor knight, trained in tournament and war. He has competed in small tournaments under false names but never at this level.
**Core skill:** Adaptive combat. Unlike nobles trained to one style, William learned from anyone who would teach him—English, French, German techniques. He fights unpredictably because he has no school.
**Fatal flaw:** His pride in being underestimated. He wants to prove himself so badly that he sometimes takes unnecessary risks to make victories more spectacular.
**Character arc:** Begins fighting for lands and legitimacy—external validation. Discovers that defeating Edmund won't make him noble; only his choices will. Ends claiming inheritance through compassion rather than pure victory.
**Voice and register:** Common-born practical beneath courtly veneer. He learned to speak like a knight but thinks like a craftsman. He measures people by what they do, not what they claim.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Edmund Blackwood, Earl of Ashford, William's legitimate half-brother
**Goal:** To eliminate William permanently and secure his inheritance. Beyond lands, he fears what William represents: proof that their father loved the bastard more.
**Method:** Hiring champions, bribing officials, and sabotaging equipment. If tournament combat doesn't kill William, "accidents" will.
**Connection to protagonist:** They share a father who loved William and tolerated Edmund. The inheritance fight is cover for a deeper wound: Edmund knows their father wished William had been born legitimate.

## Supporting Characters

**Sir Geoffrey of Kent** — An aging knight past his prime, the only champion willing to train William. He was his father's friend and believes in the claim—also, he hates Edmund.

**Lady Catherine Ashford** — Edmund's wife, who suspects her husband murdered the old Earl and married her for alliance, not love. She provides William with intelligence from inside the castle.

**Sir Jean de Boulogne** — A French champion hired by Edmund, arrogant but honourable. He comes to respect William and might be turned.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The tournament fields outside Ashford Castle, in the English Midlands
**Time period:** 1380s, during Richard II's troubled reign, when English nobility is fractious and courts unreliable
**Atmosphere:** Autumn tournament season, mud and spectacle, banners and blood. The field is surrounded by pavilions; the castle looms over everything. The air smells of horse and cooking meat and the tang of coming winter.
**Key locations:** The tournament lists (jousting ground), the melee field, the challenger's pavilion where William's small camp huddles, Ashford Castle's great hall, the forest where William practices in secret.

## Central Conflict
William must defeat twelve champions in four days of tournament—jousts, sword combat, and melee—while Edmund's agents sabotage his equipment, poison his horses, and attempt assassination between rounds.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** William claims the earldom, gaining lands, title, and justice for his father.
**If the protagonist fails:** He dies in combat or assassination, Edmund secures his hold, and the truth of the old Earl's wishes dies with William.
**Personal stakes:** William's identity. Does he fight because he believes in his claim, or because he needs the world to acknowledge what his father secretly believed: that William, not Edmund, deserved to inherit?

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: William works as a household knight to a minor lord, receiving word that his father is dead and his half-brother has taken everything. He challenges Edmund to Trial by Combat.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Edmund announces the grand tournament format: twelve champions, four days. Sir Geoffrey finds William and offers to train him for the impossible.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: The first day of jousting. William unhorses two champions through unorthodox technique, but his lance is sabotaged for the third bout. He nearly dies; the crowd begins to notice something is wrong.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Day two. Sword combats. William defeats two more champions, earning respect from the crowd and suspicion from his opponents. Sir Jean de Boulogne begins questioning Edmund's tactics.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Lady Catherine contacts William, providing evidence that Edmund murdered their father. William now fights for justice, not just inheritance.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Day three. The melee round. Edmund's men arrange for multiple champions to target William simultaneously—technically legal but clearly coordinated. William survives but is badly injured.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Wounded, with only the final day remaining, William learns Edmund has hired one more champion: a legendary German knight who has never lost. Geoffrey can barely keep William standing.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: The night before the final day. Sir Jean de Boulogne visits William, offers to withdraw—he will not serve a murderer. William refuses the gift; he will win legitimately or not at all.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The final day. William faces the German champion in single combat. The fight is the greatest of his life. He wins through a technique he invented himself—no school, no tradition, purely William.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: William earns the right to face Edmund directly. Edmund, no warrior, yields immediately—but William, instead of killing him, offers exile. Catherine's evidence convicts Edmund of murder. William takes the earldom not through blood but through law. He's Lord Ashford, legitimate at last—but what he remembers is that he chose to be merciful when he had every right not to be.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Fighting for rightful inheritance.
**Deep theme:** The difference between claiming what you're owed and earning what you deserve.
**Thematic question:** If you can only prove your nobility through violence, what does that prove about nobility?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Sabotaged Lance**: William's lance shattering on impact, the splinter driving toward his face, the desperate recovery.
2. **The Melee Ambush**: Multiple champions closing on William simultaneously, a desperate fight against coordination.
3. **The Final Strike**: William defeating the German champion with a move no one has seen before—something that is purely his.

**Book comparables:** *The Name of the Rose* by Umberto Eco, *Pillars of the Earth* by Ken Follett

## Tone and Pacing Notes
The book moves through tournament rhythms: preparation, combat, recovery, repeat. The prose should feel medieval but accessible—courtly language when necessary, plain speech when William is alone with his thoughts. Combat scenes are technical and brutal; jousting is described as the terrifying collision it actually was.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Medieval English names are pronounced simply: "Geoffrey" is JEF-ree. French names follow French pronunciation: "Jean de Boulogne" is zhahn duh boo-LOHN-yuh. Tournament terminology should be explained in context. Combat sequences need the heavy sound of impact—armour, hooves, wood splitting.

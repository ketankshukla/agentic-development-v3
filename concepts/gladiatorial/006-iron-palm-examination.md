# Iron Palm Examination

## Genre
Gladiatorial Martial Arts Fiction

## One-Line Premise
A failed imperial scholar must win the emperor's martial tournament to save his disgraced family—using fighting techniques he learned in secret from the rebels who destroyed his father.

## Expanded Premise
Song Wei was meant to take the civil service examination and restore his family's honour after his father's execution for treason. But when he arrives in Chang'an, he discovers the Emperor has suspended the civil exam and instituted a new path to glory: the Wulin Tournament, where scholars must prove they can fight as well as write. The winner earns an imperial appointment. But Wei carries a dangerous secret: his father wasn't a traitor—he was a spy who infiltrated the rebel armies and learned their forbidden fighting arts. Arts that Wei now carries. To win the tournament, Wei must use techniques that could identify him as a rebel sympathiser. To lose is to condemn his family to eternal dishonour.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Song Wei
**Age:** 24
**Occupation / Background:** Failed examination candidate from a disgraced family. His father was a general who supposedly defected to rebels; in truth, he was gathering intelligence and teaching Wei forbidden techniques before his cover was blown.
**Core skill:** Dual mastery. He knows both Imperial martial styles and rebel "heretic" techniques. He can fight orthodox and win, or fight heretic and dominate—but the second path marks him as an enemy of the state.
**Fatal flaw:** His honour. He could win easily by revealing his father's true mission, but the documents that would prove it would also expose the Emperor's spymaster—who would kill everyone Wei loves.
**Character arc:** Begins believing he can win honourably and restore his family through pure skill. Discovers that honour in a corrupt system requires choosing which principles to betray. Ends understanding that true loyalty sometimes means apparent treason.
**Voice and register:** Educated, formal, with the philosophical bent of a scholar. He thinks in classical allusions and judges himself harshly. His external manner is humble; his internal voice is proud and angry.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Prince Yong, the Emperor's third son, who runs the tournament for his own political purposes
**Goal:** To use the tournament to identify and eliminate potential rebel sympathisers while building his own power base for the succession struggle.
**Method:** Manipulating matches, planting spies among contestants, and using the tournament as a loyalty test—anyone who fights too well with heterodox techniques is marked for elimination.
**Connection to protagonist:** Prince Yong ordered General Song's exposure and execution, knowing his true mission but finding him more useful as a dead traitor than a live hero.

## Supporting Characters

**Lin Xiaoli** — A female contestant from a military family, disguised as male. She recognizes Wei's techniques because her father died fighting rebels who used them. She becomes his rival, then ally, then something more complicated when she learns the truth.

**Master Shen** — Wei's official tournament trainer, an elderly monk who once taught Wei's father. He knows the truth and risks everything to help Wei navigate the tournament without revealing his secret.

**The Whispering Blade** — A mysterious contestant who fights with pure rebel technique and no disguise. They claim to be testing whether the Empire can recognize its true enemies. They may be a rebel, a provocateur, or a trap.

## Setting

**Primary location:** Chang'an, capital of the Tang Dynasty, during the tournament
**Time period:** 756 CE, during the An Lushan Rebellion, when the empire is fracturing
**Atmosphere:** Chang'an is the largest city in the world, gleaming with cosmopolitan splendour while civil war rages in the provinces. The tournament grounds are silk pavilions and lacquered wooden platforms. Beneath the beauty, everyone is watching everyone for signs of disloyalty.
**Key locations:** The Tournament Platforms of the Vermilion Bird Gate, the Temple of the White Horse where contestants train, the Forbidden City where the Emperor watches, the Foreign Quarter where Wei meets his father's rebel contacts, the Garden of Convenient Betrayal where political deals are struck.

## Central Conflict
Wei must win thirty-two matches against the empire's finest martial artists, using skills that could get him executed if anyone identifies them. Prince Yong is watching for exactly this. The only path to victory runs directly through exposure.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Wei earns an imperial appointment, uses his position to clear his father's name, and exposes Prince Yong's corruption.
**If the protagonist fails:** His family loses everything, his father's memory remains that of a traitor, and the prince continues to build power toward a succession crisis that could tear the empire apart.
**Personal stakes:** Wei's identity. He is his father's son—is that a source of pride or a death sentence? Using his full skills honours his father but risks joining him in death.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Wei arrives in Chang'an for the civil examination, finds it cancelled, watches the tournament announcement with horror. He cannot leave—his mother mortgaged everything to send him.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Wei registers, begins training with Master Shen. Shen recognizes him, reveals he knows the truth about General Song. The tournament brackets are announced—Wei's first opponent is a prince's favourite.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Wei's first match. He wins using orthodox techniques, but barely—his opponent was better trained. He realizes he cannot survive the tournament on imperial arts alone.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Wei begins secretly practicing his father's techniques. Lin Xiaoli notices, confronts him. He tells a partial truth; she decides to watch rather than report.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Matches progress. Wei blends orthodox and heterodox techniques so subtly that observers disagree about what they saw. He becomes a dark horse favourite. The Whispering Blade defeats several champions openly using rebel styles.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Prince Yong arranges for Wei's next opponent to be The Whispering Blade. During the match, Wei is forced to reveal his full skills to survive. He wins—but half the crowd recognizes what he used. Prince Yong smiles.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Wei is arrested for interrogation. Master Shen tortured for information. Lin Xiaoli's father's killer identified as The Whispering Blade—now she wants Wei dead too.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: In prison, Wei receives a visit from his father's old rebel contact. They offer escape, confirmation of his father's honour, and vengeance—if he abandons the tournament and joins the rebellion. Wei refuses. He will win the imperial way, or not at all.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: Released for the finals (the prince wants a public spectacle), Wei faces Lin Xiaoli in the championship. The fight is personal—she fights for her father's memory, he fights for his. The match becomes a statement of philosophy as much as martial arts.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Wei wins, but in victory offers his prize to Lin Xiaoli—she needs the appointment more than he does. The gesture catches the Emperor's attention. The Emperor reveals he knew about General Song's mission all along and has been testing Wei's loyalty. Wei is appointed—but as a spy, like his father. The cycle continues.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Honour and skill in competition.
**Deep theme:** The impossibility of serving corrupt institutions honourably.
**Thematic question:** Can you restore honour through a system designed to destroy it?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Vermilion Opening**: The tournament's first day, thousands of contestants on hundreds of platforms, fights erupting simultaneously across the entire Gate complex.
2. **The Blade Recognition**: Wei fighting The Whispering Blade, the moment their techniques mirror each other perfectly, and both freeze—recognizing the same teacher.
3. **The Final Philosophy**: Wei versus Lin Xiaoli, two styles that represent two visions of honour, fought in silence before the entire court.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon* (2000), *Ip Man* (2008)
**Book comparables:** *Under Heaven* by Guy Gavriel Kay, *The Poppy War* by R.F. Kuang

## Tone and Pacing Notes
The book moves like classical martial arts cinema—extended sequences of preparation and philosophy punctuated by explosive combat. Fight scenes are balletic and precise, described in terms of principle rather than just action. Political intrigue weaves through everything. The prose should feel translated from Chinese—formal, allusive, occasionally aphoristic.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Chinese names follow Pinyin pronunciation: "Wei" is WAY, "Xiaoli" is shao-LEE. Avoid dramatizing "Oriental" stereotypes—this should sound like educated English with appropriate formality. Martial arts terminology should be explained in context. Fight scenes need rhythmic pacing—fast exchanges punctuated by stillness.

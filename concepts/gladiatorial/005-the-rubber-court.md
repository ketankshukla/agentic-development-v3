# The Rubber Court

## Genre
Gladiatorial Historical Fiction

## One-Line Premise
A disgraced Mayan priestess must win the sacred ball game seven times to save her city from volcanic annihilation—and her own people from the god they've been feeding innocent blood.

## Expanded Premise
Ix Chel Balam was the high oracle of Chichen Itza until she proclaimed that the new king's wars displeased the gods—and was thrown into the cenote as a sacrifice. She survived, but survival made her an abomination: a living dead woman, sacred to K'awiil, god of lightning and war. Now a volcano threatens to destroy the allied cities, and the priests claim only victory in the ulama ball game will appease the mountain god. They need a champion who can win seven games against seven cities. They need someone already dead. They need Ix Chel. But as she fights her way through the sacred courts, she discovers the truth: the priests have been manipulating the gods all along, and the volcano's fury isn't divine—it's a response to centuries of corrupted sacrifice.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Ix Chel Balam (Jaguar Moon)
**Age:** 31
**Occupation / Background:** Former high oracle and calendar keeper of Chichen Itza, trained since childhood in astronomy, prophecy, and the sacred mathematics of the Long Count.
**Core skill:** Sacred geometry. The ball court is a map of the cosmos. She reads trajectories like prophecies, calculates angles like calendar cycles. The game is prayer in motion, and she speaks its language.
**Fatal flaw:** Her faith. She believed in the gods so completely that their betrayal nearly destroyed her. Now she fights for people she no longer trusts, serving gods she no longer believes in.
**Character arc:** Begins as a shattered believer, going through motions. Discovers the gods are real but have been corrupted by human interpretation. Ends as a reformer, not destroying faith but purifying it—at the cost of everything she thought she knew.
**Voice and register:** Formal, liturgical, full of cosmic references. She thinks in cycles and omens. Her internal voice is mournful; her external voice is the terrifying authority of a dead woman who refuses to stay dead.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Ah Puch Kan, High Priest of Xibalba, keeper of the underworld rites
**Goal:** To maintain the corrupt sacrificial system that gives the priesthood power. The volcano's fury is real, but he's been misdirecting sacrifices to empower himself rather than appease the true gods.
**Method:** Political manipulation, control of sacred knowledge, and assassination disguised as divine will. Any threat to his interpretation of the gods must be eliminated.
**Connection to protagonist:** Ah Puch Kan was Ix Chel's teacher. He threw her into the cenote when her prophecies threatened to expose him. Her survival proves the gods rejected his judgment—or worse, accepted hers.

## Supporting Characters

**Koh** — A young ballplayer from a conquered city, forced to compete as tribute. He becomes Ix Chel's partner in doubles matches, and his innocent faith in the old gods reminds her why she once believed.

**Lady Six Skull** — A noblewoman from Tikal who runs her own political games across the allied cities. She sees Ix Chel as a weapon against Chichen Itza's dominance and offers resources and information for alliance.

**The Voice of the Mountain** — A figure Ix Chel encounters in visions—perhaps the true god of the volcano, perhaps her own fractured mind. Its demands are nothing like what the priests claim.

## Setting

**Primary location:** The ball courts of the Mayan alliance—Chichen Itza, Coba, Uxmal, Tikal, and others
**Time period:** 10th century CE, during the Terminal Classic period as Mayan civilization reaches its peak before collapse
**Atmosphere:** Stone pyramids rising from jungle, courts painted in blood and stucco, the rumble of the volcano audible in every silence. The cities are crowded with refugees from smaller settlements already destroyed. The ball courts are sacred spaces where gods watch and mortals die.
**Key locations:** The Great Ball Court of Chichen Itza (largest in Mesoamerica), the cenote where Ix Chel was sacrificed, the volcano's smoking caldera, the Observatory where she once read the stars, the underground chambers where the true gods sleep.

## Central Conflict
Ix Chel must win seven games against champions from seven cities while uncovering the truth about what's really angering the volcano. The priests want her to win, but they don't want her asking questions. Every victory brings her closer to the truth—and the truth might require destroying everything she's fighting for.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The volcano is appeased, the cities survive, and the corrupt priesthood is exposed. Sacrifice continues, but correctly—consenting warriors, not kidnapped children.
**If the protagonist fails:** The volcano erupts, destroying the alliance. The priests blame the defeat on divine displeasure and intensify the sacrifices, drowning the survivors in blood.
**Personal stakes:** Ix Chel's soul. She died once and returned. If she dies again on sacred ground, there is no second return. And if she succeeds by rejecting the gods entirely, she becomes the monster the priests accused her of being.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Ix Chel lives in hiding as a farmer's widow. The volcano's first eruption kills thousands. Priests come to her village seeking the "dead woman who lived." They offer her a purpose.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Ix Chel returns to Chichen Itza, sees the corruption that has grown since her death. She agrees to fight—not for the priests, but to understand why the gods let her survive.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Her first game, against a minor city's champion. The ball court is deadly—the rubber ball can kill, the rings are impossibly high. She wins through sacred geometry, calculating angles no one else can see.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Games two and three. Ix Chel dominates but notices patterns—the priests are choosing her opponents strategically, eliminating political rivals through "sacred" games.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Ix Chel wins game four in doubles with Koh, whose faith reminds her of her younger self. Lady Six Skull approaches with intelligence: the sacrifices aren't going where the priests claim.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Game five takes place at the volcano's base as an emergency ritual. The eruption during the game kills spectators, including people Ix Chel was beginning to trust. The priests blame her for insufficient devotion.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Imprisoned for "impiety," Ix Chel receives visions from the Voice of the Mountain. The truth: the volcano demands an end to human sacrifice entirely. The priests have been lying about what the gods want.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Ix Chel escapes, confronts Ah Puch Kan, learns he knows the truth and has been suppressing it for power. She challenges him to stake everything on the seventh game: winner dictates how the gods are worshipped.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The final game in the Great Ball Court, Ix Chel versus Ah Puch Kan's champion (a warrior specifically trained to kill her). The volcano rumbles throughout. The ball is lethal. The game becomes a theological war.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Ix Chel wins, exposes the corruption, and reforms the sacrifice system. The volcano settles—appeased by truth, not blood. But her victory costs her: she's too holy now, too touched by death. She becomes the new oracle, trapped in sacred isolation, having saved her people by losing any place among them.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Faith versus corruption.
**Deep theme:** The violence we commit in the name of belief, and what it means to worship truly.
**Thematic question:** If your gods demand blood, are they still worth believing in?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Cenote Sacrifice**: Flashback to Ix Chel's original death—bound, thrown into dark water, sinking, and then the light that shouldn't have been there.
2. **The Volcanic Court**: Game five played while the mountain erupts behind them, ash raining on the court, players dodging falling stone while the ball flies.
3. **The Final Score**: The seventh game's last point, the rubber ball screaming toward the stone ring, everything riding on a single impossible shot.

## Tone and Pacing Notes
The book should feel ancient—mythic pacing with explosive violence. Ball game sequences are rhythmic and brutal, described with the intensity of prayer. Court politics move slower but are equally deadly. The prose should incorporate Mayan cosmology naturally—the world is alive with meaning, every action is observed by gods. The volcano is a constant presence, rumbling beneath everything.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Mayan names follow Spanish-influenced pronunciation: "Ix Chel" is eesh CHEL, "Xibalba" is shee-BAHL-ba. The ball game should be narrated with building intensity—rhythmic until it explodes into action. The Voice of the Mountain should be differentiated—hollow, echoing, not quite human. Avoid dramatizing gore; the violence is sacred, not gratuitous.

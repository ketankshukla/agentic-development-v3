# Sun Catcher

## Genre
Gladiatorial Historical Fiction

## One-Line Premise
An Aztec Eagle Warrior captured in a flower war must defeat four champions in the gladiatorial sacrifice—or watch the sun go dark and his entire world end.

## Expanded Premise
Necalli was the youngest Eagle Warrior in Tenochtitlan's history, a prodigy who captured twenty enemy warriors for sacrifice before his twentieth year. But in the last flower war, he was captured himself by the armies of Tlaxcala. Now he faces the gladiatorial stone: a prisoner tied to a platform, armed with a feathered club, forced to fight four fully-armed jaguar warriors in sequence. The catch—if any captive survives all four opponents, the priests believe the gods have chosen him as too valuable to sacrifice. Instead, he becomes a divine champion. Necalli intends to survive. But he's begun to suspect something terrifying: the priests know the sun isn't actually dying, the sacrifices don't actually work, and the entire religion that shaped his life is a machine for political control.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Necalli (He Who Punishes)
**Age:** 22
**Occupation / Background:** Eagle Warrior of Tenochtitlan, son of a merchant who rose through pure martial skill to join the elite warrior class. Captured in what should have been a routine flower war engagement.
**Core skill:** Combat calculation. He reads fights like equations—angles, distances, timing, fatigue. While others fight with passion, he fights with mathematics. Four-against-one becomes four one-against-one fights if he can control the sequence.
**Fatal flaw:** His faith. He believed completely in the Aztec cosmology—that sacrifice fed the sun, that warriors died gloriously to sustain the world. The cracks in that faith undermine his certainty in everything.
**Character arc:** Begins as a true believer who assumes survival will restore his glory. Discovers the system is built on lies. Ends having to choose between the comforting false faith that made him strong and a truth that might make him free.
**Voice and register:** Warrior-direct, counting and measuring, with sudden poetry about blood and sun and sacrifice. He thinks in Nahuatl concepts that don't translate cleanly. His external voice is proud; his internal voice is beginning to question.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** High Priest Tezozomoc, Keeper of the Sacred Calendar
**Goal:** To maintain the sacrificial system that keeps the priesthood in power. Any evidence that the gods do not require blood must be suppressed—including captives who survive the gladiatorial stone.
**Method:** Manipulation of "divine signs," secret murder of inconvenient survivors, and control of the calendar itself—he decides when eclipses are predicted and when they "surprise" the people.
**Connection to protagonist:** Tezozomoc's predecessor declared Necalli's birth under an inauspicious sign; Tezozomoc reversed the prediction to prove his own power. Necalli's entire life has been shaped by the priest's politics.

## Supporting Characters

**Cualli** — A fellow captive from Tenochtitlan, an older warrior who has already accepted death and now seeks only to die well. His acceptance challenges Necalli's determination to survive.

**Citlalli** — A priestess of Tlazolteotl, the goddess who hears confessions. She encounters Necalli before his trial and senses his doubt. She carries her own doubts about the system she serves.

**Yaotl** — The chief of the four Jaguar Warriors assigned to kill Necalli. He respects Necalli's reputation and intends to give him an honourable death—not a slaughter.

## Setting

**Primary location:** Tlaxcala, the enemy city-state that has captured Necalli, and its ceremonial pyramid
**Time period:** 1502 CE, seventeen years before Cortes, at the height of Aztec power
**Atmosphere:** High altitude, thin air, constant religious ceremony. The pyramid steps are stained with old blood. The city is a complex of temples and gardens and cages for captives. Everything is watching: the sun, the gods, the priests, the crowd.
**Key locations:** The Captive House where prisoners await sacrifice, the Great Pyramid and its gladiatorial stone, the Temple of Tlazolteotl where confessions are heard, the Calendar Chamber where Tezozomoc manipulates prophecy.

## Central Conflict
Necalli must survive four sequential combats while tied to a stone platform, armed with a feathered club against warriors with obsidian swords. But even survival brings danger: Tezozomoc will never let a survivor undermine the sacrificial theology.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Necalli survives, becomes a divine champion, and must decide whether to expose the lies he's discovered or live within them for power.
**If the protagonist fails:** He dies on the stone, feeding a sun that may not need feeding, validating a system built on deception.
**Personal stakes:** Necalli's entire identity was built on faith—warrior excellence in service of divine necessity. If the faith is false, what was his life worth?

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Necalli fights in a flower war, captures an enemy warrior, then is captured himself. The sequence is disorienting—one moment triumphant, the next a prisoner. He is processed into the Captive House.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Necalli learns the rules of the gladiatorial sacrifice. He meets Cualli, who has accepted death, and Citlalli, who seems to offer something else. He begins to plan survival.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: Necalli's first combat on the stone. He defeats the first Jaguar Warrior—barely—using his mathematical approach. One down, three to go.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: Recovery between rounds. Necalli notices inconsistencies in the priestly rituals—words changed, gestures improvised. Citlalli confesses she has seen records that don't match official history.
- Ch 5 — The Push: Second and third combats. Necalli survives through desperate ingenuity. His mathematical approach begins to fail as exhaustion compounds; he has to reach for something deeper.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: The night before the final combat. Citlalli brings him evidence: records showing that predicted eclipses were manipulated, that "divine signs" are manufactured. The priests know the sun will rise regardless.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Necalli's faith collapses. If the gods don't need blood, everything he's done—every warrior he captured for sacrifice—was murder, not service. He enters the final day broken.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: Dawn. The final combat approaches. Cualli, watching Necalli's despair, offers a different interpretation: perhaps the truth is that human will matters more than divine need. Perhaps survival itself is proof of worthiness.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The fourth and final combat against Yaotl, the most skilled of the Jaguar Warriors. Necalli fights not for gods but for truth, not for glory but for the chance to expose what he knows. He survives.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Necalli is declared divine champion. Tezozomoc moves to have him quietly killed—but Necalli has prepared, using Citlalli's evidence and the crowd's witness. He denounces the priest publicly. The crowd, uncertain, turns. Tezozomoc is thrown from the pyramid. But the system remains; one truth doesn't destroy a civilization. Necalli lives, changed, uncertain what to do with a life he no longer understands.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Survival against impossible odds.
**Deep theme:** The faiths that shape us and the truths that break them.
**Thematic question:** If everything you believed made you who you are was a lie, is the person you became still real?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Capture Reversal**: Necalli taking a prisoner, then becoming one, the same ritual viewed from both sides.
2. **The Stone Combats**: Four fights on the circular platform, tethered, limited, forcing mathematical desperation.
3. **The Pyramid Accusation**: Necalli, bloodied and triumphant, denouncing the High Priest before the crowd.

## Tone and Pacing Notes
The book moves through capture, imprisonment, combat, and revelation. The Aztec worldview should be immersive—not explained but inhabited. Violence is religious, blood is sacred, death is not an end. The prose should feel translated from something older, with occasional Nahuatl concepts that have no English equivalent. The philosophical collapse should be felt, not argued.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Nahuatl names follow Spanish pronunciation conventions: "Necalli" is neh-KAHL-ee, "Tezozomoc" is teh-zoh-ZOH-mok. Religious terms should be spoken with weight—these are not exotic curiosities but lived beliefs. Combat sequences on the stone need a circular, trapped quality. The crowd should feel present but not individualized.

# Obsidian Sun

## Genre
Historical Action

## One-Line Premise
A captured enemy warrior must fight his way out of Tenochtitlan before the next sunrise—when he's scheduled to have his heart cut out atop the Great Temple.

## Expanded Premise
Citlali was a jaguar warrior of the Tlaxcalans, enemies of the Aztec Empire. Captured in a flower war, he's been kept in luxury for months—fed, trained, honored—because his death will be a gift to the gods. But on the eve of his sacrifice, a priest whispers a secret: the emperor plans to use the ceremony to announce an invasion of Tlaxcala. Every warrior and elder Citlali has ever known will die in the coming war. He has one night to escape the most heavily guarded city in the Americas, reach his homeland across enemy territory, and warn his people before dawn breaks and the gods receive their meal.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Citlali ("Star")
**Age:** 27
**Occupation / Background:** Jaguar warrior of Tlaxcala, an independent city-state surrounded by the Aztec Empire. Trained from childhood in warfare, raised to see capture as the highest honor—a warrior taken alive has proved his worth. Now he must reject everything he was taught and choose survival over glory.
**Core skill:** Combat with obsidian weapons—the macuahuitl (obsidian-edged club) and atlatl (spear-thrower). He's also a runner, trained to carry messages across mountains at speed that would kill lesser men.
**Fatal flaw:** He has internalized the belief that a warrior's death is sacred. Part of him still wants to die on the altar; that part keeps whispering that escape is cowardice.
**Character arc:** Begins torn between the honor of sacrifice and the duty to warn his people. Discovers that true honor isn't dying beautifully—it's living for something larger than yourself. Ends either having rejected his culture's death-worship or having found a way to honor it differently.
**Voice and register:** Formal and ritualistic in speech, reflecting a culture where every word carries weight. His internal voice is more chaotic—the struggle between what he was taught and what he feels.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The city of Tenochtitlan itself, and High Priest Tlacaelel who oversees sacrifices
**Goal:** The priest needs Citlali's sacrifice to legitimize the emperor's war declaration. If Citlali escapes, the ceremony is disrupted, the gods are insulted, and the invasion may be delayed.
**Method:** The entire city becomes a trap. Bridges can be raised, canals block every route, and thousands of warriors guard every exit. Tlacaelel dispatches eagle warriors—the empire's elite—to track Citlali through the night.
**Connection to protagonist:** Tlacaelel has personally prepared Citlali for sacrifice, bathing him, hearing his confessions, learning his fears. He knows Citlali better than anyone alive—and he knows exactly how to break him.

## Supporting Characters

**Xochitl** — A temple slave who helps Citlali escape for her own reasons: her family was sacrificed last year, and she's discovered she no longer believes in the gods that demand such offerings. She knows the hidden routes through the city.

**Necuametl** — A fellow captive from a different tribe who was supposed to be sacrificed alongside Citlali. He escapes with them but his motives are unclear—is he truly fleeing, or has he been turned by the priests to ensure Citlali's recapture?

**Tizoc** — An eagle warrior assigned to hunt Citlali. He is everything Citlali might have been if born Aztec—honorable, devoted, absolutely certain of his righteous cause. Their confrontation forces Citlali to articulate what he's running toward, not just what he's running from.

## Setting

**Primary location:** Tenochtitlan, capital of the Aztec Empire—a city built on an island in the middle of a lake, connected to the shore by three causeways.
**Time period:** 1518 CE, one year before the Spanish arrival—the height of Aztec power.
**Atmosphere:** A city of impossible beauty and casual horror. Pyramids rise above gardens; flowers line the canals; and drying blood stains the steps of every temple. The air smells of copal incense and copper. Everything is color—feathers, dyes, painted stone—and everything is watched. At night, torch light reflects off the lake, and the drums never stop.
**Key locations:** The Great Temple complex, where Citlali is held; the floating gardens (chinampas) where he first hides; the marketplace of Tlatelolco, crowded even at night; the causeway to Tacuba, the only possible escape route; the mountains beyond the lake, where freedom waits.

## Central Conflict
Citlali must escape an inescapable city in a single night, carrying intelligence that could save his nation, while the most powerful empire in the Americas hunts him through streets designed to prevent exactly what he's attempting.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Tlaxcala receives warning and prepares its defenses. The invasion is bloodier than the Aztecs expected. The war drags on—and when strange men with metal skins arrive a year later, Tlaxcala remembers who its true enemy is.
**If the protagonist fails:** He dies on the altar as scheduled. The invasion catches Tlaxcala unprepared. His nation falls. A year later, when Cortes arrives, there will be no indigenous allies to help him—and history changes in ways no one can predict.
**Personal stakes:** Citlali's soul, as he understands it. He was taught that dying on the altar sends him to the highest heaven. If he escapes, he's choosing to reject that reward. He must find something worth more than paradise.

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Inciting World: Citlali is prepared for tomorrow's sacrifice—bathed, dressed, honored. The high priest Tlacaelel shares the details of the ceremony with pride. After he leaves, a different priest enters with the warning about the invasion. Citlali realizes that his death will seal the doom of his people.
- Ch 2 — The Pull: Xochitl, a temple slave, offers to help him escape—she knows a route. Citlali hesitates: is dishonor worth it? When he learns that among the first targets of the invasion will be the school where his younger brother trains, the decision is made.
- Ch 3 — First Contact: The escape from the temple complex. Guards die; Citlali takes a macuahuitl from a fallen warrior. The alarm is raised. The hunt begins. In the first clash with eagle warriors, Necuametl proves himself in combat—but something about him feels wrong.

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Escalation: The city locks down. Bridges rise; patrols multiply. Citlali, Xochitl, and Necuametl hide in the floating gardens, surrounded by enemies. Xochitl reveals her knowledge of the canals—there may be a way to reach the Tacuba causeway by water.
- Ch 5 — The Push: A midnight crossing through the marketplace. They steal canoes, supplies, and a disguise for Citlali. The plan seems to be working—they reach the edge of the island with the causeway in sight.
- Ch 6 — The Reversal: Necuametl's betrayal. He signals the eagle warriors; it was a trap all along. Xochitl is killed. Citlali is surrounded, wounded, and faced with Tizoc—the warrior who will drag him back to the altar.
- Ch 7 — The Descent: Captured and returned to the temple complex. Citlali is bound and placed in view of the sunrise that will signal his death. Tlacaelel explains that he always knew about the escape attempt; it was allowed to happen to prove to the gods that this sacrifice was truly willing—because he came back.
- Ch 8 — The Reckoning: In his cell, awaiting dawn, Citlali makes peace with death—and then refuses it. His peace isn't acceptance; it's clarity. He didn't fail because he tried to escape; he failed because he trusted the wrong person. He won't make that mistake again.

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Assault: The ceremony begins as the sun rises. Citlali is led to the altar. At the final moment, he uses a concealed obsidian blade to cut his bonds and fights his way down the pyramid steps—a running battle through the most sacred space in the Aztec world. Tizoc pursues, and their duel on the causeway becomes the climax.
- Ch 10 — Resolution: Citlali defeats Tizoc but does not kill him—rejecting the cycle of sacrifice and counter-sacrifice. He escapes across the causeway as the city erupts in chaos behind him. The epilogue shows him reaching Tlaxcala, delivering his warning, and watching his brother and elders prepare for the war that's coming. He's alive; his people will survive. The gods will have to find their meal elsewhere.

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** Escape from death is not cowardice; it's choosing what to live for.
**Deep theme:** Cultural beliefs that demand death as the highest good are prisons, and breaking free requires rejecting not just the cage but the key you've been carrying.
**Thematic question:** If everything you've been taught says your death is sacred, what does it take to decide your life is worth more?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Temple Escape**: Night, torches, obsidian blades reflecting flame. Citlali fights down corridors decorated with skulls, past priests who try to stop him, over walls slick with old blood.
2. **The Canoe Chase**: Through the floating gardens, between the chinampas, paddles digging into black water while eagle warriors hurl javelins from the banks and other canoes close in.
3. **The Causeway Duel**: Sunrise painting the lake gold. Citlali and Tizoc face each other on the narrow causeway, the city's screams behind them, mountains ahead—a fight that will decide whether Citlali's flight was heroism or sacrilege.

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *Apocalypto* (2006), *Escape from New York* (1981) for the one-night citywide escape
**Book comparables:** *Aztec* by Gary Jennings, *The War of Flowers* by Tad Williams

## Tone and Pacing Notes
This book is a sprint disguised as a tragedy. The pacing is relentless—every chapter ends with a new danger, every escape leads to a worse trap. But beneath the action is a philosophical argument about the nature of sacrifice and the right to choose your own death. The prose should feel alien—reflecting a culture with different assumptions about death and honor—while remaining accessible.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Nahuatl terms (macuahuitl, atlatl, chinampa) require careful pronunciation with emphasis on vowels. Citlali's internal voice should carry formal weight—he thinks in a culture where poetry and violence are intertwined. Action scenes need rapid delivery; philosophical moments require space. The drums of Tenochtitlan should feel present in the background of every scene.

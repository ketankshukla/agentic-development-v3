# The Spider's Web

## Genre
mythological-action

## One-Line Premise
When Anansi the Spider God is captured by a forgotten deity seeking revenge, his mortal daughter must outwit creatures from every African mythology to free him—before his stories unravel reality.

## Expanded Premise
Abena never knew her father was a god. Raised by her grandmother in Kumasi, she thought Anansi was just a folk tale. When reality begins glitching—people forgetting their names, buildings vanishing, time loops forming—her dying grandmother reveals the truth. Anansi is the keeper of all stories, and without him spinning new tales, old ones are collapsing. The forgotten god Sasabonsam has trapped Anansi in a web of vengeance, and only someone who shares Anansi's blood—and his cunning—can navigate the impossible trials to free him.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Abena Mensah
**Age:** 23
**Occupation / Background:** University student studying folklore and literature; raised by her grandmother after her mother disappeared; always felt like she didn't quite fit into normal life
**Core skill:** Story-weaving—she can perceive narrative structure in reality, seeing how events connect as plots; she can manipulate minor events by changing their story
**Fatal flaw:** Overreliance on cleverness; she believes every problem can be tricked away and underestimates when direct action is needed
**Character arc:** Believes the world runs on logic and that myths are metaphors; discovers stories are the fundamental fabric of existence; becomes the bridge between divine narrative and mortal reality
**Voice and register:** Quick-witted, irreverent, talks her way through problems. Internal voice analyzes everything for angles and advantages. Her grandmother's proverbs surface at key moments, irritating her until she realizes their value.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Sasabonsam, the Forgotten Vampire God
**Goal:** To unmake the world of stories that forgot him; he was once worshipped but Anansi's tales eclipsed his cult; he seeks revenge by ending all narrative
**Method:** Trapping Anansi in a web of forgotten stories where even the spider cannot escape; using the unraveling reality to spread his influence; consuming the stories that remain
**Connection to protagonist:** Sasabonsam is, in a twisted way, her uncle—he and Anansi were once allies before jealousy destroyed their bond; he sees in Abena a chance for a different kind of family

## Supporting Characters

**Anansi** — The spider god, trapped but still able to communicate through story-echoes; provides cryptic guidance; genuinely loves his daughter but cannot express it directly
**Kweku** — A trickster spirit (child of Anansi by another mother) who serves as guide and rival; helps Abena for his own reasons; represents what she might become
**Grandmother's Spirit** — Abena's grandmother lingers as a ghost, providing wisdom and guilt; she knew the truth for decades and chose silence

## Setting

**Primary location:** Ghana and the spirit world of Akan cosmology—Kumasi, Accra, and the realm of stories where myths live
**Time period:** Present day
**Atmosphere:** Modern African city layered with ancient story-space. Markets where gods haggle, roads that fold into legend, buildings that remember what they were before colonialism renamed them. The spirit world is made of narrative—locations exist because stories require them, and forgotten places crumble into nothing.
**Key locations:** Abena's grandmother's compound in Kumasi; the story-market where myths are traded; Sasabonsam's web in the space between tales; the original World Tree where Anansi first climbed to steal stories from the sky god

## Central Conflict
Abena must complete three impossible tasks to reach Sasabonsam's domain—each task drawn from different African mythological traditions, requiring her to outthink beings who have existed since stories began. But each task also teaches her more about her father, including truths she doesn't want to accept: Anansi is selfish, manipulative, and the reason her mother disappeared.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** Anansi is freed, stories stabilize, and reality continues; Abena gains a father she's not sure she wants
**If the protagonist fails:** All stories end simultaneously; people forget who they are; existence becomes meaningless chaos without narrative to give it shape
**Personal stakes:** Abena must decide whether Anansi deserves rescue given what she learns about him; saving the world means forgiving a father who abandoned her

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Unraveling: Reality glitches around Abena—a professor forgets the lecture he's giving, a building vanishes; her grandmother dies revealing the truth
- Ch 2 — The Story-Market: Abena enters the spirit world through her grandmother's mirror; meets Kweku who offers to guide her for a price she can't yet pay
- Ch 3 — The First Task: She must retrieve a drum from Mami Wata's underwater court; succeeds through cunning, trading a story Mami Wata has never heard

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — The Father's Voice: Anansi speaks through story-echoes, teaching Abena how to see narrative; his lessons are manipulation dressed as wisdom
- Ch 5 — The Second Task: Face the Lion-God Agu in a contest of strength; Abena cannot win physically but changes the rules of the story itself
- Ch 6 — Sasabonsam's Offer: The vampire god appears, offering to release Anansi if Abena takes his place; reveals how Anansi stole stories that belonged to others
- Ch 7 — The Mother's Truth: Abena learns her mother didn't disappear—she chose to leave rather than be a character in Anansi's endless stories; Abena questions everything
- Ch 8 — The Third Task: Convince the Sky God Nyame to grant passage to the space between stories; requires admitting she might be saving someone unworthy

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Spider's Web: Abena enters Sasabonsam's trap, a maze of forgotten tales; she must navigate by becoming part of the stories; faces her father's worst versions
- Ch 10 — The New Story: Abena doesn't simply free Anansi—she rewrites the story so that Sasabonsam is remembered, Anansi is humbled, and she becomes the keeper of stories moving forward

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** A daughter must save her divine father from imprisonment
**Deep theme:** We can love flawed people while refusing to excuse their flaws
**Thematic question:** Do you owe loyalty to family who failed you, and what does that loyalty require?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Story-Market Arrival**: A bustling supernatural bazaar where creatures trade myths like currency; stories literally flying between vendors; Abena's first glimpse of how reality really works
2. **Mami Wata's Court**: The river goddess's underwater palace of beauty and danger; negotiation that is also seduction; water that speaks in riddles
3. **The Web of Forgetting**: Sasabonsam's prison—a space where narrative becomes visible, threads of story forming a three-dimensional maze that Abena must solve while her own memories try to unravel

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Black Panther meets Everything Everywhere All at Once
**Book comparables:** Anansi Boys by Neil Gaiman; Akata Witch by Nnedi Okofor

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Playful but high-stakes. The trickster tradition means humor is essential—even dark moments have wit. The prose should feel like oral storytelling, with direct address moments and narrative self-awareness. Ghana should feel modern and vibrant, not a museum of tradition. The mythology is alive, not archived. Pacing varies between frantic chase and thoughtful puzzle-solving; Abena thinks her way through more than she fights.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Akan names and terms: Abena (ah-BEH-nah), Anansi (ah-NAHN-see), Sasabonsam (sah-sah-BON-sahm), Kweku (KWEH-koo), Nyame (NYAH-meh), Mami Wata (MAH-mee WAH-tah). Anansi's voice should be quicksilver and slippery—you can never quite trust it. Sasabonsam is deep and hungry. Kweku is youthful trickster energy. Abena's grandmother speaks in proverbs. The story-market should feel chaotic and multilingual. Different mythological creatures should have distinct vocal qualities.

# The Iron Orisha

## Genre
mythological-action

## One-Line Premise
A blacksmith's apprentice forges a weapon that kills a god—and must forge six more before Ogun's brothers destroy the mortal world in vengeance.

## Expanded Premise
Adewale never meant to kill a god. When Eshu's trickster influence guides his hand during an ordinary forging, the iron he shapes becomes the first weapon that can truly slay an orisha. Now Ogun, god of iron, lies dead by Adewale's accidental strike, and the Sixteen Orishas split between those who want vengeance and those who see opportunity. Shango demands Adewale's head. Oshun offers protection—if he forges weapons to kill her divine rivals. Between war and manipulation, Adewale must find a third path or watch everything burn.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Adewale Ogundimu
**Age:** 22
**Occupation / Background:** Blacksmith's apprentice in the city of Ife; inherited his grandfather's forge when plague took his family; works alone because no one will share space with an unlucky man
**Core skill:** Divine forging—he can shape iron into forms that affect spiritual reality; what he forges becomes real on both the physical and orisha planes
**Fatal flaw:** Passivity disguised as humility; he lets others decide his fate because he doesn't believe he deserves agency
**Character arc:** Believes he is cursed and should accept whatever happens to him; discovers that refusing to choose is still a choice with consequences; learns to take responsibility for the power he wields
**Voice and register:** Quiet, apologetic, habitually positions himself as small. But when he speaks about iron and forging, authority emerges unconsciously. Observes the world as material to be shaped—what is its nature, how can it be changed?

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Shango, Orisha of Thunder
**Goal:** To avenge his brother Ogun by executing Adewale and destroying the knowledge of god-killing weapons
**Method:** Summoning storms, raising mortal armies, threatening kingdoms that shelter Adewale; he is not subtle because he has never needed to be
**Connection to protagonist:** Shango loved Ogun as only brothers can; his rage is genuine grief transformed; he cannot accept that Adewale acted without malice

## Supporting Characters

**Eshu** — The trickster orisha who set events in motion; sometimes helps Adewale, sometimes sabotages him; his goals are unknowable but his interventions are constant
**Oya** — Orisha of winds and the dead, Shango's wife but not his ally; she sees opportunity in the power shift and offers Adewale genuine protection—with strings
**Akin** — A warrior who witnessed Ogun's death and was marked by it; becomes Adewale's reluctant protector; believes in destiny and struggles with his role in it

## Setting

**Primary location:** Yorubaland—the sacred city of Ife, the forests of Oyo, and the realm of the orishas that overlays mortal reality
**Time period:** 14th century CE, during the height of Yoruba civilization
**Atmosphere:** Red earth and green forests; market cities full of bronze and iron; the shimmer where spirit-world overlaps the physical. Thunder is Shango's voice, rivers are Oshun's body, crossroads belong to Eshu. The divine is never distant—it's woven into every aspect of daily life.
**Key locations:** Adewale's forge in Ife; the shrine-city of Oyo; the crossroads where Eshu makes bargains; the realm of the dead where Oya rules; the forge of heaven where Ogun once worked

## Central Conflict
Adewale must find a way to end the divine civil war he accidentally started. The only path forward seems to be forging more god-killing weapons—either for Shango's execution or for his enemies' protection. But creating more of these weapons means more dead orishas, more chaos, and potentially the end of divine order altogether. He must find a third option: a weapon that can restore balance without more divine blood.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** He forges a weapon that can threaten but not kill, creating a new balance; the orishas accept a changed relationship with humanity
**If the protagonist fails:** The divine civil war spills into the mortal world; Yorubaland burns; either Shango purges the world or his enemies do
**Personal stakes:** Adewale can live if he accepts becoming a weapon himself—Oshun's champion, Oya's servant—but doing so means surrendering the agency he's finally learning to claim

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Accident: Adewale forges a routine tool and somehow creates a god-killer; Ogun arrives to investigate and dies; Shango's storm begins
- Ch 2 — The Trickster's Game: Eshu reveals himself as the catalyst; offers contradictory advice; Adewale must flee Ife as Shango's followers hunt him
- Ch 3 — The Wind's Bargain: Oya finds Adewale and offers sanctuary in exchange for weapons; he refuses to make more god-killers; she's intrigued rather than angry

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — The Warrior's Oath: Akin joins as protector, bound by what he witnessed; they journey toward the only one who might help—Obatala, king of orishas
- Ch 5 — The River's Seduction: Oshun intercepts them with a different offer; Adewale sees the orishas' internal politics; realizes none of them want peace
- Ch 6 — Shango's Hammer: The thunder god catches them; Akin falls defending Adewale; Eshu's intervention saves them but at the cost of Adewale learning his grandfather made similar choices
- Ch 7 — The Forge of Heaven: In the spirit realm, Adewale finds Ogun's divine forge; learns the god-killing property was always in him, not the metal
- Ch 8 — The Third Path: Adewale realizes he can forge a weapon that binds rather than kills; but doing so requires him to accept his power openly

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Final Forging: Adewale forges the Chain of Heaven, a weapon that can bind even orishas; Shango attacks during the forging; Oya and Oshun join the battle on opposite sides
- Ch 10 — The New Covenant: Adewale binds Shango—not to destroy him, but to demonstrate that mortals and orishas must negotiate; a new relationship between divine and human begins; Adewale becomes the first priest of Iron who serves no master

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** A mortal must survive a war between gods
**Deep theme:** Refusing to choose is still a choice, and accepting your power means accepting its consequences
**Thematic question:** What responsibility do we have for the tools we create?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Accidental Murder**: The forge blazes white, metal moves wrong, and Ogun—god of iron itself—crumbles to rust as Adewale watches in horror
2. **The Storm King's Fury**: Shango descending from lightning with his twin axes, the sky itself attacking as Adewale flees through a burning market
3. **The Forge of Heaven**: Adewale working Ogun's anvil as the spirit realm fragments around him, shaping something new while gods clash overhead

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Black Panther meets Clash of the Titans
**Book comparables:** Nnedi Okofor's Akata Witch; Children of Blood and Bone by Tomi Adeyemi

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Mythological weight with grounded humanity. The orishas should feel genuinely divine—not superheroes but cosmic forces with personalities. The action sequences involve gods, so scale matters, but Adewale remains the human center. Yoruba culture is presented as living and sophisticated, not exotic. The prose has oral tradition rhythm—call and response, praise names, proverbs—without becoming stilted. Tension builds through divine politics; release comes through forging and combat.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Yoruba names follow standard pronunciation: Adewale (ah-deh-WAH-leh), Ogun (oh-GOON), Shango (SHAHN-goh), Oshun (oh-SHOON), Oya (oh-YAH), Eshu (EH-shoo), Obatala (oh-bah-TAH-lah), Ife (EE-feh). Divine voices should be larger than mortal. Shango is thunder-rumble. Oshun is river-smooth. Eshu shifts tone constantly. Adewale grows from hesitant to authoritative as the story progresses. Forging sequences should have rhythm and heat.

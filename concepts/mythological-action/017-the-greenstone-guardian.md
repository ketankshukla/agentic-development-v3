# The Greenstone Guardian

## Genre
mythological-action

## One-Line Premise
The son of a disgraced chief must reclaim his family's stolen pounamu (sacred greenstone) from the spirit world before its power awakens the sleeping giant beneath New Zealand.

## Expanded Premise
Tane Raukawa was raised in exile, his father hanged by the British for rebellion. When earthquakes shake the North Island and ancient forests begin dying, a tohunga reveals the truth: his family's sacred pounamu—a weapon that bound the sleeping giant Matau beneath the earth—was stolen and carried into Te Po, the spirit world. If Tane doesn't retrieve it before the next full moon, the giant will wake and tear the islands apart. But entering Te Po means facing ancestors who may not want him to return.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Tane Raukawa
**Age:** 24
**Occupation / Background:** Laborer in Wellington, son of a chief executed by colonial authorities; educated in mission schools but secretly taught the old ways by his grandmother
**Core skill:** Wairua-walking—he can perceive and partially enter the spirit world while still living; inherited from a bloodline of boundary-walkers
**Fatal flaw:** Shame about his heritage that makes him hide who he is; he has spent years pretending to be Pakeha (European) to survive
**Character arc:** Believes his heritage is a burden that destroyed his family; discovers his ancestry is the only thing that can save his people; embraces the identity he spent years suppressing
**Voice and register:** Code-switches between educated colonial English and the rhythms of te reo Māori. His internal voice is more traditional; his external is more assimilated. As the story progresses, the internal becomes external.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** Matau, the Sleeping Giant
**Goal:** To wake and reclaim the surface world that was stolen when the sky-father and earth-mother separated; he is not evil but his awakening would devastate everything above
**Method:** His dreams seep through the earth, poisoning forests and causing earthquakes; the stolen pounamu was the only thing keeping his dreams contained
**Connection to protagonist:** Tane's ancestor Rau was the warrior who bound Matau; the family was tasked with eternal guardianship; Tane is the last of that line

## Supporting Characters

**Aroha** — A young tohunga (spiritual expert) who reveals the crisis to Tane; accompanies him as guide and guardian; challenges his colonial assimilation
**Te Kooti's Ghost** — The spirit of the Māori prophet-warrior who died in the same rebellion as Tane's father; provides guidance in Te Po; represents both inspiration and warning
**Constable Hayes** — A colonial policeman who initially pursues Tane as a suspected agitator; eventually becomes unlikely ally when he sees the supernatural threat

## Setting

**Primary location:** New Zealand's North Island—Wellington, the Urewera forests, and the overlapping spirit world Te Po
**Time period:** 1877, during the aftermath of the New Zealand Wars, as Māori resistance fades under colonial pressure
**Atmosphere:** Dense bush covers mountains that might be sleeping gods. The colonial towns are raw and new; the land beneath is old beyond human memory. Where the spirit world bleeds through, time moves differently and the dead walk beside the living. The smell of native bush—manuka, rimu, fern—mixes with coal smoke and sheep.
**Key locations:** Wellington's waterfront slums; the sacred cave where the pounamu was kept; the entrance to Te Po in the Urewera; Matau's dreaming chamber beneath volcanic mountains; the meeting ground between worlds

## Central Conflict
Tane must travel through Te Po—the spirit world where ancestors wait—and retrieve the pounamu from whoever stole it. But Te Po tests the living, and Tane must prove his worthiness to ancestors who might judge his colonial assimilation as betrayal. And something within Te Po wants Matau to wake, something that orchestrated the theft.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The pounamu is restored, Matau sleeps peacefully, and the land stabilizes; Māori survival continues
**If the protagonist fails:** Matau wakes and the North Island tears itself apart; earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and tidal waves destroy both Māori and colonial settlements; the land itself rejects humanity
**Personal stakes:** Tane must fully embrace his identity as Māori, accepting both the power and the burden of his ancestors; hiding is no longer an option

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Earthquake Sign: Wellington shakes; Tane sees cracks in reality that no one else notices; Aroha finds him and reveals his bloodline's responsibility
- Ch 2 — The Empty Shrine: They travel to the sacred cave and find the pounamu stolen; Tane's grandmother's spirit appears and confirms the mission
- Ch 3 — The Threshold: At the Urewera entrance, Tane must undergo preparation rituals he barely remembers; Aroha prepares to stay behind as anchor

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Te Po's Gates: Tane enters the spirit world; everything is familiar but wrong; the dead recognize him but don't trust his assimilated soul
- Ch 5 — The Ancestors' Test: Tane must prove his knowledge of whakapapa (genealogy) and tikanga (custom); he fails partially, passes partially; is granted limited passage
- Ch 6 — Te Kooti's Warning: The prophet's ghost reveals the thief—a Pakeha spiritualist who sought power; Tane must descend deeper where the dead don't speak
- Ch 7 — Matau Stirs: In the waking world, earthquakes increase; Aroha is arrested by Hayes; she reveals enough truth to make him reconsider everything
- Ch 8 — The Chamber of Dreams: Tane finds Matau's dreaming space; the pounamu is embedded in the giant's forehead, put there by the thief to wake him; Tane cannot simply take it

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Binding Song: Tane must sing the ancient karakia (prayer) that his grandmother taught him to calm Matau while removing the pounamu; the thief's spirit opposes him; ancestors join the song
- Ch 10 — The Guardian Returns: Tane emerges with the pounamu; the earthquakes stop; he can no longer hide his identity; becomes the public face of his people's spiritual survival; Hayes ensures colonial authorities do not interfere

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** A man must complete a mythic quest to save his homeland
**Deep theme:** Suppressing your identity doesn't protect you; embracing it does
**Thematic question:** What do you owe to ancestors who suffered for who they were when being yourself seems like a death sentence?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Wellington Earthquake**: The city shaking as the veil between worlds tears; Tane seeing spirits everywhere while colonists see only destruction
2. **The Hall of Ancestors**: Tane standing before the assembled dead, reciting whakapapa he thought he'd forgotten, while those who died in the wars decide if he's worthy
3. **The Giant's Dream**: The chamber where Matau sleeps—a being too large for human scale—as Tane climbs to remove the pounamu while the world above shakes itself apart

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** Whale Rider meets The Revenant
**Book comparables:** The Bone People by Keri Hulme; The Whale Rider by Witi Ihimaera

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Historical grounding with mythic layer. The colonial setting should feel oppressive—Tane's assimilation is survival, not choice. The mythological elements are treated as reality that colonialism has suppressed but not destroyed. The prose should shift registers as Tane does—more English in colonial spaces, more rhythmic in spiritual ones. The pacing accelerates as Matau wakes; the spirit world sections are dreamlike but urgent.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Māori words require attention: Tane (TAH-neh), Raukawa (RAU-kah-wah), pounamu (POH-nah-moo), tohunga (toh-HUNG-ah), Te Po (teh POH), wairua (WY-roo-ah), Matau (mah-TAU), whakapapa (FAH-kah-pah-pah), karakia (kah-RAH-kee-ah), Pakeha (PAH-keh-hah). Tane's voice should show his code-switching—colonial English vs. Māori rhythms. Aroha speaks with more traditional cadence. Spirit voices should have resonance. Colonial characters have period-appropriate British accents.

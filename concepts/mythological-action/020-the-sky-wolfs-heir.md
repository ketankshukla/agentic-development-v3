# The Sky Wolf's Heir

## Genre
mythological-action

## One-Line Premise
A shaman's apprentice must ride to the edge of the sky and kill the demon that devoured his master—before it eats the Eternal Blue Heaven itself.

## Expanded Premise
Temujin was three years from completing his training when the demon Erleg Khan's herald descended and consumed his teacher whole. Now the spirits that guided the shaman turn to the apprentice, demanding he complete his master's final task: ride to the place where sky meets earth and destroy the herald before it opens the gate for Erleg Khan himself. Armed with incomplete training and a horse that may or may not be divine, Temujin must cross a land where shamans are hunted and the old ways are dying.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Temujin Altan
**Age:** 19
**Occupation / Background:** Shaman's apprentice from a nomadic clan; chosen at age seven when he survived a lightning strike; trained in the ways between worlds but far from mastery
**Core skill:** Spirit-riding—he can project his consciousness into the spirit world while his body remains in the physical; can communicate with and occasionally command spirits of ancestors and nature
**Fatal flaw:** Imposter syndrome magnified by his incomplete training; he doesn't believe he's ready and that doubt weakens his power
**Character arc:** Believes he is a fraud who will fail his master's memory; discovers that power comes from will, not completion of training; becomes the shaman his people need through action, not certification
**Voice and register:** Young and uncertain, constantly second-guessing himself. Thinks in terms of what his master would do. Speaks to horses and spirits more comfortably than to people. Gradually finds his own voice as the journey progresses.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** The Herald of Erleg Khan (the death god's chief servant)
**Goal:** To tear open the veil between the living world and the underworld, allowing Erleg Khan to claim all souls without the natural process of death
**Method:** Devouring shamans who are the only mortals capable of opposing it; absorbing their power to fuel the gate-opening; hunting across the steppe for every remaining spirit-talker
**Connection to protagonist:** The Herald chose Temujin's master specifically because of Temujin—the apprentice was prophesied to threaten Erleg Khan; by killing the teacher, the Herald hoped to leave the student helpless

## Supporting Characters

**Sarangerel** — A female shaman from a rival clan, also hunted by the Herald; initially distrustful competitor who becomes ally; more trained than Temujin but carries wounds that limit her power
**Khar** — Temujin's horse, who may be a disguised sky spirit; never speaks but seems to understand everything; guides when Temujin is lost
**The Sky Wolf** — The mythical ancestor of all Mongols, appearing in visions; provides guidance but also judgment; Temujin must prove himself worthy of the bloodline

## Setting

**Primary location:** The Mongolian steppe and the spirit world that overlays it—from the Gobi Desert to the Altai Mountains to the edge of the sky
**Time period:** 1210 CE, during the rise of the Mongol Empire, as the old shamanic ways compete with Buddhism and conquest
**Atmosphere:** Endless grassland under infinite sky—the world is horizontal in every direction until mountains break the horizon. The wind never stops. Gers (yurts) cluster like white mushrooms; herds move like clouds. The spirit world is the same landscape but older, with extinct animals and ancestor spirits riding alongside the living.
**Key locations:** The sacred mountain Burkhan Khaldun; the salt desert where spirits go to die; the eternal frozen lake at the world's edge; the gate between sky and earth where the Herald waits

## Central Conflict
Temujin must complete a journey across the entire steppe—from his clan's territory to the world's edge—while the Herald hunts him and absorbs every shaman it can catch. Each shaman's power it gains makes it stronger; each one it kills is one fewer who might help Temujin. He's racing against a enemy that grows stronger while his potential allies grow fewer.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The Herald is destroyed, the gate remains closed, and Erleg Khan is denied his invasion; the shamanic tradition survives
**If the protagonist fails:** The underworld floods into the world of the living; all souls—past, present, and future—become Erleg Khan's property; existence becomes eternal death
**Personal stakes:** Temujin must complete his training through experience rather than teaching, becoming the shaman his master saw in him—or die proving his own doubts right

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Master Falls: Temujin watches helplessly as his master is devoured; the spirits turn to him with impossible demands; he flees into the steppe
- Ch 2 — The Horse's Path: Lost and pursued, Temujin follows Khar who seems to know the way; first encounter with the Herald's lesser servants
- Ch 3 — The Rival Shaman: Temujin meets Sarangerel, fighting the same servants; they clash before realizing they share enemies; reluctant alliance forms

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — Burkhan Khaldun: At the sacred mountain, they seek the Sky Wolf's blessing; Temujin must face his ancestor's judgment and prove his lineage
- Ch 5 — The Shaman Gathering: They find other surviving shamans hiding together; the Herald attacks the gathering; Temujin's actions save some but not all
- Ch 6 — The Salt Desert: Crossing the place where spirits die, Temujin speaks with his master's ghost and learns the Herald's weakness—it cannot devour willing sacrifice, only taken souls
- Ch 7 — Sarangerel's Wound: She reveals her power was broken by Erleg Khan's touch years ago; she cannot face the Herald directly; Temujin must go on alone
- Ch 8 — The Frozen Lake: At the world's edge, Temujin prepares for the final confrontation; Khar finally reveals its nature; the apprentice becomes the master through choice, not training

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Gate of Sky: Temujin faces the Herald at the barrier between worlds; the demon offers power, offers to restore his master, offers everything; Temujin refuses and fights
- Ch 10 — The Sky Wolf's Heir: Temujin defeats the Herald by offering himself willingly—which it cannot take—and striking in that moment of confusion; becomes the shaman who guards the gate; Sarangerel continues his teaching

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** An apprentice must complete his training by surviving impossible odds
**Deep theme:** Readiness is not about preparation; it's about willingness to act despite fear
**Thematic question:** Are you defined by what you've been taught or by what you choose to do?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Devouring**: Temujin's master facing the Herald—a being of shadow and hunger—as reality tears around them; the old shaman's last words carrying across the spirit world
2. **Burkhan Khaldun Judgment**: The Sky Wolf manifesting on the sacred mountain, ancient and terrible, weighing Temujin's worthiness while lesser spirits circle like wolves themselves
3. **The Gate Battle**: The edge of the sky where blue becomes void; the Herald enormous with consumed shamans' power; Temujin small but willing, spirit-riding while his body falls

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** The Eagle Huntress meets Doctor Strange
**Book comparables:** The Blue Wolf by Inoue Yasushi; Mongol by Conn Iggulden

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Coming-of-age journey across mythic landscape. The prose should feel like the steppe—spare, clean, with sudden intensity. The spiritual elements are matter-of-fact; this is a world where everyone knows shamans speak to spirits. The relationship between Temujin and Khar (his horse) is central; the animal companion carries emotional weight. The mentor's death haunts every scene. Pacing is long journey with moments of sharp violence.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Mongolian names use standard romanization: Temujin (teh-MOO-jin), Altan (AHL-tahn), Erleg Khan (AIR-leg KAHN), Sarangerel (sah-rahn-geh-REL), Burkhan Khaldun (boor-KAHN khal-DOON). The Herald should sound like multiple voices speaking in imperfect unison. Khar the horse doesn't speak but should have presence—hoof beats, breath, movement. Spirit voices echo. The Sky Wolf's voice should feel ancient and vast. Wind should underscore outdoor scenes.

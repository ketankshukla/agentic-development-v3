# The Jackal King's Heir

## Genre
Sword-and-Sorcery

## One-Line Premise
A mortician's son with the power to speak to the dead must lead an army of awakened mummies against a living god-pharaoh who has outlawed death itself.

## Expanded Premise
Kephri has always heard the dead whisper—a shameful gift in a dynasty where death is sacred and touching corpses marks one as untouchable. When the God-Pharaoh Amunhotep proclaims himself immortal and bans the death rites, trapping souls in rotting bodies, Kephri's gift becomes salvation. The old dead—properly embalmed, their ka preserved—are waking, furious at the desecration. They choose Kephri as their voice, their general, their Jackal King. Now a mortician's son must lead ten thousand years of Egypt's honored dead against the most powerful living pharaoh in history.

## Protagonist

**Name:** Kephri Setet
**Age:** 26
**Occupation / Background:** Mortician caste, "untouchable," trained in death rites but forbidden from temple service
**Core skill:** He can hear, speak to, and command the honored dead—those properly mummified and sent to the afterlife
**Fatal flaw:** He has internalized his culture's contempt; he believes himself truly unworthy, even while wielding incredible power
**Character arc:** Believes he is pollution, untouchable, lesser; discovers that the death workers are the only ones who truly understand life's value; becomes the Jackal King, bridge between living and dead
**Voice and register:** Speaks in the formal register required when addressing the dead, even in casual speech—he's never learned to talk to the living comfortably. Internal voice is self-deprecating but increasingly assertive.

## Antagonist or Antagonistic Force

**Name / Description:** God-Pharaoh Amunhotep the Eternal, who has bound his soul to Ra's own fire
**Goal:** To eliminate death itself, making Egypt immortal under his eternal rule—frozen, unchanging, perfect
**Method:** By outlawing death rites, souls cannot pass to the afterlife; they remain trapped, slowly losing themselves; eventually all will worship him as the only god
**Connection to protagonist:** Amunhotep's own father was the pharaoh Kephri's grandfather prepared for burial; the Setet family holds secrets the God-Pharaoh needs destroyed

## Supporting Characters

**Nefertari** — A priestess of Anubis who defied the ban and was executed; she's now Kephri's most trusted counselor among the dead
**Sethnakht** — A mummified general from three hundred years past, awakened and eager for war; he provides military expertise but his ethics are ancient and brutal
**Meritaten** — Amunhotep's daughter, who secretly opposes her father's madness and provides intelligence from within the palace

## Setting

**Primary location:** Kemet, an ancient Egypt-inspired realm of sand, river, pyramid, and temple
**Time period:** Secondary world equivalent of New Kingdom era
**Atmosphere:** The dry scent of natron and myrrh. Gold leaf catching torchlight. The endless patience of stone. Death as a doorway, not an end.
**Key locations:** The Mortician's District (Kephri's home), The Valley of Kings (where the great dead sleep), The Sun Palace (Amunhotep's throne), The Weighing Hall (spiritual judgment site), The First Tomb (oldest burial ground)

## Central Conflict
Kephri must unite the dead of ten thousand years—pharaohs, soldiers, priests, commoners—against an army of the living who fear death itself, while proving to himself that an "untouchable" can be worthy of the crown the dead offer him.

## Stakes

**If the protagonist succeeds:** The death rites are restored, trapped souls are freed, and the cycle of life and death continues
**If the protagonist fails:** Amunhotep's immortality curse spreads, all of Egypt becomes a nation of imprisoned souls, and death is enslaved forever
**Personal stakes:** Kephri's own father was recently executed and improperly buried; his soul is trapped, slowly dissolving; Kephri must win before his father is lost

## Plot Skeleton

**Act One (Chapters 1-3):**
- Ch 1 — The Ban: Amunhotep proclaims the ban on death rites; Kephri's father is executed for defiance; Kephri hears his father screaming from the improper grave
- Ch 2 — The First Awakening: In the mortician's temple, the old dead begin to stir; they speak through Kephri, naming him their voice; the mummies walk
- Ch 3 — The Jackal's Crown: In the oldest tomb, the spirit of Anubis (or something claiming to be Anubis) places a jackal crown on Kephri's head; he becomes visible to all the dead

**Act Two (Chapters 4-8):**
- Ch 4 — The Valley Rises: The Valley of Kings disgorges its inhabitants; Kephri meets Sethnakht and Nefertari; they begin organizing an army
- Ch 5 — The Living's Fear: Kephri's first battle against Amunhotep's soldiers—the living are terrified, but the dead are slow; victory is costly
- Ch 6 — The Palace Contact: Meritaten reaches out, offering palace intelligence; she reveals Amunhotep's weakness—his link to Ra can be severed during eclipse
- Ch 7 — The Father's Fragments: Kephri finds his father's trapped soul, barely coherent; he cannot free him without the rites, but the rites require materials Amunhotep controls
- Ch 8 — The Divine Challenge: Amunhotep himself appears, wielding Ra's fire; he burns a hundred mummies to ash; Kephri barely survives; he realizes he cannot win through battle alone

**Act Three (Chapters 9-10):**
- Ch 9 — The Eclipse War: Kephri times his final assault to the eclipse; as Ra's power dims, the dead surge forward; he reaches Amunhotep's throne room as the sun goes dark
- Ch 10 — The Weighing Heart: Kephri defeats Amunhotep not through force but through the death rites—he judges the pharaoh's heart against Maat's feather, and finds it wanting; Amunhotep dies truly, his soul devoured; the cycle resumes; Kephri frees his father and takes his place as the living guardian of death

## Thematic Core

**Surface theme:** The outcast leads the revolution
**Deep theme:** Death is not an enemy to be conquered but a doorway to be honored
**Thematic question:** If the world tells you you're worthless, what does it mean when the dead themselves name you king?

## Cinematic Set Pieces

1. **The Valley Rising**: Thousands of tomb doors opening simultaneously, bandaged hands emerging, the slow parade of Egypt's honored dead assembling for war
2. **The Solar Duel**: Kephri in his jackal crown facing Amunhotep in his sun-disk regalia, Ra's fire against Anubis's shadow, the sky itself the battlefield
3. **The Heart's Judgment**: The final confrontation rendered as a literal weighing—Amunhotep's crimes visualized as weights, Maat's feather impossibly light

## Comparable Titles

**Film comparables:** *The Mummy* (1999) meets *300*
**Book comparables:** Dan Simmons's *The Terror*; N.K. Jemisin's *The Killing Moon*

## Tone and Pacing Notes
Grand and mythic, but grounded in Kephri's outsider perspective. The dead should feel dignified, not horrific—they are the wronged party, not monsters. Pacing alternates between the slow, inevitable march of mummies and the explosive violence of divine power. Let the social commentary on caste and "untouchability" resonate without becoming preachy.

## Edge TTS Narration Notes
Egyptian-inspired names require consistent pronunciation: Kephri (KEF-ree), Amunhotep (ah-moon-HO-tep), Nefertari (nef-er-TAR-ee), Sethnakht (seth-NAKHT). Mummy voices should be dry, papery, resonant. Amunhotep's voice should boom with solar power. Desert ambience throughout—wind, sand, distant chanting.
